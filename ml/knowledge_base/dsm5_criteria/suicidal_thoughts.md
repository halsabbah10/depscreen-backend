# Recurrent Thoughts of Death or Suicidal Ideation

## Clinical Definition

**DSM-5-TR Criterion A9:** Recurrent thoughts of death (not just fear of dying), recurrent suicidal ideation without a specific plan, or a suicide attempt or a specific plan for committing suicide.

This is the most clinically urgent symptom of Major Depressive Disorder. **Any endorsement of suicidal ideation requires immediate safety assessment.** This criterion encompasses a spectrum from passive death wishes to active suicidal planning.

## Duration Requirement

- **Any occurrence** is clinically significant — this criterion does not require the 2-week duration for clinical urgency
- For MDD diagnostic purposes: recurrent (not a single fleeting thought) during the 2-week episode
- Severity and frequency determine urgency level, not just presence

## How It Manifests

This symptom exists on a severity spectrum, aligned with the **Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS)** framework:

### Level 1: Wish to Be Dead
- "I wish I could go to sleep and not wake up"
- "I wish I didn't exist"
- General desire for death without any intent to act

### Level 2: Non-Specific Active Suicidal Thoughts
- "I've thought about killing myself"
- Suicidal thoughts present but without a specific plan or method
- May be fleeting or recurrent

### Level 3: Active Suicidal Ideation with Any Methods (Not Planned)
- Thinking about methods but without a specific, organized plan
- "I've thought about how I might do it"
- No timeline or preparation

### Level 4: Active Suicidal Ideation with Some Intent to Act
- Suicidal thoughts with some intention of acting (not just fleeting)
- "I think I might actually do it"
- May or may not have a specific plan

### Level 5: Active Suicidal Ideation with Specific Plan and Intent
- Detailed plan with method, timing, and access to means
- Clear intent to carry out the plan
- **This is a psychiatric emergency requiring immediate intervention**

### Additional Presentations
- **Self-as-burden beliefs** — "Everyone would be better off without me" — a key risk factor for transition from ideation to action
- **Hopelessness** — "Things will never get better" — strongest predictor of suicidal behavior
- **Intrusive thoughts about death** — unwanted, distressing thoughts about dying that the person does not want to act on (differentiate from active ideation)
- **Preparatory behavior** — giving away possessions, writing a will/note, researching methods, acquiring means
- **Sudden calmness after agitation** — may indicate a decision has been made (high-risk sign)

## Age-Specific Presentation

### Children and Adolescents
- May not use the word "suicide" — instead: "I want to disappear," "I wish I was never born," "Nobody would miss me"
- Self-harm (cutting, burning) may or may not co-occur with suicidal ideation — assess intent of each behavior separately
- Impulsivity in adolescents means the window from ideation to attempt can be very short
- Social media, cyberbullying, and social contagion are risk amplifiers
- LGBTQ+ youth have significantly elevated risk (4x attempt rate vs. heterosexual peers)
- Ask directly — research consistently shows that asking about suicide does not increase risk

### Older Adults (65+)
- Highest completed suicide rate of any age group (especially white males 85+)
- May present with less warning — fewer prior attempts, more lethal methods, more determined intent
- Risk factors: chronic pain, disability, bereavement, social isolation, loss of independence
- May express as "I've lived long enough" or "I don't want to be a burden" — do not dismiss as age-appropriate
- Higher ratio of completed suicides to attempts compared to younger populations

### Perinatal
- Suicidal ideation during pregnancy or postpartum requires immediate assessment
- Maternal suicide is a leading cause of death in the first year postpartum in high-income countries
- May present as intrusive thoughts of harming self or the baby — assess whether ego-dystonic (unwanted, distressing) or ego-syntonic (aligned with intent)
- Infanticidal thoughts in the context of psychotic depression are a psychiatric emergency

## Cultural Considerations

- **Middle Eastern and Islamic cultures** — suicide is religiously prohibited (haram), which may serve as a protective factor but also creates intense shame that prevents disclosure. Individuals may deny ideation due to religious or social stigma. Use indirect questioning: "Have you felt that life is not worth living?" or "Have you wished you could escape from everything?"
- **Collectivist cultures** — self-as-burden beliefs may be particularly potent. The person may frame suicidal thoughts in terms of relieving family burden rather than personal suffering.
- **Cultures with honor traditions** — suicide may be seen differently when framed as preserving honor vs. escaping despair. Assess the underlying emotional state regardless of cultural framing.
- **Stigma across all cultures** — suicidal ideation is under-reported in every cultural context. Normalize the question: "Many people going through difficult times have thoughts about death — have you had any thoughts like that?"

## When It Becomes Concerning

**All suicidal ideation is clinically significant.** Urgency escalates with:

- **Frequency** — from occasional to constant
- **Specificity** — from vague wishes to detailed plans
- **Intent** — from passive ("I wish") to active ("I will")
- **Access to means** — presence of firearms, medications, or other lethal means
- **Preparatory behavior** — giving away possessions, saying goodbye, writing notes
- **Protective factor erosion** — loss of reasons to live (family, religion, future plans)
- **Acute risk factors** — recent loss, substance intoxication, impulsive personality, previous attempt, discharge from psychiatric care

## Differentiating from Normal Variants

| Normal thoughts about death | Clinical suicidal ideation |
|---|---|
| Philosophical reflection on mortality | Desire for one's own death or nonexistence |
| Grief-related "I miss them, I wish I could see them again" | "I wish I were dead" or "I want to join them" |
| Fear of dying (thanatophobia) | Wish for death — absence of fear of dying |
| Brief, fleeting thought dismissed immediately | Recurrent, persistent, or elaborated thoughts |
| No intent, plan, or desire to act | Any degree of intent, planning, or desire |
| Does not impair functioning | Causes distress, preoccupation, or behavioral change |

## Screening Context

### DepScreen Safety Architecture

DepScreen implements a **multi-layered safety system** for suicidal ideation:

- **Safety floor threshold: 0.15** — any suicidal ideation signal above this threshold triggers the safety protocol regardless of overall depression score
- **C-SSRS alignment** — ideation severity is mapped to C-SSRS levels (1-5) to communicate risk in standardized clinical language
- **Immediate crisis resources** — displayed prominently whenever suicidal ideation is detected
- **No false negatives prioritized** — the system is calibrated to err on the side of detection (high sensitivity) for this symptom, accepting higher false positive rates to minimize missed cases
- **Clinician escalation language** — screening results clearly state that any suicidal ideation finding requires professional assessment

### Detection Signals
- Direct statements about wanting to die, end life, or not exist
- Expressions of hopelessness about the future
- Self-as-burden language ("everyone would be better off without me")
- References to methods, plans, or preparations
- Sudden giving away of possessions or closure-seeking behavior
- Language about "escape," "ending the pain," or "no way out"
