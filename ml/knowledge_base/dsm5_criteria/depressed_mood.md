## Depressed Mood

Depressed mood is one of the two core symptoms of Major Depressive Disorder as defined by the DSM-5. It refers to a persistent feeling of sadness, emptiness, or hopelessness that is present most of the day, nearly every day, for at least two weeks.

### How It Manifests

Depressed mood goes beyond ordinary sadness or having a bad day. People experiencing this symptom often describe feeling "down," "blue," "empty," or "numb." It may show up as:

- A heavy, persistent sadness that does not lift with positive events
- Feeling tearful or crying more easily than usual
- A pervasive sense of hopelessness about the future
- Emotional numbness or feeling "flat"
- Irritability or frustration over small matters (especially common in adolescents and young adults)

Others may notice changes in facial expression, posture, or tone of voice. The person may appear withdrawn or disengaged from conversations and activities.

### When It Becomes Concerning

Everyone feels sad sometimes, and grief or disappointment are normal human experiences. Depressed mood becomes clinically significant when:

- It persists for most of the day, nearly every day, for two weeks or more
- It interferes with daily functioning such as work, school, or relationships
- It does not improve with changes in circumstances or positive events
- It is accompanied by other symptoms such as changes in sleep, appetite, energy, or concentration

Depressed mood is identified through self-report (the person describes feeling sad or hopeless) or through observation by others (appearing tearful or withdrawn). In children and adolescents, this symptom may present primarily as irritability rather than sadness.

This information is for screening and awareness purposes only and is not a diagnosis.
