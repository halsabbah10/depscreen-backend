# Depressed Mood

## Clinical Definition

**DSM-5-TR Criterion A1:** Depressed mood most of the day, nearly every day, as indicated by subjective report (e.g., feels sad, empty, hopeless) or observation made by others (e.g., appears tearful). *Note: In children and adolescents, can be irritable mood.*

Depressed mood is one of two **core symptoms** of Major Depressive Disorder (MDD). At least one core symptom — depressed mood or anhedonia — must be present for an MDD diagnosis.

## Duration Requirement

- Present **most of the day, nearly every day**, for a minimum of **2 consecutive weeks**
- The mood represents a clear change from the person's previous baseline functioning
- Transient sadness lasting hours or a few days does not meet this criterion

## How It Manifests

Depressed mood encompasses a range of subjective experiences and observable signs:

- **Persistent sadness** — a heavy, unrelenting low mood that does not lift with positive events
- **Emptiness** — feeling hollow, numb, or emotionally "flat"; unable to feel anything
- **Hopelessness** — a pervasive belief that things will not improve; the future feels bleak
- **Tearfulness** — crying easily, sometimes without a clear trigger
- **Irritability** — frustration, short temper, or agitation over minor matters (especially prominent in younger populations)
- **Emotional reactivity loss** — inability to "cheer up" even briefly in normally pleasurable situations
- **Somatic descriptions** — heaviness in the chest, a "knot" in the stomach, or physical pain described as emotional distress
- **Observable changes** — flat affect, reduced eye contact, slumped posture, monotone speech, social withdrawal

## Age-Specific Presentation

### Children and Adolescents
- **Irritable mood** is an accepted equivalent of sadness per DSM-5-TR and may be the predominant presentation
- May present as behavioral problems, declining school performance, or social withdrawal rather than verbalized sadness
- Somatic complaints (headaches, stomachaches) are common proxies for depressed mood
- Younger children may show mood changes through play themes (loss, death, failure)

### Older Adults (65+)
- May minimize or deny sadness; instead report "not feeling like myself" or fatigue
- Somatic complaints often predominate: pain, gastrointestinal issues, sleep disruption
- Cognitive symptoms (poor memory, confusion) may overshadow mood complaints — risk of misattribution to dementia ("pseudodementia")
- Social isolation and functional decline may be more visible than expressed sadness
- Higher rates of comorbid medical illness complicate detection

### Perinatal (Pregnancy and Postpartum)
- May present as excessive worry about the baby, feeling disconnected from the infant, or guilt about not feeling "happy enough"
- Hormonal shifts can intensify mood lability; distinguish from transient "baby blues" (resolves within 2 weeks postpartum)
- Onset can occur during pregnancy, not only after delivery
- Screening recommended at every perinatal visit (ACOG, USPSTF)

## Cultural Considerations

Depression is experienced and expressed differently across cultures:

- **Middle Eastern and Arab cultures** — depressed mood may be expressed through somatic idioms: chest tightness, heart pain, heaviness in the body. Direct expression of sadness may be stigmatized, especially for men. The concept of "huzn" (حزن) carries both spiritual and emotional connotations.
- **East and South Asian cultures** — somatic presentation is common: fatigue, headaches, "weakness of nerves." Emotional distress may be framed in terms of social harmony disruption rather than individual mood.
- **Latin American cultures** — "nervios" or "susto" may describe depressive states. Family-centered expression of distress is common.
- **Collectivist cultures broadly** — depressive mood may be described in relational terms ("I am a burden to my family") rather than intrapsychic terms ("I feel sad").
- **Gender norms** — men across many cultures may express depression as anger, risk-taking, or substance use rather than overt sadness.

Clinicians and screening tools must account for idioms of distress to avoid under-detection.

## When It Becomes Concerning

Normal sadness becomes clinically significant when:

- It persists for **most of the day, nearly every day, for ≥2 weeks**
- It represents a **change from baseline** — not the person's lifelong temperament
- It causes **functional impairment** in work, school, relationships, or self-care
- It does **not remit** with changes in circumstances or positive events
- It is accompanied by **other depressive symptoms** (sleep, appetite, energy, concentration changes)

## Differentiating from Normal Variants

| Normal sadness / grief | Clinical depressed mood |
|---|---|
| Triggered by identifiable loss or stressor | May arise without clear trigger |
| Comes in waves, interspersed with positive moments | Persistent, pervasive, most of the day |
| Self-esteem generally preserved | Often accompanied by worthlessness |
| Resolves as circumstances change | Persists despite positive changes |
| Duration typically days to weeks | ≥2 weeks at clinical threshold |
| Functional capacity largely maintained | Significant functional impairment |

*Note:* Grief and MDD can co-occur. The DSM-5-TR removed the bereavement exclusion — a major depressive episode can be diagnosed even in the context of recent loss if full criteria are met.

## Screening Context

DepScreen detects depressed mood through:

- Direct language about sadness, emptiness, hopelessness, or emotional pain
- Somatic idioms of distress (heaviness, chest pain described emotionally)
- Expressions of irritability, frustration, or emotional numbness
- Temporal markers indicating persistence ("every day," "for weeks," "all the time")
- Functional impact statements ("I can't get through the day," "nothing matters")

The model weighs both explicit mood statements and implicit indicators, including cultural and somatic expressions of depressed mood.
