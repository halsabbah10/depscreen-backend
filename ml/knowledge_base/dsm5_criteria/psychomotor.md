# Psychomotor Agitation or Retardation

## Clinical Definition

**DSM-5-TR Criterion A5:** Psychomotor agitation or retardation nearly every day (observable by others, not merely subjective feelings of restlessness or being slowed down).

This criterion is unique among MDD symptoms because it **must be observable by others** — the individual's subjective experience alone is insufficient. It reflects depression's impact on motor systems and is associated with more severe episodes and higher functional impairment.

## Duration Requirement

- Present **nearly every day** for a minimum of **2 consecutive weeks**
- Must be **observable by others** (clinician, family member, colleague) — not just self-reported
- Represents a clear change from the person's baseline motor behavior

## How It Manifests

### Psychomotor Agitation
- **Inability to sit still** — pacing, standing up repeatedly, shifting position constantly
- **Hand-wringing** — repetitive, purposeless hand movements
- **Fidgeting** — pulling at skin, clothing, hair; tapping; picking at objects
- **Pressured speech** — speaking rapidly, difficulty stopping, racing from topic to topic
- **Restless energy** — internal sense of being "driven" or "wound up" that manifests in visible motor activity
- **Irritable outbursts** — agitation boiling over into verbal or physical expressions of frustration

### Psychomotor Retardation
- **Slowed movement** — walking, gesturing, and transitioning between positions are visibly slower
- **Slowed speech** — longer pauses before responding, reduced volume, monotone delivery, fewer words
- **Reduced facial expressiveness** — flat affect, diminished eye contact, absence of reactive expressions
- **Delayed response latency** — noticeable lag between hearing a question and beginning to answer
- **Decreased gesturing** — reduced hand movements, body language becomes sparse
- **Slowed task completion** — routine physical tasks (dressing, eating) take markedly longer
- **Severe cases** — near-immobility, mutism, or stupor (approaches catatonia)

## Age-Specific Presentation

### Children and Adolescents
- Agitation may present as behavioral problems, inability to sit in class, or physical aggression
- Retardation may appear as sluggishness, slow completion of schoolwork, or "spacing out"
- Younger children may show reduced play motor activity or unusually still/quiet behavior
- Adolescent agitation may be mistaken for ADHD or oppositional behavior
- May be difficult to distinguish from normal developmental activity level variation

### Older Adults (65+)
- Psychomotor retardation is more common in late-life depression and is associated with poorer outcomes
- May be misattributed to normal aging, arthritis, or neurological disease
- Slowed gait, reduced facial expression, and decreased verbal output are key observable signs
- Must differentiate from Parkinson's disease (bradykinesia), delirium, and dementia-related apathy
- Retardation in elderly patients is a risk factor for poor treatment response and chronicity

### Perinatal
- Agitation may present as inability to rest even when the infant is sleeping
- Retardation may manifest as slowed caregiving responses, delayed feeding, reduced interaction with the infant
- Psychomotor changes affecting infant care capacity require urgent clinical attention

## Cultural Considerations

- **Middle Eastern cultures** — psychomotor agitation in men may be expressed as irritability, anger, or aggressive behavior rather than recognized as a depression symptom. Women may show restlessness through repetitive domestic activity.
- **Cultures with reserved norms** — in cultures that value physical composure and restraint, psychomotor retardation may be seen as appropriate behavior rather than a symptom. Baseline activity level is culturally mediated.
- **Collectivist cultures** — family members are often the first to notice motor changes ("She moves so slowly now," "He can't sit still"). Family informant reports are particularly valuable for this criterion.
- **Communication style differences** — speech latency varies across cultures and languages. Baseline speech tempo must be established before interpreting pauses as retardation.

## When It Becomes Concerning

Psychomotor changes become clinically significant when:

- They are **observable by others**, not just the person's internal experience
- They are present **nearly every day for ≥2 weeks**
- They represent a **clear change from the person's usual behavior**
- They **interfere with daily functioning** or communication
- They are accompanied by **other depressive symptoms**
- In severe cases: near-immobility, mutism, or inability to perform self-care (approaches catatonia — requires urgent evaluation)

## Differentiating from Normal Variants

| Normal motor variation | Clinical psychomotor change |
|---|---|
| Restlessness before a stressful event | Persistent agitation nearly every day |
| Sluggishness when sleep-deprived | Retardation despite adequate sleep |
| Fidgeting during boring activity | Purposeless agitation observable in all contexts |
| Age-related slowing (mild) | Marked retardation exceeding age norms |
| Self-reported "feeling slow" | Observable by others (required for criterion) |
| Resolves with rest or stress resolution | Persists for ≥2 weeks regardless of circumstances |

## Screening Context

DepScreen detects psychomotor changes through:

- Descriptions of inability to sit still, pacing, or restless physical movement
- Reports of feeling "slowed down," moving through "thick air," or "everything takes forever"
- Language about others noticing motor or speech changes
- Mentions of speech slowing, long pauses, or difficulty getting words out
- Descriptions of physical heaviness or inability to move
- Reports from third parties about observable behavioral changes

Because this criterion requires observable change, DepScreen assigns moderate weight to self-report and flags when descriptions suggest motor changes that would likely be noticeable to others.

This information is for screening and psychoeducation purposes only and does not constitute a clinical diagnosis.
