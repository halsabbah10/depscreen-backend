## Psychomotor Agitation or Retardation

Psychomotor changes are one of the nine DSM-5 criteria for Major Depressive Disorder. This symptom refers to observable changes in physical movement and activity level that are noticeable to others, not merely the person's subjective sense of restlessness or slowness.

### How It Manifests

Psychomotor changes can present in two opposite forms:

**Psychomotor agitation:**
- Inability to sit still, pacing, or restless movement
- Fidgeting, hand-wringing, or pulling at skin, clothing, or hair
- Feeling internally "wound up" or driven by nervous energy
- Difficulty relaxing the body even when trying to rest
- Speaking rapidly or being unable to stop talking

**Psychomotor retardation:**
- Noticeably slowed physical movements
- Speaking more slowly, with longer pauses before responding
- Reduced facial expressiveness or gesturing
- Taking longer to complete routine physical tasks
- Feeling as though the body is heavy or moving through thick air

The DSM-5 specifies that these changes must be observable by others, not just felt internally. A friend, family member, or clinician might notice the person moving, speaking, or reacting differently than usual.

### When It Becomes Concerning

Brief episodes of restlessness or sluggishness are common during periods of stress or fatigue. Psychomotor changes become clinically significant when:

- They are persistent, occurring nearly every day
- They are noticeable to other people, not just the individual
- They represent a clear change from the person's usual behavior
- They interfere with daily functioning or communication
- They accompany other depressive symptoms such as low mood, sleep changes, or fatigue

If you or someone close to you has noticed sustained changes in movement or speech patterns, it may be worth discussing with a healthcare provider.

This information is for screening and awareness purposes only and is not a diagnosis.
