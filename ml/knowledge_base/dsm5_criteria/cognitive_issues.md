## Diminished Ability to Think or Concentrate

Difficulty thinking, concentrating, or making decisions is one of the nine DSM-5 criteria for Major Depressive Disorder. This symptom reflects the cognitive impact of depression and can affect nearly every area of a person's daily life.

### How It Manifests

Depression-related cognitive difficulties are sometimes called "brain fog" and may include:

- Difficulty sustaining attention while reading, working, or in conversation
- Forgetting appointments, tasks, or things that were just said
- Struggling to make even simple decisions (what to eat, what to wear)
- Feeling mentally "slow" or as though thoughts are harder to access
- Taking much longer than usual to complete familiar tasks
- Losing track of what one was saying or doing mid-task
- Difficulty organizing thoughts or planning ahead
- Reduced problem-solving ability

These cognitive changes are not due to a lack of intelligence or effort. Depression affects brain regions involved in attention, working memory, and executive function. Research shows measurable changes in processing speed, memory, and decision-making during depressive episodes.

### When It Becomes Concerning

Occasional forgetfulness or difficulty focusing is normal, especially during stressful periods. Cognitive difficulties become clinically significant when:

- They represent a noticeable change from the person's usual level of functioning
- They persist for two weeks or more
- They interfere with work performance, academic achievement, or daily tasks
- They contribute to feelings of frustration, incompetence, or self-doubt
- They occur alongside other symptoms such as depressed mood, fatigue, or sleep disturbance

If you have noticed a sustained decline in your ability to concentrate or make decisions, it may be helpful to discuss this with a healthcare provider. Cognitive symptoms often improve with appropriate treatment for depression.

This information is for screening and awareness purposes only and is not a diagnosis.

Source: APA, DSM-5 (2013); NIMH
