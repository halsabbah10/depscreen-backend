## Appetite or Weight Changes

Significant changes in appetite or weight are one of the nine DSM-5 criteria for Major Depressive Disorder. This can present as either a marked increase or decrease in appetite, often accompanied by corresponding weight changes, when not actively dieting or attempting to gain weight.

### How It Manifests

Depression-related appetite changes can go in either direction:

**Decreased appetite:**
- Food seems unappealing or tasteless
- Forgetting to eat or skipping meals without noticing
- Feeling nauseated at the thought of eating
- Unintentional weight loss (a change of more than 5% of body weight in a month is considered significant)

**Increased appetite:**
- Craving carbohydrate-rich or high-sugar comfort foods
- Eating more than usual, especially when not physically hungry
- Using food as a way to soothe emotional distress
- Unintentional weight gain

Some people alternate between periods of eating too little and eating too much. The appetite change is typically not driven by conscious choices about diet but reflects underlying shifts in the body's regulation of hunger, energy, and reward.

### When It Becomes Concerning

Occasional fluctuations in appetite are a normal part of life, influenced by stress, illness, or changes in routine. Appetite changes become clinically significant when:

- They persist for two weeks or more
- They result in noticeable weight change (approximately 5% or more of body weight in a month)
- They occur without a clear medical cause or intentional dietary change
- They are accompanied by other symptoms of depression such as low mood, fatigue, or sleep disturbance

If you notice persistent, unexplained changes in your appetite or weight, it is worth discussing with a healthcare provider.

This information is for screening and awareness purposes only and is not a diagnosis.
