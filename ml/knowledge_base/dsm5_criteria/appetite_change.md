# Appetite or Weight Changes

## Clinical Definition

**DSM-5-TR Criterion A3:** Significant weight loss when not dieting or weight gain (e.g., a change of more than 5% of body weight in a month), or decrease or increase in appetite nearly every day. *Note: In children, consider failure to make expected weight gains.*

This is a neurovegetative symptom reflecting depression's disruption of hypothalamic appetite regulation and reward circuitry.

## Duration Requirement

- Appetite change present **nearly every day** for a minimum of **2 consecutive weeks**
- Weight change threshold: **>5% of body weight in one month** (e.g., >8 lbs / 3.6 kg for a 160 lb / 73 kg person)
- The change is **not intentional** (not due to dieting, bariatric intervention, or deliberate weight gain)

## How It Manifests

Depression-related appetite changes are bidirectional:

### Decreased Appetite / Weight Loss
- Food seems unappealing, tasteless, or repulsive
- Forgetting to eat or skipping meals without noticing
- Feeling nauseated at the thought of eating
- Needing to force oneself to eat
- Rapid unintentional weight loss
- More common in melancholic depression

### Increased Appetite / Weight Gain
- Craving carbohydrate-rich, high-sugar, or high-fat comfort foods
- Eating when not physically hungry — emotional or "boredom" eating
- Using food to soothe emotional distress or fill emotional emptiness
- Loss of satiety signals — difficulty stopping once eating starts
- Unintentional weight gain
- More common in atypical depression

### Mixed Patterns
- Some individuals alternate between periods of under-eating and over-eating within the same episode
- Appetite and weight can change independently — appetite may drop while weight remains stable, or vice versa

## Age-Specific Presentation

### Children and Adolescents
- **Failure to make expected weight gains** is the DSM-5-TR criterion for children (rather than weight loss)
- May present as picky eating, food refusal, or loss of interest in previously enjoyed foods
- Adolescents may develop disordered eating patterns that overlap with depression
- Growth trajectory deceleration on pediatric growth charts is clinically significant
- Must differentiate from eating disorders (anorexia nervosa, bulimia) which have distinct psychopathology

### Older Adults (65+)
- Decreased appetite is more common and carries higher medical risk (malnutrition, sarcopenia, immune compromise)
- "Anorexia of aging" (physiological appetite decline) complicates assessment
- Medications may suppress appetite or alter taste (many common in elderly polypharmacy)
- Weight loss in elderly depressed patients is an independent predictor of poor outcomes
- Social isolation reducing shared meals can compound the problem

### Perinatal
- Must differentiate from pregnancy-related appetite changes (nausea, cravings, increased caloric needs)
- Inability to eat during pregnancy raises nutritional concern for both mother and fetus
- Postpartum: excessive eating for comfort vs. inability to eat due to depression while caring for newborn
- Breastfeeding caloric demands add complexity to weight change assessment

## Cultural Considerations

- **Middle Eastern cultures** — food is central to social life and hospitality. Loss of appetite may manifest as declining family meals, which carries social significance. Weight loss may be noticed and remarked upon by extended family before the individual seeks help.
- **South Asian cultures** — somatic complaints about digestion, stomach problems, or "weakness" may represent appetite-related depression symptoms. Ayurvedic or traditional frameworks may attribute symptoms to dietary imbalance.
- **Collectivist cultures** — family meals are often obligatory; the depressed person may eat to avoid concern from family while experiencing no appetite or pleasure in food.
- **Cultures with food scarcity** — appetite changes must be distinguished from food insecurity. Weight loss in the context of poverty is not the same as depression-related appetite loss.
- **Fasting traditions** — religious fasting (Ramadan, Lent, Yom Kippur) must be accounted for in assessing appetite patterns and weight changes.

## When It Becomes Concerning

Appetite changes become clinically significant when:

- Appetite change is present **nearly every day for ≥2 weeks**
- Weight change exceeds **5% of body weight in a month** without intentional dieting
- The change is **not explained** by medical illness, medication, or intentional dietary modification
- It causes **nutritional deficiency**, physical weakness, or medical complications
- It is accompanied by **other depressive symptoms**
- In children: **failure to make expected weight gains** on growth trajectory

## Differentiating from Normal Variants

| Normal appetite fluctuation | Clinical appetite disturbance |
|---|---|
| Temporary appetite change with stress or illness | Persistent change nearly every day for ≥2 weeks |
| Comfort eating during a bad week | Sustained pattern with >5% weight change in a month |
| Appetite returns when stressor resolves | Persists independent of external circumstances |
| Intentional dietary changes | Unintentional, often unwanted changes |
| No functional impairment | Nutritional compromise or significant distress |

## Screening Context

DepScreen detects appetite changes through:

- Reports of eating significantly more or less than usual
- Mentions of food losing its appeal or taste
- Descriptions of emotional eating or comfort-food cravings
- References to unintentional weight change
- Statements about forgetting to eat or forcing oneself to eat
- Somatic complaints related to digestion when occurring alongside mood symptoms

Appetite change is a sensitive neurovegetative marker that often co-occurs with sleep disturbance and fatigue, forming a symptom cluster that strengthens screening confidence.

This information is for screening and psychoeducation purposes only and does not constitute a clinical diagnosis.
