# Major Depressive Disorder — Episode Specifiers

## Overview

The DSM-5-TR defines **eight specifiers** that describe the character of a Major Depressive Episode. Specifiers do not change the core diagnosis but provide clinically meaningful subtypes that influence treatment selection, prognosis, and risk assessment. Multiple specifiers can apply to a single episode.

## 1. With Anxious Distress

### Defining Features
Presence of **at least two** of the following during the majority of days:
- Feeling keyed up or tense
- Feeling unusually restless
- Difficulty concentrating due to worry
- Fear that something awful may happen
- Feeling of potential loss of control

### Severity Levels
- **Mild:** 2 symptoms
- **Moderate:** 3 symptoms
- **Moderate-severe:** 4-5 symptoms
- **Severe:** 4-5 symptoms with motor agitation

### Clinical Significance
- Present in a majority of MDD cases (estimated 50-75%)
- Associated with higher suicide risk, longer duration, and poorer treatment response
- Predicts need for anxiolytic or combined treatment approaches
- The most common specifier in clinical practice

## 2. With Mixed Features

### Defining Features
Full criteria for MDE met, plus **at least three** manic/hypomanic symptoms present most days:
- Elevated or expansive mood
- Inflated self-esteem or grandiosity
- More talkative than usual or pressure to keep talking
- Flight of ideas or racing thoughts
- Increase in energy or goal-directed activity
- Increased involvement in high-risk activities
- Decreased need for sleep (distinct from insomnia)

### Clinical Significance
- Present in approximately 25-35% of MDD cases
- Increased risk of conversion to Bipolar Disorder over time
- Antidepressant monotherapy may worsen mixed features — requires careful medication management
- Higher suicide risk than MDD without mixed features
- May require mood stabilizer consideration

## 3. With Melancholic Features

### Defining Features
**One of:**
- Loss of pleasure in all or almost all activities
- Lack of reactivity to usually pleasurable stimuli (mood does not brighten even temporarily)

**Plus three or more of:**
- Distinct quality of depressed mood (qualitatively different from grief)
- Depression regularly worse in the morning
- Early morning awakening (≥2 hours before usual)
- Marked psychomotor agitation or retardation
- Significant anorexia or weight loss
- Excessive or inappropriate guilt

### Clinical Significance
- Historically the "classic" severe depression presentation
- More likely to respond to pharmacotherapy (especially TCAs, SNRIs) than psychotherapy alone
- Strong biological underpinnings: HPA axis dysregulation, cortisol elevation
- Less likely to respond to placebo
- Prevalence: approximately 25-30% of MDD inpatients

## 4. With Atypical Features

### Defining Features
**Mood reactivity** (mood brightens in response to positive events) **plus two or more of:**
- Significant weight gain or increase in appetite
- Hypersomnia (sleeping ≥10 hours/day)
- Leaden paralysis (heavy, weighty feeling in arms or legs lasting ≥1 hour/day)
- Long-standing pattern of interpersonal rejection sensitivity that causes functional impairment

### Clinical Significance
- Despite the name "atypical," this is actually the most common subtype in outpatient settings
- More common in women, younger patients, and those with earlier onset
- MAOIs and SSRIs may be more effective than TCAs for this subtype
- Higher rates of comorbid anxiety disorders, substance use, and personality disorders
- Prevalence: approximately 15-30% of MDD outpatients

## 5. With Psychotic Features

### Defining Features
Presence of **delusions and/or hallucinations** during the depressive episode.

**Mood-congruent psychotic features:**
- Delusions of guilt, worthlessness, disease, death, nihilism, or deserved punishment
- Hallucinations with depressive content (accusatory voices, visions of decay)

**Mood-incongruent psychotic features:**
- Delusions or hallucinations without clear depressive content (persecutory delusions, thought insertion)
- Associated with poorer prognosis

### Clinical Significance
- Present in approximately 10-15% of MDD cases (higher in severe, hospitalized episodes)
- Requires antipsychotic medication in addition to antidepressant — antidepressant alone is insufficient
- ECT is particularly effective for psychotic depression
- Higher suicide risk than non-psychotic MDD
- Often under-recognized because patients may not volunteer psychotic symptoms

## 6. With Catatonia

### Defining Features
Presence of **three or more** of the following:
- Stupor (no psychomotor activity, not relating to environment)
- Catalepsy (passive induction of a posture held against gravity)
- Waxy flexibility
- Mutism
- Negativism (opposition or no response to instructions)
- Posturing
- Mannerisms or stereotypies
- Agitation not influenced by external stimuli
- Grimacing
- Echolalia or echopraxia

### Clinical Significance
- Relatively rare in MDD (estimated <5%)
- Medical emergency requiring specialized intervention
- Benzodiazepines (especially lorazepam) are first-line treatment
- ECT is highly effective for catatonic depression
- Must rule out medical causes (neuroleptic malignant syndrome, anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis)

## 7. With Peripartum Onset

### Defining Features
Onset of mood symptoms during **pregnancy or within 4 weeks following delivery** (DSM-5-TR definition). Clinical practice often extends the window to 12 months postpartum.

### Clinical Significance
- Affects approximately 10-15% of pregnancies (some estimates higher when including prenatal onset)
- Risk factors: prior perinatal depression, personal/family mood disorder history, psychosocial stressors, hormonal sensitivity
- Untreated peripartum depression affects maternal-infant bonding, child development, and family functioning
- Screening recommended at every perinatal visit (ACOG, USPSTF) using Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) or PHQ-9
- Severe cases may involve psychotic features (peripartum psychosis) — psychiatric emergency with risk of infanticide
- Treatment must consider medication safety in pregnancy/breastfeeding

## 8. With Seasonal Pattern

### Defining Features
- Regular temporal relationship between onset of MDE and a particular time of year (most commonly fall/winter)
- Full remission (or switch to mania/hypomania) at a characteristic time of year (most commonly spring/summer)
- At least **two seasonal episodes in the last 2 years** with no non-seasonal episodes
- Seasonal episodes **substantially outnumber** non-seasonal episodes over lifetime

### Clinical Significance
- Also known as Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD) in common parlance
- More common at higher latitudes with greater seasonal light variation
- Prevalence varies: 1-10% depending on latitude and diagnostic threshold
- Light therapy (10,000 lux, 30 min/day, morning) is an evidence-based first-line treatment
- Often features atypical symptoms: hypersomnia, weight gain, carbohydrate craving
- CBT adapted for SAD (CBT-SAD) shows durable efficacy

## Screening Context

DepScreen does not formally diagnose specifiers but may detect language patterns suggestive of specific subtypes:
- Anxiety-laden language → anxious distress
- Mood reactivity descriptions → atypical features
- Early morning awakening + worse-in-morning + non-reactive mood → melancholic features
- Seasonal timing patterns → seasonal pattern
- Peripartum context → peripartum onset

These patterns are noted in screening output to guide clinician assessment.

This information is for screening and psychoeducation purposes only and does not constitute a clinical diagnosis.
