## Fatigue or Loss of Energy

Fatigue or loss of energy is one of the nine DSM-5 criteria for Major Depressive Disorder. It refers to a persistent sense of tiredness, exhaustion, or reduced physical and mental energy that occurs nearly every day, even without significant physical exertion.

### How It Manifests

Depression-related fatigue is different from normal tiredness after a busy day. People experiencing this symptom often describe:

- Feeling exhausted upon waking, even after a full night of sleep
- A sense that everyday tasks (showering, cooking, getting dressed) require enormous effort
- Physical heaviness in the limbs, sometimes described as "leaden paralysis"
- Reduced stamina for activities that were previously manageable
- Difficulty initiating tasks, not from lack of motivation alone but from a genuine feeling of physical depletion
- Needing to rest after minimal activity

This fatigue affects both physical and mental energy. People may find it harder to think clearly, process information, or sustain attention, in addition to feeling physically drained.

### When It Becomes Concerning

Everyone feels tired sometimes, especially during demanding periods. Fatigue becomes clinically significant when:

- It persists for two weeks or more regardless of rest
- It is not explained by physical illness, medication side effects, or sleep deprivation alone
- It significantly impairs the ability to carry out work, school, or daily responsibilities
- It leads to withdrawal from activities or social obligations
- It occurs alongside other symptoms such as depressed mood, sleep disturbance, or difficulty concentrating

Fatigue is one of the most commonly reported symptoms of depression and is often one of the last to resolve with treatment. If persistent tiredness is affecting your quality of life, consider speaking with a healthcare provider.

This information is for screening and awareness purposes only and is not a diagnosis.
