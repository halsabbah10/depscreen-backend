# Fatigue or Loss of Energy

## Clinical Definition

**DSM-5-TR Criterion A6:** Fatigue or loss of energy nearly every day.

Fatigue in Major Depressive Disorder is a neurovegetative symptom reflecting disruption in central energy regulation, distinct from normal tiredness. It is among the most common residual symptoms after treatment and is reported by approximately 90% of individuals with MDD.

## Duration Requirement

- Present **nearly every day** for a minimum of **2 consecutive weeks**
- Represents a change from the person's pre-episode energy level
- Persists **despite adequate rest** — not explained by sleep deprivation alone

## How It Manifests

Depression-related fatigue affects both physical and cognitive energy:

- **Physical exhaustion upon waking** — feeling drained before the day begins, even after a full night of sleep
- **Leaden paralysis** — a heavy, weighted feeling in the arms and legs; characteristic of atypical depression
- **Disproportionate effort** — routine tasks (showering, dressing, cooking, climbing stairs) feel enormously effortful
- **Reduced stamina** — activities previously managed easily now require rest breaks
- **Task initiation difficulty** — not just motivational; a genuine sense that the body lacks the energy to begin
- **Post-exertional malaise** — minor activity (a short walk, a conversation) leads to extended recovery periods
- **Cognitive fatigue** — difficulty sustaining mental effort; reading a page requires re-reading; conversations feel exhausting
- **Early exhaustion** — running out of energy by mid-morning or early afternoon

## Age-Specific Presentation

### Children and Adolescents
- May present as lethargy, increased time in bed, or reluctance to participate in physical activities
- School-related complaints: "I'm too tired to go to school," declining gym participation
- May be misinterpreted as laziness, oppositional behavior, or attention-seeking
- Adolescents may withdraw to their room citing exhaustion
- Must distinguish from normal adolescent sleep phase shift and increased sleep need

### Older Adults (65+)
- Fatigue is extremely common in elderly populations, creating diagnostic complexity
- Must rule out medical causes: anemia, hypothyroidism, B12 deficiency, heart failure, chronic kidney disease, malignancy
- Medication-related fatigue is prevalent (beta-blockers, antihistamines, benzodiazepines, statins)
- Deconditioning from reduced activity creates a fatigue-inactivity cycle
- Fatigue may be the presenting complaint rather than mood symptoms
- "I just don't have the energy anymore" may be the primary depression expression

### Perinatal
- Extreme fatigue is expected in late pregnancy and early postpartum — complicates detection
- Depression-related fatigue is distinguishable by: persisting when the infant sleeps, no improvement with rest, accompanied by other mood symptoms
- Fatigue so severe that basic infant care becomes unsafe warrants urgent assessment
- Thyroid function should be checked (postpartum thyroiditis prevalence 5-10%)

## Cultural Considerations

- **Middle Eastern cultures** — fatigue and physical weakness ("da'f" / ضعف) are socially acceptable complaints that may serve as the primary idiom for depression. "I have no energy" may mean "I am depressed" in clinical context.
- **East Asian cultures** — "shenjing shuairuo" (neurasthenia) in Chinese culture encompasses fatigue, poor concentration, and somatic complaints; historically used as a depression-equivalent diagnosis.
- **South Asian cultures** — fatigue may be attributed to "weak blood," dietary imbalance, or spiritual causes before considering psychiatric origin.
- **Cultures with strong work ethic norms** — admitting to fatigue may carry stigma. Men in particular may push through exhaustion, presenting only when functional collapse occurs.
- **Socioeconomic context** — physical labor, multiple jobs, food insecurity, and inadequate housing cause real fatigue that must be distinguished from (or recognized as compounding) depression-related fatigue.

## When It Becomes Concerning

Fatigue becomes clinically significant when:

- It is present **nearly every day for ≥2 weeks** despite adequate rest
- It represents a **clear change from pre-episode energy levels**
- It causes **functional impairment** — inability to work, care for self/family, or maintain daily routines
- It **does not improve** with rest, weekends off, or vacation
- It is accompanied by **other depressive symptoms**
- Medical workup (CBC, TSH, B12, metabolic panel) does not reveal a sufficient medical explanation

## Differentiating from Normal Variants

| Normal tiredness | Clinical fatigue (MDD) |
|---|---|
| Proportional to exertion or sleep debt | Disproportionate to activity level |
| Resolves with adequate rest | Persists despite rest |
| Energy returns after sleep | Waking already exhausted |
| Limited to specific demanding periods | Present nearly every day for ≥2 weeks |
| Functional capacity largely preserved | Routine tasks feel enormously effortful |
| Motivated despite tiredness | Motivation and energy both depleted |

## Screening Context

DepScreen detects fatigue through:

- Reports of persistent exhaustion, tiredness, or lack of energy
- Descriptions of routine tasks feeling overwhelming or impossible
- Language about heaviness in the body or "leaden" limbs
- Statements about needing excessive rest or being unable to get out of bed
- Mentions of declining productivity or inability to keep up with responsibilities
- Functional impact: "I can barely get through the day," "everything takes so much effort"

Fatigue is one of the most commonly reported symptoms and one of the last to resolve with treatment. Its persistence after other symptoms improve (residual fatigue) is a risk factor for relapse.
