# Differential Diagnosis for Major Depressive Disorder

## Overview

Several psychiatric, medical, and substance-related conditions share symptoms with MDD. Accurate differentiation is essential because treatment approaches differ substantially. This document outlines the most common differential diagnoses a clinician must consider when a patient screens positive for depression.

**DepScreen is a screening tool, not a diagnostic instrument.** It cannot rule out the conditions below. All positive screens should prompt comprehensive clinical evaluation.

## Psychiatric Differentials

### 1. Bipolar II Disorder

**Key distinguishing features:**
- History of at least one **hypomanic episode** (≥4 consecutive days of elevated/irritable mood + increased energy + ≥3 manic symptoms)
- Hypomanic episodes may be experienced as "good periods" and not reported as pathological
- Depression in Bipolar II is often the presenting complaint — the hypomania is missed
- Higher rates of mixed features, atypical features, and rapid cycling

**Screening questions:**
- "Have you ever had a period of several days where you felt unusually energized, needed much less sleep, and were much more active or talkative than normal?"
- "Has anyone ever told you that you were acting hyper, had too much energy, or were not your usual self — in a way that went beyond just feeling good?"

**Why it matters:** Antidepressant monotherapy in Bipolar II can trigger hypomania, rapid cycling, or mixed states. Mood stabilizer is the foundation of treatment.

### 2. Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia)

**Key distinguishing features:**
- Depressed mood for **most of the day, more days than not, for ≥2 years** (1 year in children/adolescents)
- Fewer symptoms required (2+ of: appetite change, sleep change, fatigue, low self-esteem, poor concentration, hopelessness)
- Symptoms never absent for more than **2 months** at a time
- "Double depression" can occur: MDE superimposed on persistent depressive disorder

**Screening questions:**
- "Would you say you have felt down or depressed more days than not for the past two years or longer?"
- "Do people who know you well describe you as a pessimistic or gloomy person, or is this a change?"

**Why it matters:** Chronic course requires different treatment expectations. Combination of medication and psychotherapy often necessary.

### 3. Adjustment Disorder with Depressed Mood

**Key distinguishing features:**
- Onset within **3 months** of an identifiable stressor
- Does not meet full criteria for MDE (fewer than 5 symptoms or less than 2 weeks)
- Symptoms resolve within **6 months** of the stressor (or its consequences) ending
- Marked distress out of proportion to stressor severity, or significant functional impairment

**Screening questions:**
- "Did these feelings start after a specific event or change in your life?"
- "If that situation were resolved, do you think you would feel better?"

**Why it matters:** Often resolves with supportive therapy and stress management. Full antidepressant treatment may not be necessary.

### 4. Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

**Key distinguishing features:**
- History of exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence
- **Intrusion symptoms:** flashbacks, nightmares, intrusive memories
- **Avoidance:** of trauma-related stimuli
- **Negative alterations in cognition and mood:** which overlap substantially with MDD (guilt, detachment, negative beliefs, diminished interest)
- **Hyperarousal:** startle response, hypervigilance, sleep disturbance, irritability

**Screening questions:**
- "Have you experienced or witnessed a traumatic event that still affects you?"
- "Do you have nightmares, flashbacks, or intrusive memories of a past event?"

**Why it matters:** PTSD and MDD frequently co-occur (estimated 50% comorbidity). Trauma-focused therapy (CPT, PE, EMDR) is first-line for PTSD; treating only the depression misses the core pathology.

### 5. Normal Grief (Uncomplicated Bereavement)

**Key distinguishing features:**
- Occurs in the context of a significant loss
- Sadness comes in **waves** ("pangs of grief"), often triggered by reminders of the deceased — not constant
- **Self-esteem is preserved** — the bereaved person does not feel worthless as a person
- Positive memories and moments of humor or warmth are possible
- Content of thoughts centers on the deceased, not pervasive self-criticism
- Duration: acute grief typically peaks in first 6 months; gradual adaptation follows

**Important note:** The DSM-5-TR **removed the bereavement exclusion.** MDD can be diagnosed during grief if full criteria are met and the clinical picture is distinguishable from normal grief. Grief and MDD can co-occur.

**Screening questions:**
- "Are there times when you can feel some positive emotions or enjoy a good memory?"
- "Do you feel like you are a worthless person, or is the pain mostly about the loss?"

### 6. Prolonged Grief Disorder (new in DSM-5-TR)

**Key distinguishing features:**
- Intense grief and preoccupation with the deceased persisting ≥12 months after loss (≥6 months in children)
- Marked difficulty accepting the death, emotional numbness, identity disruption, avoidance of reminders
- Distinguished from normal grief by severity, duration, and functional impairment
- Distinguished from MDD by the content being centered on the deceased specifically

## Medical Differentials

### 7. Hypothyroidism

**Key distinguishing features:**
- Fatigue, weight gain, cognitive slowing, depressed mood — significant symptom overlap with MDD
- Also: cold intolerance, constipation, dry skin, hair loss, bradycardia
- **Diagnosis:** TSH and free T4 levels
- Prevalence: 5% of general population; higher in women and elderly

**Screening:** Thyroid function should be checked in all new presentations of depressive symptoms.

### 8. Vitamin B12 Deficiency

**Key distinguishing features:**
- Fatigue, cognitive impairment, mood changes — overlap with MDD
- Also: peripheral neuropathy (tingling, numbness), macrocytic anemia, glossitis
- Common in elderly, vegans/vegetarians, patients on metformin or PPIs
- **Diagnosis:** Serum B12, methylmalonic acid

### 9. Anemia (Iron Deficiency and Others)

**Key distinguishing features:**
- Fatigue, weakness, difficulty concentrating, irritability — overlap with MDD
- Also: pallor, dyspnea on exertion, tachycardia, pica (in severe iron deficiency)
- **Diagnosis:** CBC, iron studies, ferritin

### 10. Other Medical Conditions to Consider

- **Chronic fatigue syndrome / ME/CFS** — post-exertional malaise is the hallmark; not present in MDD
- **Sleep apnea** — fatigue, poor concentration, morning headaches; polysomnography diagnostic
- **Diabetes mellitus** — fatigue, weight changes, mood symptoms; HbA1c diagnostic
- **Chronic pain syndromes** — depression and pain share neurobiological pathways; bidirectional relationship
- **Neurological conditions** — Parkinson's disease, multiple sclerosis, stroke (especially left frontal)
- **Malignancy** — unexplained weight loss, fatigue; depression may be presenting symptom of occult cancer (especially pancreatic)

## Substance-Related Differentials

### 11. Substance/Medication-Induced Depressive Disorder

**Key distinguishing features:**
- Depressive symptoms that developed during or soon after substance intoxication, withdrawal, or medication use
- Symptoms resolve or improve with cessation of the substance
- Must exceed what is expected for the typical intoxication/withdrawal syndrome

**Common culprits:**
- Alcohol (depressant effects; also withdrawal)
- Cannabis (especially heavy, chronic use)
- Corticosteroids (prednisone, dexamethasone)
- Interferon-alpha
- Isotretinoin (Accutane)
- Hormonal contraceptives
- Certain antihypertensives (beta-blockers, reserpine)
- Benzodiazepine withdrawal
- Stimulant withdrawal (cocaine, amphetamine)

**Screening questions:**
- "Have you started or changed any medications recently?"
- "How much alcohol do you drink? Any recreational drug use?"
- "Did your mood change after starting a new medication?"

## Screening Context

DepScreen flags positive depression screens but cannot perform differential diagnosis. The screening output includes:

- A recommendation for professional evaluation on every positive screen
- A note that medical causes should be ruled out (thyroid, B12, anemia)
- A flag when substance use language is detected, prompting substance use assessment
- A note when trauma-related language is detected, suggesting PTSD screening
- A reminder that bipolar disorder must be ruled out before initiating antidepressant treatment

Clinicians receiving DepScreen results should use them as one data point within a comprehensive assessment that includes medical history, physical examination, laboratory studies, and structured clinical interview.

This information is for screening and psychoeducation purposes only and does not constitute a clinical diagnosis.
