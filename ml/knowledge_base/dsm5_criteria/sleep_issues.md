# Sleep Disturbance (Insomnia or Hypersomnia)

## Clinical Definition

**DSM-5-TR Criterion A4:** Insomnia or hypersomnia nearly every day.

Sleep disturbance is a neurovegetative symptom of Major Depressive Disorder reflecting disruption of circadian and homeostatic sleep regulation. It is one of the most prevalent symptoms, reported in approximately 80-90% of MDD cases.

## Duration Requirement

- Present **nearly every day** for a minimum of **2 consecutive weeks**
- Represents a change from the person's pre-episode sleep pattern
- Not solely attributable to environmental factors (noise, shift work, newborn care)

## How It Manifests

Depression affects sleep architecture in multiple ways:

### Insomnia Presentations
- **Initial insomnia** — difficulty falling asleep; lying awake 30+ minutes with racing, ruminative, or anxious thoughts
- **Middle insomnia** — waking during the night (often 2-4 AM) and struggling to return to sleep
- **Terminal insomnia (early morning awakening)** — waking 1-2 hours before the intended time, unable to return to sleep; classically associated with melancholic depression
- **Non-restorative sleep** — adequate hours in bed but waking feeling unrefreshed

### Hypersomnia Presentations
- Sleeping 10+ hours per night yet still feeling exhausted
- Difficulty getting out of bed; prolonged "sleep inertia"
- Excessive daytime napping without feeling rested
- Using sleep as escape from distressing thoughts or situations
- More common in atypical depression and younger patients

### Polysomnographic Findings (Clinician Note)
- Reduced sleep efficiency and total sleep time
- Shortened REM latency (earlier onset of first REM period)
- Increased REM density
- Reduced slow-wave (deep) sleep — may explain non-restorative sleep complaints

## Age-Specific Presentation

### Children and Adolescents
- Nightmares or sleep terrors may increase during depressive episodes
- Adolescents may show dramatic hypersomnia (sleeping 12-14 hours on weekends)
- Bedtime resistance in younger children may mask insomnia
- Circadian phase delay (staying up very late, sleeping very late) is common in teens and complicates assessment
- Screen use exacerbates sleep disruption and confounds clinical picture

### Older Adults (65+)
- Sleep architecture changes with aging (less deep sleep, more awakenings) — must distinguish age-related from depression-related changes
- Early morning awakening is particularly common and distressing
- Comorbid medical conditions (pain, nocturia, restless legs) complicate differential
- Medication effects on sleep must be assessed (beta-blockers, corticosteroids, diuretics)
- Sleep complaints may be the presenting complaint, overshadowing mood symptoms

### Perinatal
- Sleep disruption due to pregnancy discomfort and infant care must be distinguished from depression-related insomnia
- Inability to sleep even when the baby sleeps is a red flag for perinatal depression
- Hypersomnia beyond expected postpartum fatigue warrants screening
- Sleep deprivation itself is a risk factor for perinatal mood episodes

## Cultural Considerations

- **Middle Eastern cultures** — sleep complaints may be more socially acceptable to report than mood symptoms, making insomnia a common entry point for depression detection. Family co-sleeping norms affect sleep assessment.
- **East Asian cultures** — insomnia is a frequent presenting complaint in primary care; may be the primary idiom through which depression is expressed and help-seeking occurs.
- **Shift-work cultures** — in regions with high rates of shift work (industrial, healthcare, oil/gas), depression-related sleep disturbance must be differentiated from occupational disruption.
- **Religious practices** — early morning prayer schedules (e.g., Fajr) or fasting periods (e.g., Ramadan) alter sleep timing and must be accounted for in assessment.

## When It Becomes Concerning

Sleep disturbance becomes clinically significant when:

- It occurs **nearly every night for ≥2 weeks**
- It represents a **change from the person's usual sleep pattern**
- It significantly impairs **daytime functioning** (concentration, energy, mood, safety)
- It does **not respond** to basic sleep hygiene improvements
- It is accompanied by **other depressive symptoms**
- There is a **bidirectional worsening** — poor sleep worsens mood, and low mood worsens sleep

## Differentiating from Normal Variants

| Normal sleep variation | Clinical sleep disturbance |
|---|---|
| Occasional poor night due to stress or travel | Nearly every night for ≥2 weeks |
| Difficulty sleeping before an important event | Persistent without identifiable acute trigger |
| Catching up on sleep after a demanding week | Hypersomnia that does not resolve with rest |
| Temporary jet lag or schedule adjustment | Chronic disruption unrelated to schedule changes |
| Resolves with sleep hygiene changes | Persists despite behavioral interventions |

## Screening Context

DepScreen detects sleep disturbance through:

- Reports of difficulty falling asleep, staying asleep, or waking too early
- Descriptions of sleeping excessively yet feeling unrested
- Language about exhaustion despite adequate time in bed
- Mentions of nighttime rumination, racing thoughts, or anxiety at bedtime
- Temporal patterns ("I haven't slept well in weeks," "I sleep all day")
- Functional impact statements ("I'm so tired I can't function")

Sleep disturbance is often among the earliest symptoms to appear and the last to resolve, making it a sensitive marker for both depression onset and treatment response.

This information is for screening and psychoeducation purposes only and does not constitute a clinical diagnosis.
