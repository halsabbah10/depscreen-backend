## Sleep Disturbance (Insomnia or Hypersomnia)

Sleep disturbance is one of the nine DSM-5 criteria for Major Depressive Disorder. It can present as insomnia (difficulty sleeping) or hypersomnia (sleeping excessively), occurring nearly every day.

### How It Manifests

Depression affects sleep in different ways for different people:

**Insomnia may include:**
- Difficulty falling asleep, often lying awake with racing or repetitive thoughts
- Waking up in the middle of the night and struggling to fall back asleep
- Early morning awakening (waking hours before the alarm with an inability to return to sleep)
- Feeling restless or unrefreshed despite spending adequate time in bed

**Hypersomnia may include:**
- Sleeping significantly more than usual (10 or more hours) yet still feeling tired
- Difficulty getting out of bed in the morning
- Napping frequently during the day without feeling rested
- Using sleep as an escape from distressing emotions or situations

Sleep and mood have a bidirectional relationship. Poor sleep can worsen depressive symptoms, and depression can further disrupt sleep architecture. This cycle can be difficult to break without intervention.

### When It Becomes Concerning

Most people experience occasional sleep difficulty due to stress, schedule changes, or environmental factors. Sleep disturbance becomes clinically significant when:

- It occurs nearly every night for two weeks or more
- It significantly affects daytime functioning, energy, or concentration
- It does not improve with basic changes to sleep habits
- It appears alongside other symptoms such as depressed mood, fatigue, or appetite changes

Persistent sleep problems are among the earliest and most common signs of depression. Tracking your sleep patterns can provide useful information for a healthcare provider.

This information is for screening and awareness purposes only and is not a diagnosis.

Source: APA, DSM-5 (2013); NIMH; National Sleep Foundation
