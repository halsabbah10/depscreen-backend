## Why Language Matters

Language is not a neutral container for symptoms. The words a patient uses to describe their experience shape what the clinician hears, what gets documented, what gets diagnosed, and what gets treated. In multilingual clinical settings like Bahrain, where patients may speak Arabic, English, Hindi, Urdu, Tagalog, Bengali, or other languages, the gap between the patient's inner experience and the clinician's understanding can be significant.

Depression screening tools like the PHQ-9 were developed and validated in English. While Arabic-validated versions exist, translation is never purely linguistic. It involves mapping concepts across different emotional vocabularies, cultural frameworks, and levels of psychological literacy.

## Arabic Clinical Terminology for Depression

### Formal Clinical Terms

- **Iktiaab** (اكتئاب): The formal Arabic term for depression. Used in clinical and academic contexts. Many patients, especially those with lower educational levels or from non-urban backgrounds, may not recognize or use this term.
- **Idhtirab al-mizaj** (اضطراب المزاج): Mood disorder. Used in clinical documentation.
- **Qalaq** (قلق): Anxiety. Commonly used and widely understood. Patients may use this term even when describing depressive symptoms.
- **Huzn** (حزن): Sadness. A commonly understood term, but patients may distinguish between huzn (normal sadness) and iktiaab (clinical depression) differently than clinicians.

### How Patients Describe Symptoms

Patients who speak Arabic as their primary language often describe depressive symptoms differently from English-speaking patients:

| English clinical term | Common Arabic patient description | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Depressed mood | "Qalbi ta'aban" (my heart is tired), "ana za'laan" (I am upset/sad), "ana dayiq" (I am constricted) | Heart-centered language is common |
| Anhedonia | "Ma fi ta'am lil hayah" (life has no taste), "ma abi shay" (I do not want anything) | Loss of desire/flavor as metaphor |
| Fatigue | "Jismi thaqeel" (my body is heavy), "ma fini taqa" (I have no energy) | Somatic framing dominant |
| Insomnia | "Ma agdar anam" (I cannot sleep), "rasi mashghool" (my mind is busy/occupied) | Cognitive component acknowledged |
| Worthlessness | "Ana ma aswa shay" (I am not worth anything), "ana fayid" (I am useless) | Direct equivalents exist but may carry more shame |
| Concentration problems | "Rasi mush ma'i" (my mind is not with me), "ma agdar arakkiz" (I cannot focus) | Common and directly expressed |
| Suicidal ideation | "Abgha amoot" (I want to die), "ya reit ma khalaqni Allah" (I wish God had not created me) | Religious framing is common; does not diminish clinical significance |

### Important Nuances

- The Arabic word "za'laan" can mean sad, upset, angry, or annoyed depending on context and dialect. Clarify what the patient means.
- "Mashghool al-baal" (worried/preoccupied mind) may describe anxiety, depression, or both. Explore further.
- Some patients use "a'sabi" (nervous) as a catch-all for psychological distress without distinguishing anxiety from depression.
- Gulf Arabic (Khaleeji) dialects differ from Levantine, Egyptian, and North African Arabic. A term that is clear in one dialect may be confusing in another.

## Code-Switching in Clinical Settings

Many patients in Bahrain are bilingual or multilingual and may switch between Arabic and English during clinical encounters. This code-switching can be clinically informative:

- A patient who switches to English for medical terms ("I think I have depression") but describes the experience in Arabic may be compartmentalizing. The English term carries clinical legitimacy while the Arabic description carries emotional truth.
- Patients educated in English may lack the Arabic vocabulary for psychological concepts and vice versa. Neither language may fully capture their experience.
- Some patients use English as a distancing mechanism, discussing symptoms in a more detached, clinical register. This does not mean the distress is less real.

**Clinical recommendation**: Ask the patient which language they prefer for discussing their emotional state. "Which language feels most natural when you describe how you are feeling?" Then listen in that language.

## Translation Challenges in PHQ-9 and GAD-7

### PHQ-9 Translation Issues

The Arabic-validated PHQ-9 is widely used in the GCC, but several items present translation challenges:

- **Item 1 ("Little interest or pleasure in doing things")**: The concept of anhedonia maps well to Arabic, but the word "pleasure" (mut'a) may carry different connotations. "Have you stopped enjoying things you used to enjoy?" is often clearer.
- **Item 2 ("Feeling down, depressed, or hopeless")**: Three distinct concepts in one item. "Feeling down" does not translate directly. Clinicians may need to unpack this into: "Have you been feeling sad? Hopeless? Like nothing will get better?"
- **Item 7 ("Trouble concentrating on things, such as reading the newspaper or watching television")**: Newspaper reading is less culturally relevant in younger populations. Updating the example to phone use or conversation focus may improve validity.
- **Item 9 ("Thoughts that you would be better off dead, or of hurting yourself")**: This item must be handled with extreme care in Arabic. The phrase "better off dead" may need to be explicitly unpacked: "Have you had thoughts that you would rather not be alive? Have you thought about hurting yourself?"

### GAD-7 Translation Issues

- **"Feeling nervous, anxious, or on edge"**: The Arabic term "qalaq" covers anxiety well, but "on edge" (ala a'sabi) may be interpreted as irritability rather than anxiety in some dialects.
- **"Not being able to stop or control worrying"**: The concept of uncontrollable worry maps reasonably well to Arabic, but some patients may interpret "worry" (ham) as a moral or spiritual failing rather than a cognitive symptom.

## Recommendations for Clinicians

1. **Use the patient's preferred language for emotional content**, even if the clinical interaction is primarily in a different language.
2. **Clarify ambiguous terms.** Do not assume you understand what "za'laan" or "dayiq" means. Ask: "Can you tell me more about what that feels like?"
3. **Be aware of somatic idioms.** When a patient presents with "my heart hurts" or "my body is heavy," screen for depression even if the presenting complaint appears medical.
4. **Use back-translation mentally.** When using a translated PHQ-9, consider whether the patient is interpreting the question as you intend. If in doubt, rephrase.
5. **Document the language of the interview** and any points where translation may have affected clinical assessment.
6. **Provide interpreter services** for patients whose primary language is neither Arabic nor English. Do not use family members as interpreters for mental health assessments, as this compromises confidentiality and accuracy.
7. **Recognize that literacy varies.** Some patients may not be able to complete self-report questionnaires. Administer the PHQ-9 verbally when needed.

## Implications for DepScreen

DepScreen's AI-generated recommendations are currently in English. The system should:
- Acknowledge that screening scores may not fully capture the experience of patients who think and feel in Arabic or other languages.
- Use plain language in recommendations, avoiding clinical jargon that may not translate well.
- Encourage patients to discuss their screening results with a clinician who can explore cultural and linguistic dimensions.
- Consider offering key psychoeducation content in Arabic in future iterations.

This information is for clinical and educational purposes. Linguistic generalizations should be applied with care; individual patients' language use varies based on education, dialect, generation, and personal preference.
