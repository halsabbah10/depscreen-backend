# Substance Use and Depression Comorbidity

## Bidirectional Relationship

- Depression and substance use disorders (SUDs) co-occur at 2-3x the rate expected by chance
- **Depression → substance use**: Self-medication of negative affect, insomnia, anhedonia; alcohol and cannabis are the most commonly used substances
- **Substance use → depression**: Alcohol is a CNS depressant that disrupts serotonin, norepinephrine, and GABA; chronic use causes neuroadaptation that mimics or induces depressive states; cannabis use associated with amotivation and anhedonia
- **Shared vulnerability**: Genetic overlap, childhood adversity, and dysregulated stress response systems contribute to both

## Specific Substances

### Alcohol
- Most common comorbidity: ~30-40% of individuals with AUD have comorbid MDD
- Alcohol acutely relieves anxiety but worsens depression over days-weeks
- Heavy use disrupts sleep architecture (suppresses REM, causes early morning awakening)
- Withdrawal produces anxiety, dysphoria, and insomnia that mimic depression
- Rule of thumb: if depression persists after 2-4 weeks of sustained abstinence, it is likely independent MDD requiring treatment

### Cannabis
- Regular use associated with increased depression risk, especially with early-onset use
- THC can cause acute anxiety; chronic use associated with amotivation and emotional blunting
- Complicates treatment: reduced engagement in therapy, cognitive dulling, may counteract antidepressant benefit
- CBD alone has limited evidence for depression

### Opioids
- Chronic opioid use causes dysphoria, anhedonia, and emotional numbing
- Opioid use disorder and depression are highly comorbid; both involve reward circuitry dysfunction
- Buprenorphine (for OUD) may have antidepressant properties; methadone less so
- Pain-depression-opioid cycle requires integrated management

### Stimulants (Cocaine, Methamphetamine)
- Acute use produces euphoria; crash/withdrawal produces severe depressive symptoms
- Chronic stimulant use depletes monoamine systems, causing persistent anhedonia
- Depression during early abstinence is common and usually improves with sustained sobriety

## Screening Tools

- **AUDIT** (Alcohol Use Disorders Identification Test): 10 items; score ≥8 suggests hazardous drinking; ≥20 suggests dependence
- **AUDIT-C**: 3-item abbreviated version; score ≥4 (men) or ≥3 (women) is positive screen
- **CAGE**: 4 questions (Cut down, Annoyed, Guilty, Eye-opener); ≥2 positive answers = further assessment needed
- **DAST-10** (Drug Abuse Screening Test): 10 items for non-alcohol substance use
- Screen all depressed patients for substance use and vice versa

## Treatment: Integrated Approach

### Principles
- **Treat both conditions simultaneously** — sequential treatment (substance first, then depression) leads to worse outcomes for both
- **Integrated treatment programs** combining addiction counseling and depression treatment are superior to parallel or sequential models
- Do not withhold antidepressant treatment because of active substance use (unless medically contraindicated)

### Pharmacotherapy Considerations
- **SSRIs**: Safe in most SUDs; sertraline and fluoxetine have the most evidence in comorbid depression + AUD
- **SNRIs**: Venlafaxine, duloxetine — acceptable; monitor blood pressure
- **Bupropion**: May help with smoking cessation alongside depression; avoid in alcohol withdrawal (seizure risk)
- **Mirtazapine**: Can help with insomnia and appetite loss in early sobriety; some evidence in AUD
- **TCAs**: Avoid — lethal in overdose (higher risk in SUD population), anticholinergic burden, cardiac risk with alcohol
- **MAOIs**: Avoid — dangerous interactions with alcohol, tyramine, and many substances of abuse
- **Naltrexone**: For AUD; does not treat depression directly but reducing drinking often improves mood
- **Disulfiram**: For AUD; no antidepressant effect; drug interactions with some antidepressants

### Psychotherapy
- **CBT**: Adapted protocols address both depressive cognitions and substance use triggers/coping
- **Motivational Interviewing (MI)**: Enhances motivation for change; effective for ambivalent patients
- **Contingency Management**: Reinforces abstinence with tangible rewards; strongest evidence for stimulant use disorders
- **12-step facilitation**: Can be combined with professional treatment; social support reduces isolation

### Safety Considerations
- Assess overdose risk: depression + access to substances = elevated intentional overdose risk
- Alcohol + benzodiazepines: never co-prescribe; respiratory depression risk
- Alcohol + antidepressants: increased sedation, impaired judgment; counsel about interaction but do not withhold treatment
- Monitor hepatic function if prescribing medications metabolized by the liver in patients with heavy alcohol use

## Key Messages for Patients

- "Alcohol and drugs can temporarily relieve depression but make it worse over time."
- "You do not need to be completely sober before we can treat your depression — we can address both at the same time."
- "Reducing substance use will likely help your depression improve, and treating your depression may reduce the urge to use."
- "There is no shame in having both conditions — they are medically linked, and integrated treatment works."
