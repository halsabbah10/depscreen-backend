# Trauma, PTSD, and Depression Comorbidity

## Comorbidity Rates

- Approximately 50% of individuals with PTSD have comorbid MDD (Flory & Yehuda, 2015)
- Comorbid PTSD + MDD is associated with greater symptom severity, functional impairment, suicidality, and treatment resistance compared to either condition alone
- Trauma exposure without PTSD also increases depression risk — not all trauma leads to PTSD, but it can directly precipitate MDD
- Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs) dose-dependently increase lifetime risk of both depression and PTSD

## Symptom Overlap

PTSD and depression share several symptoms, complicating differential diagnosis:

| Symptom | In PTSD | In Depression |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| **Low mood / sadness** | Often present; part of PTSD "negative alterations in cognitions and mood" cluster | Core diagnostic criterion |
| **Anhedonia** | Loss of interest in previously enjoyed activities | Core diagnostic criterion |
| **Sleep disturbance** | Nightmares, hyperarousal-related insomnia | Insomnia or hypersomnia |
| **Concentration difficulties** | Hypervigilance-driven distractibility | Cognitive slowing, indecisiveness |
| **Social withdrawal** | Avoidance of trauma-related stimuli, emotional numbing | Loss of interest, fatigue, worthlessness |
| **Guilt** | Survivor guilt, self-blame about the trauma | Excessive, generalized guilt |
| **Irritability** | PTSD arousal cluster | Can be a depression feature (especially in adolescents/men) |

### Distinguishing Features
- **Re-experiencing symptoms** (flashbacks, intrusive memories, nightmares of the specific event) are PTSD-specific, not part of depression
- **Avoidance of trauma reminders** (specific places, people, conversations) is PTSD-specific
- **Hyperarousal** (exaggerated startle, hypervigilance, scanning for threat) is PTSD-specific
- Depression without PTSD does not involve re-experiencing or organized avoidance of specific stimuli

## Trauma-Informed Screening

### Principles
1. **Ask about trauma history routinely** — but frame questions with sensitivity: "Sometimes experiences in our past can affect how we feel now. Have you experienced anything that was frightening, harmful, or deeply upsetting?"
2. **Do not require details of the trauma** in a screening context — knowing that trauma occurred is sufficient to guide assessment; detailed recounting is for therapy, not intake
3. **Normalize disclosure**: "Many people have experienced difficult things. There is no judgment here."
4. **Avoid retraumatization**: Do not press for specifics if the patient shows distress. Signal safety: "You don't have to share more than you're comfortable with right now."
5. **Screen for PTSD** when trauma history is positive: PC-PTSD-5 (5 items, validated primary care screener; score ≥3 = positive screen) or PCL-5 (20-item PTSD Checklist)

### When to Screen
- Any patient presenting with depression who reports a trauma history
- Depression that is treatment-resistant or has prominent avoidance/numbing features
- Patients with comorbid substance use (trauma is a common underlying driver)
- Patients with chronic pain without clear medical explanation (somatization of trauma)

## Treatment Approaches

### Trauma-Focused Psychotherapies (First-Line for PTSD)

**Prolonged Exposure (PE)**
- Gradual, systematic exposure to trauma memories and avoided situations
- Typically 8-15 sessions, 60-90 minutes each
- Reduces avoidance and habituates fear response
- Strong evidence for PTSD; depression symptoms typically improve as PTSD resolves

**Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT)**
- Addresses maladaptive trauma-related beliefs ("It was my fault," "The world is completely unsafe," "I can't trust anyone")
- 12 sessions, structured protocol with written trauma accounts
- Directly addresses guilt, shame, and negative cognitions shared by PTSD and depression
- Developed and widely used in VA/military settings; effective in civilian populations

**Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR)**
- Uses bilateral stimulation (eye movements, tapping) during trauma memory processing
- Typically 8-12 sessions
- Less homework-intensive than PE or CPT; may be preferred by some patients
- Evidence base is strong, though mechanism is debated

### When to Refer for Trauma-Focused Therapy
- PTSD symptoms are prominent (re-experiencing, avoidance, hyperarousal)
- Depression is not responding to standard antidepressant treatment and trauma history is present
- Patient identifies trauma as central to their distress
- Standard depression treatment (SSRI + general CBT) is producing only partial response

### Pharmacotherapy
- **SSRIs**: Sertraline and paroxetine are FDA-approved for PTSD; SSRIs also treat comorbid depression
- **Venlafaxine XR**: Strong evidence for PTSD; treats depression simultaneously
- **Prazosin** (1-15 mg at bedtime): Alpha-1 antagonist; reduces PTSD-related nightmares; does not treat depression directly but improving sleep can improve mood
- **Avoid benzodiazepines**: Contraindicated in PTSD — they impair fear extinction, increase risk of developing PTSD after trauma, and carry dependence risk
- **Avoid re-experiencing triggers during medication initiation**: Start SSRIs at low doses in PTSD patients; activation and anxiety side effects may be more pronounced

### Integrated Treatment
- When both PTSD and depression are present, trauma-focused therapy is generally the priority — treating PTSD often resolves comorbid depression
- If depression is severe (PHQ-9 ≥20, suicidality) and impairing engagement in trauma therapy, stabilize with antidepressant first, then begin trauma-focused work
- CBT for depression alone (without trauma processing) may produce partial response if PTSD is unaddressed

## Safety Considerations

- Assess suicide risk carefully: comorbid PTSD + MDD carries higher suicide risk than either alone
- Screen for substance use (self-medication of PTSD symptoms)
- Assess for dissociation — may indicate complex PTSD and affect therapy approach
- Domestic violence or ongoing trauma: ensure safety planning before initiating trauma processing
- Military and veteran populations: screen for moral injury (guilt/shame about actions during service) as distinct from PTSD

## Key Messages for Patients

- "Difficult past experiences can affect your mood and how you feel now. This is a normal brain response, not a weakness."
- "There are specific therapies designed to help people process traumatic experiences safely. They are among the most effective treatments in mental health."
- "You do not have to share details of what happened until you are ready and in a safe therapeutic relationship."
- "Treating the effects of trauma often improves depression too — they are connected."
