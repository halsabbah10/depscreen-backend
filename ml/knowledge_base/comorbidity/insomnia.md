# Insomnia and Depression Comorbidity

## Prevalence and Relationship

- Approximately 80% of patients with MDD report insomnia symptoms; ~15-20% report hypersomnia
- Insomnia is both a symptom of depression and a risk factor for developing depression
- Insomnia doubles the risk of developing MDD (Baglioni et al., 2011, meta-analysis OR = 2.1)
- Residual insomnia after depression treatment is the strongest predictor of relapse
- The relationship is bidirectional: insomnia perpetuates depression through fatigue, cognitive impairment, and emotional dysregulation; depression perpetuates insomnia through rumination, hyperarousal, and circadian disruption

## Types of Sleep Disturbance in Depression

| Pattern | Presentation | Clinical Notes |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| **Sleep-onset insomnia** | >30 min to fall asleep | Often associated with comorbid anxiety, rumination |
| **Sleep-maintenance insomnia** | Frequent or prolonged awakenings | Most common pattern in depression |
| **Early morning awakening** | Waking 1-2+ hours before intended time, unable to return to sleep | Classic "melancholic" depression pattern |
| **Hypersomnia** | Sleeping ≥10 hours with persistent fatigue | More common in atypical depression, younger patients |
| **Non-restorative sleep** | Adequate duration but unrefreshing | May indicate sleep apnea, periodic limb movements, or depression-related sleep architecture disruption |

## CBT for Insomnia (CBT-I): First-Line Treatment

CBT-I is the recommended first-line treatment for chronic insomnia, including insomnia comorbid with depression (ACP, AASM, NICE guidelines). It is more effective than sleeping pills long-term and has no side effects.

### Core Components
1. **Sleep restriction**: Limit time in bed to actual sleep time (e.g., if sleeping 5 hours, only allow 5 hours in bed); gradually expand as sleep efficiency improves (target ≥85%)
2. **Stimulus control**: Bed is for sleep and sex only; if unable to sleep after 15-20 minutes, get up; no screens, reading, or worrying in bed; consistent wake time 7 days/week
3. **Cognitive restructuring**: Challenge catastrophic beliefs about sleep ("I won't function at all tomorrow"), unrealistic expectations, and effort to control sleep
4. **Sleep hygiene education**: Supportive but insufficient alone for clinical insomnia
5. **Relaxation training**: Progressive muscle relaxation, diaphragmatic breathing, body scan

### Format
- Typically 4-8 sessions, individual or group
- Digital CBT-I programs (Sleepio, Insomnia Coach) have RCT evidence
- Can be delivered by trained therapists, psychologists, or even primary care with training

### Evidence in Comorbid Depression
- Manber et al. (2008): Adding CBT-I to antidepressant treatment significantly improved both insomnia AND depression outcomes beyond antidepressant alone
- Treating insomnia directly accelerates depression remission

## Pharmacotherapy for Insomnia in Depression

### Antidepressants with Sedating Properties

**Trazodone (25-100 mg at bedtime)**
- Most commonly prescribed medication for insomnia in depression
- At low doses, primarily acts as a sedating antihistamine/5-HT2A antagonist (not antidepressant at this dose)
- Advantages: no dependence, no tolerance, can be added to SSRI/SNRI
- Side effects: orthostatic hypotension, morning sedation, priapism (rare)

**Mirtazapine (7.5-15 mg at bedtime)**
- NaSSA; more sedating at lower doses (antihistamine effect predominates)
- Dual benefit: treats depression and insomnia simultaneously
- Side effects: increased appetite, weight gain (limits use in some patients)
- May be the right antidepressant choice when insomnia and appetite loss are prominent

**Doxepin (3-6 mg at bedtime)**
- Ultra-low-dose TCA; FDA-approved for insomnia at this dose
- Selective H1 antihistamine at low doses; minimal anticholinergic effects
- Can be added to SSRI/SNRI

### Medications to Use Cautiously or Avoid

- **Benzodiazepines** (temazepam, lorazepam): Short-term only (2-4 weeks max); dependence, tolerance, cognitive impairment, fall risk; avoid in elderly, SUDs
- **Z-drugs** (zolpidem, eszopiclone, zaleplon): Better than benzodiazepines but still carry dependence risk; complex sleep behaviors (sleepwalking, sleep-driving); short-term use only
- **Antihistamines** (diphenhydramine, hydroxyzine): OTC sleep aids; tolerance develops rapidly; anticholinergic burden; not recommended for chronic insomnia
- **Melatonin/ramelteon**: Mild effect; may help with circadian rhythm issues; limited evidence for comorbid insomnia-depression

## Sleep Hygiene: Necessary but Not Sufficient

Sleep hygiene alone is insufficient for clinical insomnia. It is a foundation, not a treatment.

Key elements:
- Consistent wake time (most important single habit)
- Cool, dark, quiet sleep environment
- No caffeine after noon; no alcohol within 3 hours of bedtime (alcohol fragments sleep)
- Regular exercise (not within 2-3 hours of bedtime)
- Wind-down routine 30-60 minutes before bed (dim lights, no screens)
- No clock-watching during the night

## Assessment

- Screen with the Insomnia Severity Index (ISI): 7 items, score ≥15 = moderate clinical insomnia
- Keep a sleep diary for 1-2 weeks (bed time, wake time, sleep latency, awakenings, subjective quality)
- Rule out other sleep disorders: sleep apnea (snoring, witnessed apneas, obesity, daytime sleepiness → polysomnography), restless legs syndrome, circadian rhythm disorders
- PHQ-9 Item 3 captures sleep disturbance but does not distinguish type — clinical interview needed

**Sources**: Baglioni et al. (2011), Manber et al. (2008).
