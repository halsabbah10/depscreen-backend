# Eating Disorders and Depression Comorbidity

## Appetite and Weight Changes as Shared Symptoms

- Appetite and weight changes are core DSM-5 criteria for MDD (Criterion A3)
- Depression can present with decreased appetite and weight loss OR increased appetite and weight gain (atypical features)
- This symptom overlap makes differential diagnosis and comorbidity assessment essential
- Direction of appetite change has clinical implications: decreased appetite is more common in melancholic depression; increased appetite and carbohydrate craving are more common in atypical depression and seasonal affective disorder

## Comorbidity Rates

- Lifetime mood disorder prevalence in eating disorder patients: 40-80% (Godart et al., 2015)
- **Anorexia nervosa (AN)**: 40-60% comorbid MDD; depression may be partly secondary to malnutrition and starvation effects
- **Bulimia nervosa (BN)**: 50-70% comorbid MDD; mood instability linked to binge-purge cycles
- **Binge eating disorder (BED)**: 50-60% comorbid MDD; strongest bidirectional relationship; emotional eating as a core feature
- Depression often predates eating disorder onset, but eating disorders can also precipitate depression through nutritional deficiency, shame, social isolation, and functional impairment

## Key Clinical Considerations

### Body Image Disturbance
- Body dissatisfaction is a risk factor for both depression and eating disorders
- In depressed patients with significant body image concerns, screen for disordered eating behaviors (restriction, bingeing, purging, compulsive exercise)
- PHQ-9 Item 5 (appetite changes) may be an entry point for screening, but dedicated tools are needed

### Nutritional Effects on Mood
- Severe caloric restriction impairs serotonin synthesis (tryptophan is the precursor)
- Malnutrition causes fatigue, cognitive impairment, irritability, and depressed mood that mimic MDD
- In anorexia nervosa, weight restoration often improves depressive symptoms significantly — antidepressants are less effective in the malnourished state
- B-vitamin, zinc, iron, and omega-3 deficiencies are common in eating disorders and independently worsen mood

### Screening Tools
- **SCOFF**: 5-question screen for eating disorders (Sick, Control, One stone, Fat, Food)
- **EDE-Q** (Eating Disorder Examination Questionnaire): 28 items; gold standard self-report
- Screen depressed patients for eating disorders when: significant appetite/weight change is present, body image disturbance is reported, or the patient is adolescent/young adult

## Pharmacotherapy Considerations

### Bupropion Is Contraindicated in Bulimia Nervosa and Anorexia Nervosa
- **Seizure risk**: Bupropion lowers the seizure threshold; electrolyte imbalances from purging (hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia) and low body weight further increase this risk
- This is a black box contraindication — do not prescribe bupropion in patients with current or prior history of bulimia or anorexia
- If a depressed patient is on bupropion and develops an eating disorder, switch to a different antidepressant

### Recommended Pharmacotherapy
- **Fluoxetine**: FDA-approved for bulimia nervosa (60 mg/day — higher than typical depression dose); also first-line for MDD
- **SSRIs** (sertraline, escitalopram): First-line for MDD with comorbid BED; may reduce binge frequency
- **Lisdexamfetamine (Vyvanse)**: FDA-approved for moderate-to-severe BED; not an antidepressant but reduces binge episodes; monitor for misuse
- **TCAs**: Avoid in AN/BN (cardiac risk with electrolyte imbalance, lethal in overdose)
- **MAOIs**: Avoid in BN (dietary restriction requirements conflict with eating disorder management)

### For Anorexia Nervosa
- No medication has strong evidence for treating AN itself
- Antidepressants are generally ineffective during the underweight phase — treat depression after weight restoration
- Olanzapine (low-dose) may help with weight gain and anxiety in AN but is not an antidepressant

## Psychotherapy

- **CBT**: First-line for BN and BED; also addresses comorbid depression
- **CBT-E** (Enhanced CBT for eating disorders): Transdiagnostic approach that addresses the eating disorder psychopathology while also targeting mood disturbance
- **IPT**: Effective for BED and depression comorbidity; addresses interpersonal triggers for both
- **Family-Based Treatment (FBT/Maudsley)**: First-line for adolescent AN; parents take charge of refeeding

## Integrated Treatment Principles

1. Treat the eating disorder and depression simultaneously; do not wait for one to resolve before addressing the other
2. Nutritional rehabilitation is essential — antidepressants work poorly in malnourished states
3. Monitor weight, electrolytes, and vital signs in patients with restrictive or purging behaviors
4. Address body image disturbance as part of both conditions
5. Coordinate care between psychiatrist, therapist, dietitian, and medical provider
