# ADHD and Depression Comorbidity

## Overlapping Symptom Domains

ADHD and depression share several symptoms, making differential diagnosis challenging:

| Symptom | In ADHD | In Depression |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| **Poor concentration** | Chronic, lifelong; worse with boring tasks; can hyperfocus on interesting tasks | Episodic; worse during depressive episodes; global impairment |
| **Low motivation** | Task-specific (avoids tedious tasks); can be highly motivated for novel/rewarding tasks | Pervasive loss of interest/pleasure (anhedonia); applies to previously enjoyed activities |
| **Executive dysfunction** | Planning, organization, time management deficits; chronic | Decision-making difficulty, indecisiveness; episodic |
| **Fatigue** | Less common; more restlessness and hyperactivity | Core symptom; pervasive tiredness |
| **Sleep problems** | Difficulty settling at night (racing thoughts, stimulation-seeking); delayed sleep phase | Insomnia (onset, maintenance, or early awakening) or hypersomnia |
| **Emotional dysregulation** | Rapid mood shifts, low frustration tolerance, rejection sensitivity | Persistent low mood, guilt, worthlessness, hopelessness |
| **Forgetfulness** | Chronic, trait-like; "absent-minded" pattern | State-dependent; worse during episodes |

## Key Differential: Chronicity vs. Episodicity

The single most important diagnostic distinction:

- **ADHD is chronic**: Symptoms are present from childhood (before age 12 per DSM-5), persistent, and trait-like. The patient has "always been this way."
- **Depression is episodic**: Symptoms represent a change from baseline functioning. There was a period of normal concentration, motivation, and energy before the episode.

If a patient reports lifelong concentration difficulties that predate any mood episodes, ADHD should be evaluated. If concentration problems emerged alongside depressed mood, they are likely depressive cognitive symptoms.

## Comorbidity Prevalence

- ~20% of adults with MDD meet criteria for ADHD (McIntyre et al., 2010)
- ~50% of adults with ADHD experience at least one major depressive episode in their lifetime
- ADHD is a risk factor for depression through secondary pathways: chronic underachievement, relationship failures, low self-esteem, repeated negative feedback
- Comorbid ADHD + MDD predicts poorer antidepressant response, more treatment-resistant courses, and higher suicide risk

## Assessment

### For Suspected ADHD in a Depressed Patient
1. **Take a developmental history**: Were concentration, organization, and impulsivity problems present before age 12? Get collateral information (parent report, school records) if possible
2. **Assess during euthymia**: ADHD symptoms persist when depression remits; depressive cognitive symptoms resolve
3. **Use validated screens**: Adult ADHD Self-Report Scale (ASRS-v1.1) — 6-item screener; Conners Adult ADHD Rating Scale (CAARS) — comprehensive
4. **Rule out other causes**: Anxiety (concentration impaired by worry), substance use, sleep deprivation, thyroid dysfunction, and medications (sedating antihistamines, benzodiazepines)

### For Suspected Depression in an ADHD Patient
- PHQ-9 as standard screen
- Distinguish ADHD-related frustration and demoralization from clinical MDD
- ADHD-related emotional dysregulation may produce brief mood dips that do not meet duration criteria for MDD (requires ≥2 weeks)

## Treatment Sequencing

### General Principle: Treat Depression First, Then Reassess

1. **Treat MDD first**: Start antidepressant treatment (SSRI/SNRI per standard guidelines)
2. **Reassess concentration at remission**: Once PHQ-9 < 5 and mood has normalized, re-evaluate attention, organization, and executive function
3. **If cognitive symptoms persist in euthymia**: Formally assess for ADHD; consider neuropsychological testing
4. **If ADHD is confirmed**: Add ADHD-specific treatment to the existing antidepressant regimen

### Rationale for This Sequence
- Depressive cognitive symptoms can fully mimic ADHD; treating depression first removes this confound
- Starting stimulants during an active depressive episode is problematic: stimulants can worsen anxiety, insomnia, and appetite suppression
- Accurate ADHD diagnosis requires assessment during euthymic baseline

## Pharmacotherapy for Comorbid ADHD + MDD

### Antidepressants with Potential ADHD Benefit
- **Bupropion**: Norepinephrine-dopamine mechanism; modest evidence for ADHD symptoms; treats depression simultaneously; reasonable first-line choice for comorbid presentation
- **Venlafaxine/duloxetine**: Norepinephrine activity may provide some attention benefit; less direct ADHD evidence
- **Atomoxetine**: FDA-approved for ADHD (not depression); some antidepressant-like effects; can be combined with SSRIs

### Adding Stimulants to Antidepressants
- Methylphenidate or amphetamine-based medications can be added once depression is stabilized
- Monitor for: increased anxiety, insomnia, appetite suppression, cardiovascular effects (heart rate, blood pressure)
- Start at lowest effective dose; titrate slowly
- Drug interactions: stimulants with MAOIs are contraindicated (hypertensive crisis); generally safe with SSRIs/SNRIs/bupropion

### Non-Stimulant ADHD Medications
- **Atomoxetine** (40-80 mg/day): Selective NRI; slower onset (4-6 weeks); no abuse potential
- **Guanfacine XR** (1-4 mg/day): Alpha-2 agonist; helps with impulsivity and emotional dysregulation; sedation is common
- **Clonidine XR** (0.1-0.4 mg/day): Similar to guanfacine; more sedating

## Psychotherapy

- **CBT for adult ADHD**: Addresses procrastination, disorganization, time management, emotional dysregulation — complements depression-focused CBT
- **Skills training**: Practical organizational systems, planning tools, routines, accountability structures
- **Combined approach**: When both conditions are present, therapy can address both cognitive distortions (depression) and executive function deficits (ADHD) in an integrated framework

## Key Messages for Patients

- "Difficulty concentrating can be caused by depression, ADHD, or both. We need to figure out which is driving your symptoms."
- "We will treat your depression first, and then reassess your concentration. If it persists after your mood improves, we will evaluate for ADHD."
- "ADHD and depression are both real, treatable conditions. Having both does not make you harder to help — it means we need a more targeted treatment plan."
