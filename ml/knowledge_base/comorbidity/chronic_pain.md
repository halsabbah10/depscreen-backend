# Chronic Pain and Depression Comorbidity

## The Pain-Depression Cycle

- 30-50% of chronic pain patients have comorbid depression; 50-65% of depressed patients report significant pain
- The relationship is bidirectional and self-reinforcing:
  - Pain → reduced activity → social isolation → loss of role function → depressed mood
  - Depression → reduced pain threshold → catastrophizing → avoidance → deconditioning → more pain
- Shared neurobiology: Both involve serotonin and norepinephrine pathways, dysregulated HPA axis, neuroinflammation, and altered brain connectivity (anterior cingulate cortex, prefrontal cortex, insula)
- Each condition worsens the prognosis of the other: comorbid pain reduces antidepressant response; comorbid depression amplifies pain perception and disability

## Somatic Symptom Overlap

Depression and chronic pain share multiple symptom domains, complicating assessment:
- **Fatigue**: Present in both; contributes to functional impairment
- **Sleep disturbance**: Pain disrupts sleep; poor sleep worsens both pain and mood
- **Cognitive difficulties**: Pain and depression both impair concentration and memory ("fibro fog")
- **Appetite changes**: Reduced by depression; some pain conditions also affect appetite
- **Psychomotor changes**: Pain-related guarding and reduced mobility overlap with psychomotor retardation

When assessing PHQ-9 in chronic pain patients, acknowledge that some items (fatigue, sleep, concentration, psychomotor) may be influenced by pain. Do not dismiss elevated PHQ-9 scores as "just from the pain" — screen and treat depression regardless.

## Common Pain Conditions Comorbid with Depression

- **Fibromyalgia**: Central sensitization disorder; depression comorbidity ~50-70%; duloxetine and milnacipran FDA-approved
- **Chronic low back pain**: Most common chronic pain condition; depression doubles disability duration
- **Neuropathic pain**: Diabetic neuropathy, post-herpetic neuralgia; duloxetine and TCAs are dual-purpose
- **Chronic headache/migraine**: Depression comorbidity ~40%; amitriptyline prevents both migraine and treats depression
- **Arthritis (OA, RA)**: Pain, disability, and inflammation contribute to depression; biologics for RA may improve mood via anti-inflammatory effects

## Treatment: Dual-Target Approach

### Pharmacotherapy with Dual Efficacy

**Duloxetine (60-120 mg/day)**
- FDA-approved for MDD, GAD, diabetic neuropathic pain, fibromyalgia, and chronic musculoskeletal pain
- First-line when depression and pain coexist
- Number needed to treat (NNT): ~6 for 50% pain reduction in neuropathic pain
- Side effects: nausea (most common, usually transient), dry mouth, constipation, dizziness

**Venlafaxine XR (75-225 mg/day)**
- SNRI with analgesic properties at higher doses (≥150 mg for norepinephrine effect)
- Evidence for neuropathic pain, migraine prophylaxis, and depression
- Monitor blood pressure at higher doses

**TCAs (low-dose)**
- Amitriptyline (10-75 mg/day) or nortriptyline: Used for neuropathic pain, migraine prophylaxis, and fibromyalgia
- Analgesic effect at lower doses than antidepressant doses
- Side-effect burden limits use in elderly and cardiac patients

**Bupropion**
- No direct analgesic properties; but effective for depression without worsening pain
- Useful when SSRIs cause sexual dysfunction or emotional blunting in pain patients

### Psychotherapy

**CBT for Pain Management**
- Addresses catastrophizing, fear-avoidance beliefs, and pain-related disability
- Behavioral components: pacing, graded activity, relaxation training, sleep improvement
- Cognitive components: restructuring catastrophic pain beliefs, reducing helplessness
- Evidence: reduces pain intensity, disability, and depression simultaneously

**Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)**
- Emphasizes acceptance of pain experience rather than elimination
- Values-based activity engagement despite pain
- Growing evidence for chronic pain with comorbid depression

### Physical and Integrative Approaches
- **Graded exercise**: Start very low, increase gradually; combats deconditioning and improves mood
- **Physical therapy**: Targeted rehabilitation reduces fear-avoidance and disability
- **Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR)**: 8-week program with evidence for both chronic pain and depression

## Assessment Recommendations

- Screen all chronic pain patients for depression (PHQ-9)
- Screen all depressed patients for pain (ask about location, severity, duration, functional impact)
- Track both pain (0-10 NRS or Brief Pain Inventory) and depression (PHQ-9) longitudinally
- Do not attribute all symptoms to one condition — treat both actively

## Key Messages for Patients

- "Pain and depression are connected in the brain — treating one helps the other."
- "Some medications can treat both your pain and your mood simultaneously."
- "Staying active, even in small amounts, is one of the most important things you can do for both conditions."
- "CBT can help you manage both pain and low mood — it teaches practical skills for coping."
