# Medical Conditions Mimicking or Comorbid with Depression

## Why Medical Workup Matters

Multiple medical conditions can produce symptoms indistinguishable from MDD — fatigue, cognitive impairment, sleep disturbance, appetite changes, weight fluctuation, and low mood. Failing to identify a medical cause can result in ineffective antidepressant treatment while the underlying condition progresses.

**Guideline**: All patients presenting with new-onset depressive symptoms should have a basic medical evaluation, including targeted laboratory tests.

## Recommended Laboratory Workup for New-Onset Depression

| Test | Target Condition | Rationale |
|------|-----------------|-----------|
| **TSH** (Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone) | Hypothyroidism / Hyperthyroidism | Most important single test; hypothyroidism mimics depression almost exactly |
| **CBC** (Complete Blood Count) | Iron-deficiency anemia | Fatigue, cognitive impairment, poor concentration |
| **Vitamin B12** | B12 deficiency | Fatigue, cognitive decline, mood changes; common in elderly, vegans, metformin users |
| **Folate** | Folate deficiency | Depression risk factor; required for monoamine synthesis; check in pregnancy |
| **Fasting glucose / HbA1c** | Diabetes mellitus | Bidirectional depression comorbidity; fatigue, cognitive effects |
| **CMP** (Comprehensive Metabolic Panel) | Electrolyte, renal, hepatic abnormalities | Calcium (hypercalcemia causes fatigue/depression); liver/kidney dysfunction affects medication metabolism |
| **Vitamin D** | Vitamin D deficiency | Associated with depression; common in northern latitudes, indoor lifestyles |
| **Ferritin** | Iron stores (even without anemia) | Low ferritin causes fatigue before frank anemia develops |

Additional tests based on clinical suspicion: cortisol (Cushing's), testosterone (hypogonadism in males), ESR/CRP (inflammatory conditions), HIV, hepatitis B/C, RPR/VDRL (syphilis).

## Specific Medical Conditions

### Hypothyroidism
- **Prevalence**: 5-10% of adults; subclinical hypothyroidism even more common
- **Overlap with depression**: Fatigue, weight gain, cognitive slowing, depressed mood, constipation, cold intolerance, dry skin
- **Key**: TSH is the screening test; elevated TSH (>4.5 mIU/L) with low free T4 confirms diagnosis
- **Treatment**: Levothyroxine replacement; depressive symptoms often resolve with adequate thyroid correction
- **Clinical pearl**: Subclinical hypothyroidism (elevated TSH, normal free T4) can still cause depressive symptoms and may warrant treatment, especially if TSH >10 or anti-TPO antibodies are present

### Vitamin B12 Deficiency
- **Risk groups**: Elderly (decreased absorption), vegans/vegetarians, patients on metformin or proton pump inhibitors, post-bariatric surgery
- **Symptoms**: Fatigue, cognitive impairment ("brain fog"), mood changes, peripheral neuropathy, macrocytic anemia
- **Screening**: Serum B12; methylmalonic acid (MMA) is more sensitive for tissue-level deficiency
- **Treatment**: IM or high-dose oral B12 supplementation; neuropsychiatric symptoms may improve over weeks to months

### Folate Deficiency
- **Relevance to depression**: Folate is a cofactor in monoamine synthesis (serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine); deficiency impairs neurotransmitter production
- **L-methylfolate** (15 mg/day): FDA-recognized medical food as adjunct to antidepressants; MTHFR polymorphism may affect folate metabolism
- **Food sources**: Leafy greens, legumes, fortified grains

### Iron-Deficiency Anemia
- **Symptoms**: Fatigue, poor concentration, irritability, exercise intolerance, restless legs
- **Screening**: CBC (low hemoglobin, low MCV) + ferritin (most sensitive marker of iron stores)
- **Key**: Ferritin <30 ng/mL warrants supplementation even without frank anemia; fatigue may improve before hemoglobin normalizes

### Diabetes Mellitus
- **Bidirectional comorbidity**: Depression is 2-3x more common in diabetic patients; depression worsens glycemic control, adherence, and diabetic complications
- **Shared mechanisms**: Insulin resistance, inflammation, HPA axis dysregulation, vascular changes affecting brain function
- **Treatment considerations**: Avoid medications that worsen glycemic control (mirtazapine, olanzapine — weight gain); bupropion may have mild weight-neutral to weight-reducing effects

### Cardiovascular Disease
- **Depression post-MI**: 20-30% prevalence; associated with 2-3x increased mortality risk
- **Mechanism**: Autonomic dysregulation, platelet activation, inflammation, reduced adherence to cardiac rehab
- **Treatment**: Sertraline has the strongest cardiac safety data (SADHART trial); escitalopram also safe; avoid TCAs (cardiac conduction delays, arrhythmia risk)

### Chronic Fatigue Syndrome / Myalgic Encephalomyelitis (ME/CFS)
- **Overlap**: Profound fatigue, cognitive impairment, sleep disturbance, pain
- **Key differentiator**: Post-exertional malaise (PEM) — symptom worsening after physical or cognitive exertion — is characteristic of ME/CFS but not depression
- **Important**: Do not assume CFS is "just depression." CBT and graded exercise therapy remain controversial in ME/CFS; antidepressants may help comorbid depression but do not treat the underlying condition

## When to Refer

- Abnormal lab results: refer to appropriate specialist (endocrinology for thyroid, hematology for persistent anemia, etc.)
- Medical condition identified as primary cause: treat the medical condition first; reassess depression after correction
- Depression persisting after medical condition is treated: proceed with standard MDD treatment
- Both conditions present and independent: treat both simultaneously

## Key Messages for Patients

- "Some medical conditions can cause symptoms that look and feel exactly like depression. A blood test can check for these."
- "If we find a medical cause, treating it may improve your mood without needing an antidepressant."
- "Even if a medical condition is contributing, you may also have depression — and both can be treated."
