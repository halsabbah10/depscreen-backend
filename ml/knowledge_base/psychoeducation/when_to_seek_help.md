## Why This Matters

Many people wait months or even years before seeking professional support for depression. Common reasons include minimizing symptoms ("it's not that bad"), feeling shame or weakness, believing it should be possible to handle things alone, or simply not recognizing that what they are experiencing is depression. Seeking help is not a sign of weakness. It is a practical, courageous, and often necessary step toward recovery.

Depression is a medical condition with effective treatments. The earlier it is addressed, the better the outcomes tend to be.

## Signs It May Be Time to Seek Help

Consider reaching out to a healthcare provider or mental health professional if you recognize any of the following:

- You have been feeling persistently **sad, empty, or hopeless for two weeks or more**. Two weeks is the clinical threshold used in the DSM-5 for a depressive episode.
- You have **lost interest or pleasure** in activities you used to enjoy (anhedonia). Things that once felt rewarding now feel flat or meaningless.
- Your symptoms are **interfering with daily life**: work performance has dropped, relationships are strained, self-care tasks feel overwhelming, or you are falling behind on responsibilities.
- You are experiencing changes in **sleep** (difficulty falling asleep, waking too early, sleeping excessively) that do not improve on their own.
- Your **appetite has changed significantly** (eating much more or much less than usual) or you have had unexplained weight changes.
- You feel **persistent fatigue or loss of energy** that is not explained by physical activity or sleep deprivation.
- You are experiencing **difficulty concentrating, remembering things, or making decisions** that is new or worsening.
- You feel **worthless, excessively guilty**, or have harsh, unrelenting self-critical thoughts that feel disproportionate to the situation.
- You are **withdrawing** from friends, family, or social activities that you previously valued.
- You are using **alcohol, drugs, or other substances** to cope with your feelings.
- People close to you have expressed **concern** about changes in your behavior, mood, or functioning.

## The PHQ-9 as a Self-Assessment Guide

The PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire-9) is a validated screening tool used worldwide. While not a diagnostic instrument, it provides useful thresholds:

- **Score 0-4**: Minimal depression. Monitor and practice self-care.
- **Score 5-9**: Mild depression. Consider watchful waiting; seek help if symptoms persist beyond two weeks or worsen.
- **Score 10-14**: Moderate depression. **Professional evaluation is recommended.** This is the threshold at which evidence-based treatment (therapy, medication, or both) typically provides meaningful benefit.
- **Score 15-19**: Moderately severe depression. Active treatment is strongly recommended.
- **Score 20-27**: Severe depression. Immediate professional evaluation is important. Combination treatment (therapy plus medication) is typically indicated.

A score of 10 or above, or answering "more than half the days" or "nearly every day" on the question about thoughts of death or self-harm (Question 9), warrants a conversation with a healthcare provider regardless of total score.

## Seek Help Immediately If

- You are having **thoughts of death or suicide**.
- You are thinking about **harming yourself**.
- You have a **plan or means** to end your life.
- You feel you are **in crisis** or cannot keep yourself safe.

## Self-Assessment Prompts

If you are unsure whether to seek help, ask yourself these questions honestly:

1. Has my mood, energy, or interest in life been noticeably different for two weeks or more?
2. Am I functioning at a level that is clearly below my normal capacity at work, school, or home?
3. Have I stopped doing things I used to enjoy, not because of time constraints but because nothing feels worth doing?
4. Am I having trouble sleeping, eating, or taking care of basic needs?
5. Do I find myself thinking that things will never get better, that I am a burden, or that the world would be better off without me?
6. Have people who know me well expressed concern about how I seem?

If you answered yes to two or more of these questions, speaking with a healthcare provider is a reasonable and worthwhile step.

## How to Talk to Your Doctor

- **Be honest about what you are experiencing.** You do not need to use clinical language. Saying "I feel flat and I can't enjoy anything anymore" or "I've been crying every day for three weeks" is clear enough.
- **Mention the duration.** "This has been going on for about six weeks" gives your doctor important context.
- **Bring up functional impact.** "I've missed four days of work this month" or "I'm not eating properly" helps your doctor understand severity.
- **Ask about your options.** Treatment for depression is not one-size-fits-all. Ask what your doctor recommends and why.
- **It is okay to bring notes.** Writing down your symptoms and questions before the appointment can help if you feel nervous or forgetful.

## What to Expect When You Seek Help

- Your provider will likely ask about your symptoms, their duration, and their impact on your life.
- They may use a screening questionnaire like the PHQ-9 or GAD-7.
- They will ask about your medical history and any medications you are taking.
- They may order blood tests to rule out medical causes (thyroid dysfunction, vitamin deficiencies, anemia).
- Together, you will discuss treatment options, which may include therapy, medication, lifestyle changes, or a combination.
- The first visit is informational and collaborative. You will not be pressured into any treatment you are not comfortable with.

## You Do Not Have to Be in Crisis to Deserve Support

If something feels wrong, that is reason enough to reach out. Depression is not a character flaw, and it is not something you need to earn the right to treat. Early intervention leads to better outcomes.
