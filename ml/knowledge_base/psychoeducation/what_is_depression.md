## What Is Depression?

Depression, clinically known as Major Depressive Disorder (MDD), is a medical condition that affects how you feel, think, and function. It is not a personal weakness, a character flaw, or something you can simply "snap out of." It is a real illness with measurable biological, psychological, and social dimensions.

### The Biopsychosocial Model

Depression arises from the interaction of three domains:

**Biological factors:**
- Genetics account for roughly 30-40% of risk. Having a first-degree relative with depression doubles your likelihood.
- Neurotransmitter systems (serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine) show altered function during depressive episodes.
- The hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, which governs the stress response, is often overactive in depression, leading to elevated cortisol.
- Neuroimaging studies show reduced volume in the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex, and altered activity in the amygdala.
- Chronic inflammation (elevated IL-6, TNF-alpha, CRP) is increasingly recognized as both a contributor and consequence.

**Psychological factors:**
- Negative cognitive patterns (e.g., "I'm worthless," "nothing will ever improve") maintain depressive episodes.
- Rumination — repetitive, passive focus on distress — worsens and prolongs symptoms.
- Early adverse experiences shape vulnerability through lasting effects on stress response and self-concept.

**Social factors:**
- Stressful life events (loss, conflict, financial hardship) are the most common triggers.
- Social isolation both contributes to and results from depression, creating a self-reinforcing cycle.
- Socioeconomic disadvantage, discrimination, and lack of access to care increase risk.

### How Common Is It?

Depression is one of the most prevalent health conditions worldwide:

- Approximately 280 million people globally experience depression (WHO, 2023).
- It is the leading cause of disability worldwide.
- Lifetime prevalence is roughly 15-20% in high-income countries.
- Depression can occur at any age but most commonly first appears between ages 18 and 25.
- It affects people of all backgrounds, genders, and socioeconomic levels — though women are diagnosed at roughly twice the rate of men, partly due to biological factors and partly due to differences in how symptoms present and are reported.

### More Than Sadness

Everyone experiences sadness, grief, or low periods. Depression is different in three important ways:

1. **Duration:** Symptoms persist for at least two weeks, most of the day, nearly every day.
2. **Severity:** Symptoms significantly interfere with daily functioning — work, relationships, self-care.
3. **Breadth:** Depression affects multiple systems simultaneously — mood, energy, sleep, appetite, concentration, motivation, and sense of self-worth.

### Common Misconceptions

- "Depression means you're sad all the time." Many people with depression describe feeling empty or numb rather than sad. Loss of interest or pleasure (anhedonia) is equally central.
- "You need a reason to be depressed." Depression can occur without an obvious trigger due to biological vulnerability.
- "If you can still function, it's not real depression." Many people with depression maintain routines through enormous effort. Functional depression is still depression.
- "Medication changes who you are." Antidepressants treat the illness — they do not alter personality.
- "Children and older adults don't get depressed." Depression affects all age groups, though it may present differently (irritability in children, somatic complaints in older adults).

### The Good News: Depression Is Treatable

Depression is one of the most treatable mental health conditions:

- 60-70% of people respond to first-line treatment (psychotherapy, medication, or both).
- Combination treatment (therapy plus medication) has the highest response rates for moderate-to-severe depression.
- Even treatment-resistant depression has effective options, including augmentation strategies and newer interventions.

If you think you or someone you know may be experiencing depression, reaching out to a healthcare provider is an important first step. Early intervention leads to better outcomes.
