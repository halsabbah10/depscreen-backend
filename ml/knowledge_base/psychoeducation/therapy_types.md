## Types of Therapy for Depression

Several forms of psychotherapy have strong evidence for treating depression. Understanding the main approaches can help you make informed decisions about your care. What works best varies from person to person, and a good therapeutic relationship is one of the strongest predictors of positive outcomes regardless of the approach.

### Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

CBT is the most extensively researched therapy for depression. It focuses on identifying and changing negative thought patterns and behaviors that maintain depressive symptoms. Key techniques include cognitive restructuring (challenging distorted thoughts), behavioral activation (re-engaging with rewarding activities), and problem-solving skills. CBT is typically structured, time-limited (12 to 20 sessions), and goal-oriented.

### Interpersonal Therapy (IPT)

IPT focuses on improving interpersonal relationships and communication patterns that may contribute to or worsen depression. It addresses four key areas: grief, role disputes, role transitions, and interpersonal deficits. IPT is typically 12 to 16 sessions and has strong evidence for treating major depression.

### Behavioral Activation (BA)

BA is both a standalone therapy and a component of CBT. It focuses specifically on increasing engagement with meaningful, rewarding activities to counteract the withdrawal and avoidance that characterize depression. Research shows BA can be as effective as full CBT and antidepressant medication.

### Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)

ACT helps people develop psychological flexibility by accepting difficult thoughts and feelings rather than fighting them, while committing to actions aligned with personal values. It uses mindfulness, acceptance, and values-clarification techniques.

### Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT)

Originally developed for borderline personality disorder, DBT skills (mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, interpersonal effectiveness) are increasingly used for depression, especially when emotional dysregulation is prominent.

### Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT)

MBCT combines CBT principles with mindfulness meditation practices. It is particularly effective for preventing relapse in people who have experienced three or more depressive episodes.

### Medication

While not a form of therapy, antidepressant medications (such as SSRIs and SNRIs) are an important treatment option, often used alongside psychotherapy. A psychiatrist or prescribing provider can help determine whether medication may be appropriate for your situation.

### Choosing the Right Approach

There is no single "best" therapy for everyone. Consider your preferences, the severity of your symptoms, and practical factors like availability and cost. Many therapists integrate techniques from multiple approaches. The most important step is starting the conversation.
