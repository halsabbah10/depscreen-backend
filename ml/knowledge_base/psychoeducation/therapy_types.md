## Overview

Several forms of psychotherapy have strong research support for treating depression. No single therapy is best for everyone. What matters most is finding an approach that fits your needs and a therapist you feel comfortable working with. The therapeutic relationship itself is one of the strongest predictors of positive outcomes, regardless of the specific approach used.

## Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

CBT is the most extensively researched psychotherapy for depression, with decades of clinical trial evidence supporting its effectiveness. It works by helping you identify and change the negative thought patterns and behaviors that maintain depressive symptoms.

**Core techniques:**
- **Cognitive restructuring**: Learning to recognize distorted thinking patterns (such as all-or-nothing thinking, catastrophizing, or personalization) and develop more balanced perspectives.
- **Behavioral activation**: Gradually re-engaging with activities that provide pleasure or a sense of accomplishment.
- **Problem-solving skills**: Breaking overwhelming problems into manageable steps.
- **Behavioral experiments**: Testing negative predictions against reality.

**What to expect:** CBT is typically structured, time-limited (12 to 20 sessions), and goal-oriented. Your therapist will likely assign homework between sessions. Research shows that gains from CBT tend to be durable, with lower relapse rates compared to medication alone.

## Interpersonal Therapy (IPT)

IPT focuses on improving the interpersonal relationships and communication patterns that contribute to or worsen depression. Rather than examining thought patterns, IPT addresses the social context of your depression.

**Four focus areas:**
- **Grief**: Complicated or unresolved mourning.
- **Role disputes**: Conflicts with significant others (partner, family, coworker).
- **Role transitions**: Adjusting to major life changes (retirement, divorce, new parenthood, migration).
- **Interpersonal deficits**: Patterns of social isolation or difficulty forming relationships.

**What to expect:** IPT is typically 12 to 16 sessions. It has strong evidence for treating major depression and is particularly well-suited for depression triggered by interpersonal difficulties or life transitions.

## Behavioral Activation (BA)

BA is both a standalone therapy and a component of CBT. It specifically targets the withdrawal and avoidance that characterize depression. The core principle is that action precedes motivation: you do not need to feel like doing something to benefit from doing it.

**What to expect:** BA involves tracking your activities and their effect on mood, then gradually scheduling more activities that provide pleasure or mastery. Multiple clinical trials have found BA to be as effective as full CBT and antidepressant medication for moderate depression.

## Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT)

MBCT combines CBT principles with mindfulness meditation practices. It teaches you to observe thoughts and feelings without judgment, breaking the automatic negative thinking patterns that fuel depressive relapse.

**What to expect:** MBCT is typically delivered in 8-week group programs. It is particularly effective for preventing relapse in people who have experienced three or more depressive episodes. The evidence for relapse prevention is strong, with reductions of approximately 40 to 50 percent compared to treatment as usual.

## Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)

ACT helps develop psychological flexibility by accepting difficult thoughts and feelings rather than fighting them, while committing to actions aligned with personal values. It does not aim to change the content of your thoughts but to change your relationship with them.

**Core processes:** Cognitive defusion (distancing from thoughts), acceptance, present-moment awareness, self-as-context, values clarification, and committed action.

**What to expect:** ACT can be delivered individually or in groups. It is effective for depression, particularly when depression involves experiential avoidance or a sense of being stuck and disconnected from personal values.

## Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) Skills

Originally developed for borderline personality disorder, DBT teaches four skill sets that are increasingly used for depression, especially when emotional dysregulation is prominent:

- **Mindfulness**: Observing and describing experience without judgment.
- **Distress tolerance**: Surviving crisis moments without making things worse.
- **Emotion regulation**: Understanding and managing intense emotions.
- **Interpersonal effectiveness**: Asking for what you need while maintaining relationships and self-respect.

**What to expect:** Full DBT includes individual therapy, skills group, phone coaching, and therapist consultation team. For depression specifically, the skills training component is often used as an adjunct to other treatments.

## Medication

While not a form of psychotherapy, antidepressant medications are an important treatment option and are often used alongside therapy. The most commonly prescribed classes include SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors) and SNRIs (serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors). A psychiatrist or prescribing provider can evaluate whether medication is appropriate for your situation. For moderate to severe depression, the combination of therapy and medication is often more effective than either alone.

## What to Expect in Your First Session

Regardless of approach, a first therapy session typically involves:

- The therapist asking about your current symptoms, their history, and what brought you to seek help now.
- A discussion of your goals for therapy.
- Questions about your medical history, family history, and current life situation.
- An explanation of how the therapist works, including confidentiality and its limits.
- An opportunity for you to ask questions and decide if this feels like a good fit.

It is normal to feel nervous. You do not need to have everything figured out before your first session.

## How to Find a Therapist

- Ask your primary care provider for a referral.
- Check your insurance provider's directory for in-network therapists.
- Use professional directories such as Psychology Today, the APA Psychologist Locator, or your country's equivalent professional body.
- Community mental health centers often offer sliding-scale or subsidized services.
- Many therapists now offer telehealth sessions, which can increase accessibility.
- If cost is a barrier, ask about sliding-scale fees, training clinics at universities, or Employee Assistance Programs (EAPs).

The most important step is starting the conversation. If the first therapist is not a good fit, that is normal. Finding the right match sometimes takes more than one try.
