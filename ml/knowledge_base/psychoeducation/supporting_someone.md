## Supporting Someone with Depression

When someone you care about is experiencing depression, it can be difficult to know what to say, what to do, or how to help without overstepping. This guide is for family members, friends, and partners who want to support someone through a depressive episode.

### Understanding What Depression Looks Like from the Inside

Depression is not laziness, ingratitude, or a choice. The person you care about may:

- Withdraw from activities and relationships — not because they do not care, but because everything requires enormous effort.
- Seem irritable or distant rather than visibly sad.
- Have difficulty making decisions, even about small things.
- Sleep much more or much less than usual.
- Neglect personal care, household tasks, or responsibilities.
- Express hopelessness, guilt, or worthlessness that seems disproportionate to their circumstances.

These are symptoms of the illness, not reflections of their character or their feelings about you.

### What to Say

**Helpful things:**
- "I'm here for you, and I'm not going anywhere."
- "You don't have to explain or justify how you feel."
- "What would be most helpful for you right now?"
- "I've noticed you seem to be having a hard time. I care about you."
- "You don't have to face this alone."
- "It's okay to not be okay."

**When they express hopelessness:**
- "I hear you. This sounds really painful."
- "Depression is lying to you about your worth and your future."
- "Treatment works for most people. This can get better."

### What NOT to Say

These are well-intentioned but harmful:
- "Just cheer up" / "Look on the bright side" / "Think positive."
- "Other people have it worse."
- "You have so much to be grateful for."
- "Have you tried exercising / yoga / going outside?"
- "You just need to push through it."
- "I know how you feel" (unless you genuinely have experienced clinical depression).
- "You're bringing everyone down."

These statements minimize the person's experience and often increase shame and withdrawal.

### Practical Ways to Help

Depression impairs executive function, making daily tasks feel overwhelming. Practical support can be more helpful than words:

- **Offer specific help** instead of "let me know if you need anything." Try: "I'm going to the store — can I pick up groceries for you?" or "Can I help you make that appointment?"
- **Maintain gentle contact.** Send a text saying you are thinking of them. Do not require a response.
- **Invite without pressure.** "I'm going for a walk — you're welcome to join, but no pressure." Repeated low-pressure invitations matter more than one big gesture.
- **Help with logistics.** Offer to research therapists, drive them to appointments, or sit with them while they make a phone call.
- **Reduce decision load.** Instead of "what do you want for dinner?" try "I made pasta — want some?"

### When to Involve Professionals

Contact a professional or emergency services if the person:

- Expresses suicidal thoughts or a plan to harm themselves.
- Talks about being a burden to others.
- Gives away possessions or says goodbye.
- Shows a sudden shift from deep depression to calm (this can indicate a decision has been made).
- Engages in self-harm.
- Is unable to care for themselves (not eating, not getting out of bed for extended periods).

You do not need their permission to call for help if you believe they are in immediate danger. Contact 999 (Bahrain) or your local emergency number.

### Setting Boundaries

Supporting someone with depression does not mean sacrificing your own well-being:

- You are not their therapist. You cannot and should not try to be.
- It is okay to say: "I love you and I want to help, but I'm feeling overwhelmed right now. Can we find you some professional support?"
- You are allowed to have your own feelings about the situation — frustration, helplessness, grief.
- Supporting someone is a marathon, not a sprint. Pace yourself.

### Caregiver Burnout

Burnout among family members and caregivers of people with depression is common and serious. Watch for these signs in yourself:

- Persistent exhaustion or feeling emotionally drained.
- Resentment toward the person you are caring for.
- Neglecting your own health, relationships, or responsibilities.
- Feeling helpless or hopeless about the situation.
- Withdrawal from your own social connections.

**If you recognize these signs:**
- Seek your own support — a therapist, a support group (NAMI Family-to-Family is evidence-based), or a trusted friend.
- Maintain activities that sustain you.
- Remember: you cannot pour from an empty cup. Taking care of yourself is not selfish — it is necessary.

### Key Takeaways

- Show up consistently. Presence matters more than having the perfect words.
- Listen more than you advise.
- Resist the urge to fix. Validate their experience.
- Encourage professional help without ultimatums.
- Take care of yourself throughout the process.
