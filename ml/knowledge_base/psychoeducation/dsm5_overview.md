## DSM-5 Criteria for Major Depressive Disorder: An Overview

The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition (DSM-5), published by the American Psychiatric Association, provides the standard criteria used by mental health professionals to diagnose Major Depressive Disorder (MDD). Understanding these criteria can help you recognize when symptoms may warrant professional evaluation.

### The Nine Symptoms

To meet DSM-5 criteria for MDD, a person must experience **five or more** of the following symptoms during the **same two-week period**, with at least one being either (1) depressed mood or (2) loss of interest or pleasure:

1. **Depressed mood** most of the day, nearly every day (feeling sad, empty, or hopeless; in children/adolescents, may present as irritability)
2. **Markedly diminished interest or pleasure** (anhedonia) in all or almost all activities
3. **Significant weight loss or gain**, or decrease or increase in appetite
4. **Insomnia or hypersomnia** nearly every day
5. **Psychomotor agitation or retardation** observable by others
6. **Fatigue or loss of energy** nearly every day
7. **Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt** that may be delusional in nature
8. **Diminished ability to think, concentrate, or make decisions**
9. **Recurrent thoughts of death**, suicidal ideation, or suicidal behavior

### Important Qualifications

The DSM-5 also requires that:

- The symptoms cause **clinically significant distress** or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning
- The episode is **not attributable** to the physiological effects of a substance or another medical condition
- The episode is **not better explained** by another mental disorder

### What This Means for Screening

Screening tools like the PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire-9) are based on these nine criteria. They help identify people who may benefit from further evaluation but are not diagnostic on their own. A formal diagnosis requires a comprehensive clinical assessment by a qualified professional.

Experiencing some of these symptoms does not automatically mean you have depression, but it does suggest that talking with a healthcare provider could be helpful.

This information is for educational and awareness purposes only and is not a diagnosis.
