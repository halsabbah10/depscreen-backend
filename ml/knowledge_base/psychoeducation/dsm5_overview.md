## What Is the DSM-5?

The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition (DSM-5), published by the American Psychiatric Association (APA) in 2013 and updated as the DSM-5-TR in 2022, is the standard classification system used by mental health professionals in the United States and many other countries to diagnose mental health conditions. It provides a common language for clinicians, researchers, and insurance systems.

The DSM-5 is not a checklist that produces an automatic diagnosis. It is a framework that requires trained clinical judgment. Two people with the same diagnosis may present very differently, and the manual explicitly recognizes cultural, developmental, and individual variation.

## How Diagnosis Works

A clinician diagnosing Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) conducts a comprehensive assessment that includes a clinical interview, a review of medical history, collateral information when available, and sometimes standardized questionnaires. The DSM-5 criteria provide structure, but the diagnosis ultimately rests on the clinician's judgment about whether the pattern of symptoms, their duration, and their impact meet the threshold for a disorder.

Diagnosis is not a label applied to a person. It is a clinical description of a pattern of suffering that has been studied well enough to guide treatment.

## The Nine Symptom Criteria for MDD

To meet DSM-5 criteria for a Major Depressive Episode, a person must experience **five or more** of the following symptoms during the **same two-week period**, with at least one being symptom (1) or (2):

1. **Depressed mood** most of the day, nearly every day. May present as sadness, emptiness, or hopelessness. In children and adolescents, may appear as irritability.
2. **Markedly diminished interest or pleasure** (anhedonia) in all, or almost all, activities most of the day, nearly every day.
3. **Significant weight change** (more than 5% in a month) or a notable change in appetite nearly every day.
4. **Insomnia or hypersomnia** nearly every day.
5. **Psychomotor agitation or retardation** observable by others (not merely subjective restlessness or slowing).
6. **Fatigue or loss of energy** nearly every day.
7. **Feelings of worthlessness** or excessive or inappropriate guilt (which may be delusional), nearly every day.
8. **Diminished ability to think, concentrate, or make decisions**, nearly every day.
9. **Recurrent thoughts of death**, recurrent suicidal ideation without a specific plan, a suicide attempt, or a specific plan for committing suicide.

## Required Qualifications

The DSM-5 requires that all of the following also be true:

- The symptoms cause **clinically significant distress or impairment** in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.
- The episode is **not attributable** to the physiological effects of a substance (e.g., alcohol, medication) or another medical condition (e.g., hypothyroidism).
- The episode is **not better explained** by schizoaffective disorder, schizophrenia, or another psychotic disorder.
- There has **never been a manic or hypomanic episode** (which would indicate bipolar disorder instead).

## The Role of Clinical Judgment

Criteria are necessary but not sufficient. A clinician considers:

- **Cultural context**: How does this person's culture express distress? Somatic complaints (headaches, fatigue, body pain) may be the primary presentation in many cultures, particularly in the Middle East and Asia.
- **Developmental stage**: Depression in children often presents as irritability rather than sadness. Older adults may emphasize cognitive complaints over mood changes.
- **Severity specifiers**: The DSM-5 allows clinicians to specify mild, moderate, or severe episodes, as well as features like anxious distress, mixed features, melancholic features, atypical features, psychotic features, catatonia, peripartum onset, and seasonal pattern.
- **Differential diagnosis**: Ruling out grief, adjustment disorder, bipolar disorder, substance-induced mood disorder, and medical conditions is essential.

## Screening vs. Diagnosis: A Critical Distinction

Screening tools like the PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire-9) are designed to identify people who may benefit from further clinical evaluation. They are **not diagnostic instruments**. A PHQ-9 score of 10 or above suggests moderate depression and warrants a clinical conversation, but many people who score above the threshold will not meet full DSM-5 criteria upon clinical evaluation, and some who score below may still have clinically significant depression.

DepScreen uses the PHQ-9 as a screening tool. A screening result is a starting point for a conversation with a qualified professional, never a final answer.

## Limitations and Criticisms

The DSM-5 is the best available framework, but it has known limitations:

- It draws categorical boundaries around experiences that exist on a continuum.
- It was developed primarily within Western clinical contexts and may not fully capture how depression is experienced and expressed across all cultures.
- The bereavement exclusion was removed in DSM-5, generating ongoing debate about the boundary between grief and depression.
- Reliability depends on clinician training and the quality of the clinical interview.

These limitations do not invalidate the framework. They underscore why diagnosis requires a trained professional, not a checklist.

## What This Means for You

If you are reading this because you or someone you care about may be experiencing depression: the criteria above are meant to help professionals understand and treat your experience. They are not a test you pass or fail. Experiencing some symptoms does not automatically mean you have MDD, and not meeting full criteria does not mean your suffering is invalid.

Depression is one of the most treatable mental health conditions. Effective treatments exist and are accessible. If something feels wrong, that is reason enough to talk with a healthcare provider.

This information is for educational and awareness purposes only and is not a diagnosis.
