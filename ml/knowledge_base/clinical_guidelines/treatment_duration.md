# Antidepressant Treatment Duration and Discontinuation

## Three Phases of Antidepressant Treatment

### Acute Phase: 6-12 Weeks

**Goal**: Achieve remission (PHQ-9 < 5).

- Begin with adequate therapeutic dose; titrate as needed every 2-4 weeks
- Assess response at Week 4 and Week 8 using PHQ-9
- If remission not achieved by 8-12 weeks at optimized dose, modify treatment (switch, augment, or add psychotherapy)
- Most antidepressants require 2-4 weeks for initial effect; full effect may take 6-8 weeks
- Do not discontinue prematurely if partial response is occurring — continue optimizing

### Continuation Phase: 4-9 Months After Achieving Remission

**Goal**: Prevent relapse (return of symptoms from the same episode).

- Maintain the same medication and dose that achieved remission — do not reduce "because the patient feels better"
- Relapse risk without continuation treatment: ~50% within 6 months (Geddes et al., 2003)
- Relapse risk with continuation treatment: ~25% — a 50% relative risk reduction
- Continue PHQ-9 monitoring monthly
- Psychotherapy during this phase further reduces relapse risk
- Minimum duration: 4-6 months after full remission for first episode

### Maintenance Phase: ≥1 Year (Potentially Indefinite)

**Goal**: Prevent recurrence (a new episode of depression).

Maintenance treatment is recommended for patients with:
- **Two or more prior episodes**: Continue for at least 1-2 years
- **Three or more prior episodes**: Continue for at least 2-3 years; some guidelines recommend indefinite treatment
- **Severe episodes**: Psychotic features, suicidal behavior, severe functional impairment — longer maintenance
- **Chronic depression** (persistent depressive disorder, episodes lasting >2 years): Extended maintenance strongly recommended
- **Residual symptoms**: Incomplete remission with ongoing subsyndromal symptoms increases recurrence risk and warrants longer maintenance
- **Late onset** (after age 60): Higher recurrence risk; extended maintenance appropriate

## Discontinuation: When and How

### When to Consider Discontinuation

- First episode with full remission sustained for ≥6-9 months on continuation therapy
- No significant risk factors for recurrence (see maintenance criteria above)
- Patient preference after informed discussion of recurrence risk
- Shared decision-making is essential — the patient should understand the trade-offs

### How to Taper

**Never stop antidepressants abruptly.** Gradual tapering is essential.

- **Standard taper**: Reduce dose by 25% every 2-4 weeks
- **Slow taper** (for long-term use >1 year, or paroxetine/venlafaxine): Reduce by 10% every 2-4 weeks; final reductions may need to be smaller (e.g., liquid formulations for precise dosing)
- **Total taper duration**: 4 weeks minimum; 2-6 months for patients on long-term treatment or high-risk medications
- Horowitz & Taylor (2019) demonstrated that hyperbolic tapering (smaller reductions at the end) matches receptor occupancy changes and reduces withdrawal symptoms

### Medications with Higher Discontinuation Risk
| Medication | Risk Level | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-------|
| Paroxetine | High | Short half-life, potent; taper very slowly |
| Venlafaxine | High | Short half-life; even missed doses can cause symptoms |
| Duloxetine | Moderate-High | Capsule formulation limits dose reduction flexibility |
| Desvenlafaxine | Moderate | Fewer data but similar mechanism to venlafaxine |
| Sertraline | Moderate | Generally manageable with standard taper |
| Citalopram/Escitalopram | Low-Moderate | Usually straightforward taper |
| Fluoxetine | Low | Long half-life (self-tapers); often no formal taper needed |
| Bupropion | Low | Rarely causes discontinuation syndrome |

## Antidepressant Discontinuation Syndrome

### Symptoms (FINISH mnemonic)
- **F**lu-like symptoms (fatigue, myalgia, chills, sweating)
- **I**nsomnia (vivid dreams, nightmares, sleep disruption)
- **N**ausea (GI disturbance, diarrhea, abdominal cramps)
- **I**mbalance (dizziness, vertigo, lightheadedness, ataxia)
- **S**ensory disturbances ("brain zaps," electric shock sensations, paresthesia, tinnitus)
- **H**yperarousal (anxiety, irritability, agitation, crying spells)

### Key Differentiators from Relapse
| Feature | Discontinuation Syndrome | Relapse |
|---------|-------------------------|---------|
| Onset | Within days of dose reduction | Gradual, over weeks |
| Duration | Usually resolves in 1-3 weeks | Persistent, worsening |
| "Brain zaps" | Characteristic, common | Not present |
| Response to restarting medication | Rapid improvement (within 24-48 hours) | Takes 2-4 weeks |

### Management
- If symptoms are mild: reassurance, symptom management, continue taper
- If symptoms are moderate-severe: return to previous tolerated dose, resume slower taper
- If distinguishing from relapse: restart previous dose; if symptoms resolve within 48 hours, it was withdrawal; if they persist 2+ weeks, evaluate for relapse

## Communication with Patients

Key messages to convey at treatment initiation:
1. "Antidepressants are not addictive, but your body adjusts to them. Stopping suddenly can cause temporary withdrawal symptoms."
2. "Even when you feel better, continuing medication for several months protects against the depression coming back."
3. "We will decide together when and how to stop. It's a gradual process, not a sudden one."
4. "If you want to stop, please talk to your doctor first — we will make a safe plan."
