# Depression Treatment in Special Populations

## Adolescents (Ages 12-17)

### Key Considerations
- Depression presentation differs from adults: irritability may be more prominent than sadness; somatic complaints, academic decline, social withdrawal, and behavioral changes are common
- High comorbidity with anxiety disorders, ADHD, and substance experimentation

### Pharmacotherapy
- **Fluoxetine**: Only SSRI with FDA approval for adolescent depression (ages 8+); first-line medication choice
- **Escitalopram**: FDA-approved for ages 12+; second-line
- Other SSRIs (sertraline, citalopram) used off-label with moderate evidence
- **Start low**: fluoxetine 10 mg/day for 1 week, then 20 mg/day; maximum 60 mg/day

### Black Box Warning — Context and Evidence
- FDA black box warning (2004): antidepressants may increase suicidal thinking and behavior in patients under 25
- Absolute risk increase is small (~2% vs. ~4% for suicidal ideation, not completed suicides)
- No increase in completed suicides was observed in clinical trials
- Untreated depression itself carries far greater suicide risk than antidepressant treatment
- The warning led to a 30% decrease in antidepressant prescribing and a subsequent increase in adolescent suicide rates — the unintended consequence of under-treatment
- **Clinical implication**: Treat depression, but monitor closely

### Monitoring Protocol
- Weekly contact for first 4 weeks, biweekly for weeks 5-8, then monthly
- Screen for emerging suicidal ideation, agitation, akathisia, behavioral activation, and irritability at each visit
- TADS trial: combination fluoxetine + CBT was superior to either alone

### Psychotherapy
- CBT adapted for adolescents: first-line, alone or combined with fluoxetine
- Family involvement is essential: psychoeducation, communication training, reducing family conflict
- IPT for adolescents (IPT-A): strong evidence, particularly for interpersonal triggers

## Elderly (Ages 65+)

### Key Considerations
- Depression is NOT a normal part of aging — it is undertreated in this population
- Presentation may emphasize somatic symptoms, cognitive complaints ("pseudodementia"), apathy, and social withdrawal over explicit sadness
- High comorbidity with medical conditions, polypharmacy, and bereavement

### Pharmacotherapy: "Start Low, Go Slow — But Go"
- **Preferred**: Escitalopram (5-10 mg) or sertraline (25-100 mg) — fewest drug interactions, tolerable
- **Avoid**: Paroxetine (anticholinergic burden), TCAs (cardiac conduction, falls, cognitive impairment), fluoxetine (long half-life, CYP2D6 interactions with common geriatric medications)
- **Anticholinergic burden**: Screen using the Anticholinergic Cognitive Burden Scale; cumulative anticholinergic load increases confusion, constipation, urinary retention, falls, and cognitive decline
- **Fall risk**: SSRIs do carry some fall risk (hyponatremia, initial sedation); SNRIs and mirtazapine also; balance against untreated depression fall risk
- Start at half the usual adult dose; titrate slowly (every 2-4 weeks); therapeutic doses may still be needed for efficacy
- Monitor sodium (hyponatremia risk with SSRIs, especially with concurrent diuretics)

### Psychotherapy
- CBT and PST have strong evidence in older adults
- Adaptations: slower pace, repetition, written summaries, involvement of caregivers
- Address grief, role transitions (retirement, loss of independence), and social isolation

### Assessment
- Screen for cognitive impairment (MoCA) — depression can mimic or coexist with dementia
- PHQ-9 remains valid in elderly populations
- Geriatric Depression Scale (GDS) is an alternative validated specifically for older adults

## Perinatal Depression (Pregnancy and Postpartum)

### Key Considerations
- Affects 10-20% of pregnant and postpartum individuals
- Screening recommended: Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) or PHQ-9 at first prenatal visit, third trimester, and postpartum visits
- Untreated perinatal depression carries substantial risks: preterm birth, low birth weight, impaired bonding, developmental delays, partner and family impact, maternal suicide

### Pharmacotherapy
- **Sertraline**: Most safety data in pregnancy and breastfeeding; first-line choice
- **Escitalopram**: Reasonable alternative with growing safety data
- **Paroxetine**: Avoid in first trimester (small increased risk of cardiac malformations)
- **Fluoxetine**: Generally acceptable; long half-life may cause neonatal adaptation issues
- All SSRIs: Small risk of neonatal adaptation syndrome (jitteriness, respiratory distress, feeding difficulty — usually mild, self-limiting within days)
- **Risk-benefit**: For moderate-to-severe depression, untreated illness poses greater risk to mother and child than medication exposure

### Non-Pharmacological
- CBT and IPT have strong evidence for perinatal depression
- IPT specifically validated for postpartum depression
- Exercise programs, peer support, and partner involvement as adjuncts
- Brexanolone (IV neurosteroid) FDA-approved for postpartum depression; zuranolone (oral) approved 2023 — both are specialist-administered

## Treatment-Resistant Depression (TRD)

### Definition
- **Standard definition**: Failure to achieve adequate response (≥50% PHQ-9 reduction) after ≥2 adequate antidepressant trials of different classes, each at therapeutic dose for ≥4-6 weeks
- Affects approximately 30% of patients with MDD

### Evaluation Before Labeling TRD
1. Were prior trials truly adequate? (dose, duration, adherence)
2. Is the diagnosis correct? (screen for bipolar II, personality disorders, substance use, medical conditions)
3. Are comorbidities being addressed? (anxiety, pain, insomnia, substance use)
4. Are psychosocial perpetuating factors present? (ongoing abuse, unresolvable stressors)

### Treatment Options
- **Augmentation**: Lithium, aripiprazole, quetiapine (see CANMAT inadequate response algorithm)
- **Esketamine (Spravato)**: FDA-approved nasal spray for TRD as adjunct to oral antidepressant; requires REMS program, in-clinic administration
- **ECT**: Most effective treatment for severe TRD (~50-70% response); consider when multiple medication trials have failed or rapid response is needed
- **TMS**: FDA-cleared; non-invasive; 20-30 daily sessions; response rates ~50-60%
- **MAOIs**: Phenelzine, tranylcypromine — effective but require dietary restrictions and careful drug interaction management
- **Psychotherapy**: If not yet tried, adding CBT/IPT can convert partial responders to full responders
- **VNS (Vagus Nerve Stimulation)**: FDA-approved for chronic TRD; implanted device; long-term adjunct

### Specialist Referral Criteria
- 2+ failed adequate trials → refer to psychiatrist
- Psychotic features, active suicidal ideation, or severe functional impairment → urgent psychiatric evaluation
- Considering ECT, ketamine/esketamine, or MAOIs → specialist required
