# APA Treatment Guidelines for Major Depressive Disorder

## Overview

The American Psychiatric Association (APA) 2019 guideline provides evidence-based recommendations for treating MDD across three age cohorts: children/adolescents, adults, and older adults. It emphasizes individualized treatment, measurement-based care, and shared decision-making.

## Core Principles

1. **Comprehensive assessment**: Diagnosis confirmed via clinical interview; rule out bipolar disorder, medical causes, substance-related mood changes
2. **Measurement-based care**: Use validated tools (PHQ-9) at baseline and regular intervals to guide treatment decisions objectively
3. **Individualized treatment**: Consider symptom severity, patient preference, prior treatment history, comorbidities, and psychosocial context
4. **Shared decision-making**: Inform patients about treatment options, expected benefits, side effects, and timelines; collaborate on the treatment plan
5. **Safety monitoring**: Assess suicide risk at every visit; heightened monitoring during initial treatment and dose changes

## Psychotherapy Recommendations

### Strong Evidence (Grade A)
- **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)**: Effective for mild, moderate, and severe depression; can be used alone for mild-moderate or combined with medication for moderate-severe
- **Interpersonal Therapy (IPT)**: Targets interpersonal conflicts, role transitions, grief, and social isolation; equivalent efficacy to CBT in head-to-head trials

### Moderate Evidence (Grade B)
- **Behavioral Activation (BA)**: Focuses on increasing engagement in valued activities; effective as standalone for mild-moderate depression
- **Problem-Solving Therapy (PST)**: Structured approach to improving coping; particularly useful in primary care settings
- **Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT)**: Strong evidence specifically for relapse prevention in recurrent depression

## Pharmacotherapy Recommendations

### First-Line Agents
- **SSRIs**: Fluoxetine, sertraline, citalopram, escitalopram, paroxetine — generally well-tolerated, broad evidence base
- **SNRIs**: Venlafaxine, duloxetine, desvenlafaxine — dual mechanism; may have advantages for pain comorbidity
- **Others**: Bupropion (norepinephrine-dopamine; favorable weight/sexual side-effect profile), mirtazapine (useful for insomnia, poor appetite)

### Selection Factors
- Prior response history (personal and family)
- Side-effect profile matched to patient concerns (sexual dysfunction, weight, sedation)
- Drug-drug interactions and medical comorbidities
- Cost and access
- Patient preference

## Treatment by Age Cohort

### Children and Adolescents
- Fluoxetine is the first-line pharmacotherapy (strongest pediatric evidence)
- CBT is the first-line psychotherapy
- Combined treatment (fluoxetine + CBT) superior to either alone
- FDA black box warning: monitor closely for suicidal ideation, especially in first 4 weeks

### Adults
- CBT, IPT, or antidepressant medication as first-line options
- Moderate-severe: combined treatment (psychotherapy + medication) preferred
- Reassess at 4-8 weeks; switch or augment if inadequate response

### Older Adults
- Start pharmacotherapy at lower doses; titrate slowly
- Avoid medications with high anticholinergic burden (paroxetine, TCAs)
- Screen for cognitive impairment — depression and dementia overlap significantly
- Address polypharmacy and fall risk
- Psychotherapy equally effective; consider access barriers (mobility, transportation)

## Measurement-Based Care Protocol

- Administer PHQ-9 at every visit during active treatment
- Define treatment targets: response (≥50% reduction) by 8 weeks, remission (PHQ-9 < 5) by 12 weeks
- If targets not met, systematically adjust treatment (see APA Treatment Algorithm)
- Continue monitoring through continuation and maintenance phases

## When to Refer

- Diagnostic uncertainty (bipolar features, personality disorder comorbidity)
- Treatment resistance (failure of 2+ adequate trials)
- Active suicidal ideation with plan or intent
- Psychotic features
- Severe functional impairment requiring intensive outpatient or inpatient care
- Comorbid substance use disorder requiring integrated treatment

**Sources**: APA Practice Guideline (2010), APA Clinical Practice Guideline Across Three Age Cohorts (2019), Gelenberg et al. (2010).
