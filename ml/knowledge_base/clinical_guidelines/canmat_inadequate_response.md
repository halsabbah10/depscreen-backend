# CANMAT Algorithm for Inadequate Response to Antidepressants

## Before Declaring Non-Response: Systematic Verification

### Step 0: Confirm the Basics
1. **Verify adequate dose**: Is the patient at a therapeutic dose, not a subtherapeutic starting dose?
2. **Verify adequate duration**: Has the patient been on a therapeutic dose for at least 4-8 weeks? Partial responders may need up to 8 weeks before concluding insufficient response.
3. **Verify adherence**: Up to 50% of patients are non-adherent within 6 months. Ask non-judgmentally. Consider pharmacy refill records, pill counts.
4. **Reassess diagnosis**: Could it be bipolar II (mixed features, hypomania history), persistent depressive disorder, adjustment disorder, or a medical condition (thyroid, anemia, substance use)?
5. **Address comorbidities**: Untreated anxiety, substance use, chronic pain, or insomnia can perpetuate depression despite adequate antidepressant treatment.
6. **Psychosocial factors**: Ongoing stressors (relationship conflict, financial stress, bereavement) may limit pharmacological response.

## Step 1: Dose Optimization

- Titrate current medication to the maximum tolerated dose within the recommended range
- Example: sertraline 50 mg → 100 mg → 150 mg → 200 mg at 2-week intervals if tolerated
- Evidence: A substantial proportion of partial responders improve with dose optimization alone
- Timeline: Re-evaluate 2-4 weeks after each dose increase

## Step 2: Switch Within Class

- If the current agent is an SSRI, switch to a different SSRI
- Rationale: Individual pharmacokinetic and pharmacogenomic variation means one SSRI may work where another did not
- Example: sertraline → escitalopram, or citalopram → fluoxetine
- STAR*D data: ~25% of SSRI non-responders achieve remission with a second SSRI
- Cross-taper or direct-switch depending on half-lives; no washout needed within SSRIs (except fluoxetine → other SSRI, allow 1-2 weeks due to long half-life)

## Step 3: Switch Across Class

- Switch to a medication with a different mechanism of action
- **SSRI → SNRI**: Venlafaxine XR or duloxetine; adds norepinephrine activity
- **SSRI → Bupropion**: Norepinephrine-dopamine mechanism; useful if fatigue, hypersomnia, sexual dysfunction, or weight gain are problems
- **SSRI → Mirtazapine**: NaSSA mechanism; useful if insomnia and appetite loss are prominent
- **SSRI → Vortioxetine**: Multimodal; cognitive symptoms may improve
- Allow adequate washout when switching to/from MAOIs (2 weeks minimum; 5 weeks for fluoxetine)

## Step 4: Augmentation Strategies

Add a second agent to the current antidepressant rather than switching.

### First-Line Augmentation (Level 1-2 Evidence)
- **Aripiprazole** (2-10 mg/day): Best-studied augmentation agent; monitor for akathisia, weight gain, metabolic effects
- **Quetiapine XR** (150-300 mg/day): Effective augmentation; sedation, weight gain, metabolic monitoring required
- **Lithium** (600-900 mg/day, target level 0.6-1.0 mEq/L): Strong evidence; requires renal, thyroid monitoring, and serum level checks; narrow therapeutic index

### Second-Line Augmentation
- **Thyroid hormone (T3, liothyronine)** (25-50 mcg/day): Evidence primarily with TCAs, some with SSRIs; no thyroid monitoring needed at augmentation doses in euthyroid patients
- **Bupropion** (added to SSRI/SNRI): Complementary mechanism; avoids weight gain and sexual side effects
- **Mirtazapine** (added to SSRI/SNRI): "California rocket fuel" combination (venlafaxine + mirtazapine); complementary receptor profile

### Third-Line Augmentation
- **Modafinil/armodafinil**: For residual fatigue and cognitive symptoms
- **Methylphenidate/dextroamphetamine**: Limited evidence; consider in apathetic depression with comorbid ADHD features

## Step 5: Combination Antidepressant Therapy

- Combine two antidepressants with different mechanisms
- **SSRI + bupropion**: Most common combination; well-tolerated
- **SSRI/SNRI + mirtazapine**: Complementary receptor profiles; mirtazapine may offset SSRI sexual side effects and insomnia
- Avoid combining two serotonergic agents at high doses (serotonin syndrome risk)
- Avoid SSRI/SNRI + MAOI (absolute contraindication)

## Step 6: Advanced Options

When Steps 1-5 have been exhausted:
- **MAOIs**: Phenelzine or tranylcypromine under specialist supervision; dietary restrictions and drug interaction management required
- **ECT**: Most effective treatment for severe refractory depression (~50-70% response rate); consider when 2+ adequate medication trials have failed
- **Ketamine/esketamine**: Rapid-acting for treatment-resistant depression; esketamine (Spravato) nasal spray FDA-approved as adjunct; requires monitored administration
- **TMS (Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation)**: FDA-cleared for treatment-resistant depression; non-invasive; typically 20-30 sessions

## Decision Framework

| Scenario | Preferred Strategy |
|----------|-------------------|
| Partial response, tolerable side effects | Dose optimization → augmentation |
| No response, tolerable side effects | Switch within class → switch across class |
| Intolerable side effects | Switch (within or across class) |
| Two failed adequate trials | Augment or combine; consider TRD workup |
| Three+ failed trials | Specialist referral; ECT, ketamine, or MAOI evaluation |

## Key Principles

- **One change at a time**: Assess effect of each modification before layering another
- **Document each trial**: Dose, duration, response percentage, side effects, reason for change
- **Patient engagement**: Explain rationale; treatment resistance is not the patient's fault
- **Measurement-based**: PHQ-9 at every decision point; response = ≥50% reduction, remission = <5

**Sources**: CANMAT 2016 (Kennedy et al.), STAR*D (Rush et al., 2006).
