# APA Depression Treatment Decision Algorithm

## Step 1: Comprehensive Assessment

Before selecting treatment:
1. Confirm MDD diagnosis via structured clinical interview (DSM-5 criteria)
2. Rule out bipolar disorder (screen with MDQ or BSDS — antidepressant monotherapy contraindicated in bipolar)
3. Rule out medical causes (thyroid dysfunction, anemia, vitamin D/B12 deficiency, substance use)
4. Establish PHQ-9 baseline score
5. Assess suicide risk (Item 9, Columbia-Suicide Severity Rating Scale if indicated)
6. Identify comorbidities (anxiety, substance use, chronic pain, personality disorders)
7. Review prior treatment history and family response history

## Step 2: Severity-Based Treatment Selection

### Minimal Depression (PHQ-9 0-4)
- No formal treatment indicated
- Psychoeducation about depression signs and when to seek help
- Schedule follow-up PHQ-9 in 4-8 weeks if clinical concern exists

### Mild Depression (PHQ-9 5-9)
- **Watchful waiting**: Rescreen in 2-4 weeks; many mild episodes self-resolve
- **Self-help interventions**: Structured exercise programs, behavioral activation self-help materials, sleep hygiene
- **Guided self-help**: Clinician-supported digital CBT (e.g., computerized CBT programs)
- **Brief psychotherapy**: If symptoms persist after 2-4 weeks of watchful waiting
- Formal pharmacotherapy generally not first-line for mild depression

### Moderate Depression (PHQ-9 10-14)
- **Psychotherapy**: CBT or IPT as monotherapy is appropriate; patient preference guides choice
- **Pharmacotherapy**: SSRI or SNRI as monotherapy is appropriate if psychotherapy is unavailable, declined, or previously ineffective
- **Combined treatment**: May be offered based on patient preference and clinical judgment
- Reassess with PHQ-9 at 4 weeks

### Moderately Severe Depression (PHQ-9 15-19)
- **Combined treatment preferred**: Psychotherapy (CBT/IPT) plus pharmacotherapy yields superior outcomes to either alone
- If only one modality available, either CBT/IPT or medication is acceptable
- More frequent monitoring: PHQ-9 every 2 weeks during acute phase
- Assess functional impairment and consider accommodations (work, school)

### Severe Depression (PHQ-9 20-27)
- **Combined treatment strongly recommended**: Medication plus psychotherapy
- **Pharmacotherapy initiation**: Start SSRI/SNRI at therapeutic dose; do not under-dose
- **Safety assessment**: Evaluate for hospitalization criteria (active suicidal ideation with plan/intent, inability to care for self, psychotic features)
- **Frequent monitoring**: Weekly PHQ-9 and safety checks during first 4 weeks
- **Consider**: ECT for treatment-resistant severe depression or when rapid response is critical

## Step 3: Assess Treatment Response (4-8 Weeks)

Evaluate PHQ-9 change from baseline:

| Response Category | PHQ-9 Change | Action |
|-------------------|-------------|--------|
| Remission | Score < 5 | Continue current treatment; transition to continuation phase |
| Response | ≥50% reduction | Continue current treatment; optimize if not yet in remission |
| Partial response | 25-49% reduction | Optimize current treatment (see Step 4) |
| Non-response | <25% reduction | Change treatment strategy (see Step 4) |

## Step 4: Inadequate Response Algorithm

### 4A — Optimize Current Treatment
- **Medication**: Increase dose to maximum tolerated within recommended range; ensure adequate trial duration (minimum 4-6 weeks at therapeutic dose)
- **Psychotherapy**: Confirm adequate dose (weekly sessions); ensure evidence-based protocol is being followed with fidelity
- **Adherence**: Assess medication adherence, session attendance, homework completion
- **Reassess diagnosis**: Consider undiagnosed comorbidities, bipolar spectrum, personality disorders, substance use

### 4B — Switch Strategy (After Optimization Fails)
- **Switch within class**: Different SSRI (e.g., sertraline → escitalopram) — ~25% of SSRI non-responders respond to a different SSRI
- **Switch across class**: SSRI → SNRI, or SSRI → bupropion, or SSRI → mirtazapine — different mechanism may succeed
- **Switch psychotherapy modality**: CBT → IPT or behavioral activation if CBT-specific factors (homework, cognitive restructuring) are barriers

### 4C — Augmentation Strategy
- **Lithium augmentation**: Strong evidence; requires monitoring (renal, thyroid, levels)
- **Atypical antipsychotic augmentation**: Aripiprazole, quetiapine, or brexpiprazole added to SSRI/SNRI; monitor metabolic effects
- **Thyroid hormone (T3)**: Evidence for augmentation, especially with TCAs
- **Add psychotherapy**: If on medication alone, adding CBT/IPT improves outcomes

### 4D — Combination Strategies
- **Dual antidepressant therapy**: SSRI + bupropion, or SSRI + mirtazapine (complementary mechanisms)
- **Combined psychotherapy approaches**: CBT + behavioral activation components
- Reserve for patients who have failed Steps 4A-4C

## STAR*D Benchmark Data

The STAR*D trial provides real-world context for setting expectations:
- **Step 1** (initial SSRI): ~37% remission rate
- **Step 2** (switch or augment): ~31% remission rate
- **Step 3** (second switch/augment): ~14% remission rate
- **Step 4** (third switch/augment): ~13% remission rate
- **Cumulative remission** after all steps: ~67%

These data underscore that persistence through multiple treatment steps is important, and that treatment resistance should be addressed systematically rather than with resignation.
