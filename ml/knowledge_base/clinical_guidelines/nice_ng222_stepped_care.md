# NICE NG222 Stepped Care Model for Depression

## Overview

The NICE stepped care framework matches treatment intensity to clinical severity and response. Patients enter at the step appropriate to their presentation and step up only when a less intensive intervention has been tried and found insufficient.

## Step 1: Recognition, Assessment, and Initial Management

**Who**: All individuals presenting with depressive symptoms in any healthcare setting.

- Screen using validated tools (PHQ-9, HADS)
- Confirm diagnosis against ICD-11 / DSM-5 criteria
- Assess risk factors: suicide/self-harm (PHQ-9 Item 9), psychosocial stressors, comorbidities
- Provide psychoeducation: explain depression as a medical condition, expected course, treatment options
- Active monitoring: schedule follow-up within 2 weeks; many subthreshold episodes resolve
- Promote lifestyle factors: regular physical activity, sleep hygiene, reduced alcohol intake
- Discuss patient preferences — shared decision-making is central to NICE guidance

## Step 2: Low-Intensity Interventions

**Who**: Persistent subthreshold or mild-to-moderate depression not responding to Step 1.

### Guided Self-Help
- Based on CBT principles; delivered via structured written or digital materials
- Supported by a trained practitioner over 6-8 sessions (phone, video, or in-person)
- Covers behavioral activation, problem-solving, cognitive restructuring basics

### Computerized CBT (cCBT)
- Approved programs (e.g., Beating the Blues, SilverCloud) with practitioner check-ins
- Typically 8 modules completed over 9-12 weeks
- Evidence of comparable efficacy to face-to-face therapy for mild-to-moderate cases

### Group-Based Exercise Programs
- Structured, supervised exercise 3 times per week for 10-14 weeks
- Moderate-intensity aerobic activity (45-60 minutes per session)
- Evidence supports clinically meaningful reductions in PHQ-9 scores

### Group-Based Peer Support and Psychoeducation
- Facilitated groups providing information, coping skills, and mutual support
- Not a substitute for structured therapy but can reduce isolation

## Step 3: High-Intensity Interventions

**Who**: Moderate-to-severe depression, or mild-to-moderate not responding to Step 2.

### Pharmacotherapy
- First-line: SSRIs (sertraline, citalopram, escitalopram, fluoxetine)
- Discuss expected onset (2-4 weeks), side-effect profile, and minimum trial duration (4-6 weeks)
- Review at 2 weeks for tolerability, then every 2-4 weeks during acute phase
- If inadequate response at 4-6 weeks on adequate dose: optimize, switch, or augment

### High-Intensity Psychological Therapies
- **Individual CBT**: 16-20 sessions over 3-4 months; strongest evidence base
- **Interpersonal Therapy (IPT)**: 16-20 sessions; focuses on grief, role transitions, interpersonal disputes
- **Behavioral Activation (BA)**: 16-20 sessions; as effective as CBT for moderate depression
- **Couples therapy**: When relationship quality is a significant maintaining factor

### Combined Treatment
- For moderate-to-severe depression, combined pharmacotherapy plus high-intensity therapy produces better outcomes than either alone
- NICE recommends offering combined treatment when severity warrants it

## Step 4: Specialist and Crisis Interventions

**Who**: Severe, complex, or treatment-resistant depression; risk to life; psychotic features.

- **Crisis resolution and home treatment teams**: Intensive support as alternative to inpatient admission
- **Inpatient care**: When safety cannot be managed in the community (active suicidal intent, severe self-neglect, psychotic symptoms)
- **Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT)**: Rapid response needed, life-threatening depression, catatonic features, or multiple failed medication trials
- **Complex pharmacotherapy**: Augmentation strategies (lithium, atypical antipsychotics), combination antidepressants, MAOIs — managed by specialist psychiatrists
- **Specialist psychological therapies**: Schema therapy, CBASP (Cognitive Behavioral Analysis System of Psychotherapy) for chronic depression

## Stepping Up Criteria

Move to the next step when:
1. The current intervention has been delivered at adequate dose and duration
2. PHQ-9 shows <25% reduction at expected review point (non-response) or <50% (partial response without functional improvement)
3. Patient preference — some patients may reasonably prefer to step up earlier
4. Risk escalation — new suicidal ideation, functional deterioration, or psychotic features warrant immediate escalation

## Stepping Down Criteria

Step down when:
1. Sustained remission (PHQ-9 < 5 for ≥2 months at current step)
2. Patient preference for less intensive maintenance
3. Continuation/maintenance phase entered — reduce frequency of contact while maintaining treatment

## Key Principles

- **Least restrictive effective intervention first** — avoids over-treatment and respects patient autonomy
- **No step is skipped without justification** — but severity/risk can warrant starting at Step 3 or 4
- **Measurement-based care** — PHQ-9 at every contact drives stepping decisions
- **Patient preference is a valid clinical factor** — not every patient wants medication, not every patient wants therapy
