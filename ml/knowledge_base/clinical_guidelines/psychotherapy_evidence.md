# Evidence-Based Psychotherapies for Depression

## Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

**Evidence level**: Strongest evidence base of any psychotherapy for depression. Recommended as first-line by APA, NICE, CANMAT, and WHO.

### How It Works
- Identifies and challenges negative automatic thoughts, cognitive distortions (all-or-nothing thinking, catastrophizing, mental filtering), and maladaptive core beliefs
- Uses behavioral experiments to test distorted beliefs against reality
- Incorporates behavioral activation, activity scheduling, and graded task assignment
- Teaches skills that persist beyond treatment (relapse prevention benefit)

### Format and Dose
- Typically 12-20 individual sessions, 50-60 minutes each, over 3-5 months
- Structured, manual-guided, with between-session homework assignments
- Effective in individual, group, and digital/online formats

### Best Suited For
- Mild to severe depression (all severity levels)
- Patients with prominent negative thinking patterns, rumination, or self-criticism
- Patients who prefer active, skill-building approaches
- Relapse prevention after medication discontinuation (sustained effects beyond treatment end)

### Key Evidence
- Equivalent to antidepressants for moderate depression
- Combined CBT + medication superior to either alone for severe depression
- Lower relapse rates than medication alone after treatment ends (enduring effect)

## Interpersonal Therapy (IPT)

**Evidence level**: Strong (Level 1). Recommended as first-line alongside CBT.

### How It Works
- Focuses on current interpersonal problems maintaining depression, not childhood history
- Addresses four domains: grief/loss, role transitions, interpersonal role disputes, and interpersonal deficits (social isolation)
- Improves communication patterns, social support, and interpersonal functioning

### Format and Dose
- 12-16 individual sessions over 3-4 months
- Three phases: assessment and formulation (sessions 1-3), middle work (sessions 4-12), termination and relapse prevention (sessions 13-16)

### Best Suited For
- Depression triggered by relationship conflict, bereavement, major life transitions, or social isolation
- Patients who prefer a relational focus over cognitive restructuring
- Perinatal depression (strong evidence in this population)

## Behavioral Activation (BA)

**Evidence level**: Strong (Level 1). As effective as CBT and medication for moderate-to-severe depression.

### How It Works
- Addresses withdrawal and avoidance that maintain depression
- Systematically schedules meaningful, rewarding, or mastery-building activities
- Monitors mood-activity links; increases approach behavior, decreases avoidance
- Simpler to deliver than full CBT — can be delivered by trained non-specialists

### Format and Dose
- 12-16 sessions over 3-4 months
- Highly structured; uses activity logs, scheduling, and graduated exposure to avoided activities

### Best Suited For
- Patients with prominent withdrawal, inactivity, or avoidance patterns
- Patients who find cognitive restructuring abstract or unhelpful
- Resource-limited settings (simpler training requirements)
- Can be delivered as a standalone treatment or as a component of CBT

### Key Evidence
- Dimidjian et al. (2006): BA was as effective as antidepressant medication for severe depression, and both outperformed cognitive therapy in the severe subgroup

## Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT)

**Evidence level**: Strong for relapse prevention (Level 1). Not first-line for acute treatment.

### How It Works
- 8-week group program combining mindfulness meditation with cognitive therapy elements
- Teaches awareness of negative thought patterns without engaging or reacting to them
- Develops "decentering" — observing thoughts as mental events, not facts
- Targets ruminative thinking, a key vulnerability factor for recurrent depression

### Format and Dose
- 8 weekly group sessions (2-2.5 hours each) plus a full-day silent retreat
- Daily home practice (30-45 minutes) with guided audio
- Ongoing personal mindfulness practice after program completion

### Best Suited For
- Relapse prevention in patients with ≥3 prior depressive episodes (reduces recurrence by ~40%)
- Patients in remission who want to reduce long-term antidepressant dependence
- Residual depressive symptoms after acute treatment
- NOT recommended as sole treatment for acute moderate-to-severe depression

## Problem-Solving Therapy (PST)

**Evidence level**: Moderate (Level 2). Effective for mild-to-moderate depression, particularly in primary care settings.

### How It Works
- Teaches structured problem-solving skills: problem identification, goal setting, brainstorming solutions, evaluating pros/cons, implementing and reviewing
- Addresses the helplessness and overwhelm that maintain depressive states
- Brief and practical — well-suited to primary care contexts

### Format and Dose
- 6-12 sessions, 30-60 minutes each
- Can be delivered by trained primary care providers, not only psychotherapists

### Best Suited For
- Depression linked to identifiable practical problems (financial, occupational, relationship)
- Patients who prefer concrete, action-oriented approaches
- Primary care or integrated behavioral health settings
- Older adults (good evidence in this population)

## Choosing Between Therapies

| Factor | Recommended Therapy |
|--------|-------------------|
| Prominent negative thinking | CBT |
| Relationship problems or life transitions | IPT |
| Withdrawal, inactivity, avoidance | BA |
| Recurrent depression (3+ episodes), relapse prevention | MBCT |
| Overwhelm from practical problems | PST |
| Severe depression, acute phase | CBT or BA + medication |
| Patient preference for skill-building | CBT or BA |
| Patient preference for relational work | IPT |

## How to Find a Therapist

1. Ask for a referral from your primary care provider or psychiatrist
2. Verify the therapist is trained in the specific evidence-based modality (not generic "talk therapy")
3. Resources: Psychology Today therapist finder (filter by specialty and modality), ABCT therapist directory, IAPT services (UK), local university training clinics (often lower cost)
4. Telehealth/online therapy platforms (BetterHelp, Talkspace) offer structured CBT — verify the therapist's credentials and that they follow a manual-guided protocol
5. Ask the therapist: "Do you follow a structured CBT/IPT/BA protocol?" and "Do you use session-by-session outcome measures?"
