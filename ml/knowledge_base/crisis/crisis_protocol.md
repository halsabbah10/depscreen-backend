## Scope

This protocol outlines recommended clinical steps when a patient presents with active suicidal ideation, self-harm behavior, or an immediate safety concern. A crisis is any situation in which a person's behavior or mental state puts them at risk of harming themselves or others, or prevents them from caring for themselves.

This protocol is intended to guide clinical decision-making, not replace it. Every crisis is unique, and the clinician's judgment remains paramount.

## Immediate Assessment

### Step 1: Ensure Physical Safety

If there is immediate danger to life:
- **In Bahrain**: Call **999** for emergency services.
- Remain with the patient. Do not leave them alone.
- If in a clinical setting, follow institutional emergency protocols.
- Remove or secure any accessible means (sharp objects, medications, cords).

### Step 2: Establish Rapport and De-Escalate

- Speak calmly and with empathy. Use the patient's name.
- Normalize the conversation: "I am glad you told me. This is exactly what I am here for."
- Do not express shock, disapproval, or panic.
- Avoid minimizing ("It is not that bad") or premature reassurance ("Everything will be fine").
- Ask open-ended questions to understand the patient's current experience.

### Step 3: Conduct Structured Risk Assessment (C-SSRS)

The Columbia-Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS) provides a standardized framework for assessing suicide risk severity and determining the appropriate level of intervention.

**C-SSRS Ideation Severity Levels:**

| Level | Description | Clinical Implication |
|-------|-------------|---------------------|
| 1 | Wish to be dead | Monitor; strengthen protective factors |
| 2 | Non-specific active suicidal thoughts | Increased contact; safety planning |
| 3 | Active ideation with method (no intent) | Safety planning; means counseling; consider referral |
| 4 | Active ideation with some intent to act | Urgent intervention; safety planning; possible hospitalization |
| 5 | Active ideation with specific plan and intent | Immediate intervention; hospitalization likely indicated |

**C-SSRS Behavior Assessment:**
- Actual attempt (interrupted, aborted, or completed).
- Preparatory acts or behaviors (acquiring means, writing a note, giving away possessions).
- Non-suicidal self-injury (distinguish from suicidal behavior; different risk profile and treatment approach).

### Step 4: Assess Risk and Protective Factors

**Risk factors to document:**
- Previous suicide attempts (strongest predictor).
- Current psychiatric diagnosis (depression, bipolar, PTSD, substance use, psychosis).
- Access to lethal means (especially firearms and medications).
- Recent loss, crisis, or humiliation.
- Social isolation, lack of belonging.
- Chronic pain, terminal illness.
- Family history of suicide.
- Recent discharge from psychiatric hospitalization (high-risk period: first 30 days).
- History of childhood trauma or abuse.
- Intoxication or active substance use.

**Protective factors to identify and strengthen:**
- Reasons for living (family, children, faith, pets, goals).
- Social connectedness and support network.
- Ongoing therapeutic relationship.
- Willingness to seek help.
- Problem-solving capacity.
- Cultural or religious prohibitions against suicide.
- Absence of access to lethal means.

## Clinical Decision Points

### Low Risk (C-SSRS Level 1-2, Strong Protective Factors)

- Create or review a safety plan (see safety_planning_guide.md).
- Schedule follow-up within one week.
- Provide crisis contact information.
- Document assessment, risk factors, protective factors, and rationale.

### Moderate Risk (C-SSRS Level 3, or Level 2 with Weak Protective Factors)

- Create or update a detailed safety plan (Step 6: means restriction is critical).
- Conduct means counseling with patient and, if possible, a support person.
- Increase contact frequency (twice weekly or more).
- Consider psychiatric referral for medication evaluation.
- Coordinate with the patient's support network (with consent).
- Document clinical rationale for outpatient management.
- Schedule follow-up within 48 to 72 hours.

### High Risk (C-SSRS Level 4-5, Recent Attempt, Active Psychosis)

- Do not allow the patient to leave alone.
- Contact psychiatric emergency services: **Salmaniya Medical Complex +973 1728 8888**.
- If the patient is in imminent danger and refuses help: consider involuntary evaluation under applicable Bahraini mental health law.
- If in a community setting, call **999** and remain with the patient until help arrives.
- Coordinate hospital transfer and communicate risk assessment findings to the receiving team.
- Contact next of kin or emergency contact (if legally permissible and clinically appropriate).

## Means Counseling

Means counseling is the process of working with patients and their support systems to reduce access to lethal means. It is one of the most effective suicide prevention interventions.

- **Firearms**: Store outside the home, give to a trusted person, use a gun safe with the combination held by someone else.
- **Medications**: Dispense limited quantities, give excess to a trusted person, use a locked medication box.
- **Other means**: Assess and address situation-specific means.

Means counseling should be collaborative, not coercive. Frame it as a temporary measure during a high-risk period.

## Documentation Requirements

Every crisis encounter should document:

1. The presenting concern and the patient's own words.
2. C-SSRS screening results (ideation severity, behavior history).
3. Identified risk factors and protective factors.
4. Clinical assessment of overall risk level (low, moderate, high, imminent).
5. Interventions provided (safety plan, means counseling, referral, hospitalization).
6. Clinical rationale for the chosen level of care.
7. Follow-up plan with specific dates and contact methods.
8. Who was notified (emergency contacts, other providers).
9. Patient's response to the intervention and stated plan for safety.

## Post-Crisis Follow-Up

The period immediately following a crisis is high-risk. Follow-up is not optional.

- Contact the patient within 24 to 48 hours after a crisis encounter.
- After psychiatric hospitalization, schedule outpatient follow-up within 72 hours of discharge (the first week post-discharge is the highest-risk period for reattempt).
- Review and update the safety plan at the next appointment.
- Monitor medication adherence and side effects.
- Re-assess risk factors and protective factors at each contact.
- Coordinate with any other involved providers (psychiatrist, primary care, family therapist).
