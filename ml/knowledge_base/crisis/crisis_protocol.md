## Crisis Protocol

This document outlines the recommended steps when someone is experiencing a mental health crisis, including active suicidal ideation, self-harm, or an immediate safety concern. A crisis is any situation in which a person's behavior puts them at risk of hurting themselves or others, or prevents them from being able to care for themselves.

### Immediate Steps

**If there is immediate danger to life, call 911 or your local emergency number.**

**If the person is in distress but not in immediate physical danger:**

1. **Stay calm and present.** Your steady presence matters. Speak in a calm, non-judgmental tone.
2. **Ask directly.** It is safe to ask, "Are you thinking about suicide?" Research shows that asking does not increase risk; it opens the door to help.
3. **Listen without judgment.** Let the person express their feelings. Avoid minimizing ("It's not that bad") or offering quick fixes ("Just think positive").
4. **Reduce access to means.** If possible, help remove or secure firearms, medications, sharp objects, or other lethal means.
5. **Connect to professional support.** Help the person contact a crisis service or accompany them to an emergency department.

### Crisis Resources

- **988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline:** Call or text **988** (US, available 24/7)
- **Crisis Text Line:** Text **HOME** to **741741**
- **Veterans Crisis Line:** Call **988**, then press **1**
- **International Association for Suicide Prevention:** https://www.iasp.info/resources/Crisis_Centres/
- **Emergency services:** **911** (US) or local emergency number

### After the Crisis

- Ensure the person has a follow-up appointment with a mental health professional
- Help them create or review a safety plan (see safety_planning.md)
- Check in regularly in the days and weeks following the crisis
- Take care of your own wellbeing as a supporter

**Every crisis is an opportunity for connection. Your willingness to be present can make a life-saving difference.**

Source: NIMH; 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline; SAMHSA
