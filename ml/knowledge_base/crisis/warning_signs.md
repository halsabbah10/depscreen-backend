## Overview

Recognizing warning signs early can be life-saving. The following signs may indicate that someone is in or approaching a mental health crisis, particularly related to suicidal thinking. Not everyone will show the same signs, and some people conceal their distress effectively. Trust your instincts: if something feels wrong, it is better to ask and be wrong than to stay silent.

**If there is immediate danger to life, call 999 (Bahrain) or your local emergency number.**

## Verbal Warning Signs

- Talking about wanting to die or wanting to kill themselves.
- Saying things like "I wish I had never been born" or "Everyone would be better off without me."
- Expressing feelings of being trapped, in unbearable pain, or having no reason to live.
- Talking about being a burden to others.
- Saying goodbye or expressing finality: "I will not be around much longer," "I just want it to stop."
- Asking questions about lethal means, methods, or doses.
- Talking about feeling empty, hopeless, or having no future.

## Behavioral Warning Signs

- Searching online for methods of suicide or acquiring means (firearms, stockpiling medications).
- Giving away prized possessions or putting affairs in order without a clear practical reason.
- Withdrawing from friends, family, and social activities, especially when this is a change from baseline.
- Increasing use of alcohol or drugs.
- Significant changes in sleep patterns (sleeping much more or much less).
- Acting recklessly or engaging in self-destructive behaviors (reckless driving, unprotected sex, excessive spending).
- Visiting or calling people to say goodbye.
- A sudden, unexplained improvement in mood after a period of deep depression (this may signal that a decision to act has been made, providing a sense of relief).
- Neglecting personal hygiene, nutrition, or responsibilities.
- Dropping out of treatment or missing appointments.

## Emotional Warning Signs

- Extreme mood swings, particularly rapid shifts from despair to calm.
- Expressing hopelessness about the future.
- Intense anxiety, agitation, or restlessness.
- Feeling humiliated, shamed, or publicly disgraced.
- Loss of interest in everything, including things previously cared about deeply.
- Rage, desire for revenge, or a sense of being wronged.
- Feeling disconnected, numb, or "already gone."

## Risk Factors

Risk factors do not cause suicide, but they increase vulnerability, especially when multiple factors converge:

- **Previous suicide attempt**: The single strongest predictor of future attempts.
- **Mental health conditions**: Depression, bipolar disorder, PTSD, substance use disorders, personality disorders.
- **Access to lethal means**: Firearms, large quantities of medication, other means.
- **Recent loss or crisis**: Death, relationship breakdown, job loss, legal problems, public humiliation.
- **Chronic pain or terminal illness**.
- **History of trauma or abuse**.
- **Social isolation or lack of belonging**.
- **Family history of suicide**.
- **Exposure to suicide**: Knowing someone who died by suicide, media coverage, cluster effects.

## Protective Factors

Protective factors reduce risk and should be actively identified and strengthened:

- Strong social connections and a sense of belonging.
- Reasons for living (family, faith, goals, responsibilities).
- Access to mental health care and ongoing therapeutic relationship.
- Problem-solving and coping skills.
- Restricted access to lethal means.
- Cultural, religious, or moral beliefs that discourage suicide.
- Sense of purpose and future orientation.
- Pets or dependents who rely on the person.

## C-SSRS Integration: A Framework for Assessment

The Columbia-Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS), developed by Posner et al. (2011), provides a standardized framework for assessing suicide risk. It distinguishes between:

1. **Wish to be dead**: Passive thoughts ("I wish I could go to sleep and not wake up").
2. **Non-specific active suicidal thoughts**: "I have thought about killing myself" without a method or plan.
3. **Active suicidal ideation with method**: Thoughts of suicide with a specific method in mind.
4. **Active suicidal ideation with intent**: A plan with some intention to act.
5. **Active suicidal ideation with plan and intent**: A specific plan with intention and timeline.

Escalation along this continuum requires escalating levels of intervention. Items 3 through 5 typically warrant immediate professional evaluation.

## What to Do If You Notice Warning Signs

### In Someone Else

1. **Ask directly and compassionately**: "Are you thinking about suicide?" or "Are you thinking about hurting yourself?" Research consistently shows that asking does not increase risk. It opens the door to help.
2. **Listen without judgment.** Do not argue, minimize ("It's not that bad"), or offer platitudes ("Things will get better"). Validate their pain.
3. **Stay with them.** Do not leave someone in crisis alone.
4. **Help connect them to support:**
   - Bahrain: Emergency 999, Shamsaha 17651421, Salmaniya +973 1728 8888.
   - International: findahelpline.com, iasp.info.
5. **Help reduce access to lethal means** if possible and safe to do so.
6. **Follow up.** Check in during the days and weeks following the crisis.

### In Yourself

1. Recognize that these thoughts are symptoms of a treatable condition, not truths about your worth.
2. Reach out to someone: a trusted person, a crisis helpline, your therapist.
3. Use your safety plan if you have one (see safety_planning.md).
4. Remove or secure access to means.
5. Go to a safe place (a public space, a friend's home, an emergency department).

## For Clinicians: Documentation and Decision Points

When warning signs are identified in a clinical setting:
- Document the specific warning signs observed, using the patient's own words when possible.
- Complete a structured risk assessment (C-SSRS or equivalent).
- Identify and document risk factors and protective factors.
- Determine the level of intervention required (safety planning, increased contact frequency, voluntary hospitalization, involuntary hold if imminent risk).
- Involve the patient in safety planning (see safety_planning_guide.md).
- Document the clinical decision-making rationale.
- Arrange follow-up within 24 to 72 hours for elevated-risk patients.

This information is for educational purposes and crisis support. It does not replace professional clinical judgment.
