## What Is a Safety Plan?

A safety plan is a prioritized, written list of coping strategies and resources that an individual can use during a suicidal crisis. Developed by Barbara Stanley and Gregory Brown, the Safety Planning Intervention (SPI) is an evidence-based, brief intervention that has been shown to reduce suicidal behavior by approximately 45 percent in the six months following creation (Stanley et al., 2018).

A safety plan is **not** a no-suicide contract. No-suicide contracts lack empirical support and may create a false sense of security. A safety plan is a practical, actionable toolkit created collaboratively between the clinician and patient.

## When to Create a Safety Plan

- During initial assessment when suicidal ideation is identified.
- After a suicidal crisis or attempt, before discharge.
- As a proactive measure for patients with recurrent depression and a history of suicidal thoughts.
- When a patient's risk level escalates.
- During routine treatment review for patients with chronic suicidal ideation.

**Create the plan when the patient is relatively calm and able to think clearly, not during acute crisis when cognitive capacity is impaired.**

## The Six Steps: Detailed Template

### Step 1: Warning Signs

**Purpose**: Help the patient recognize their personal signals that a crisis is building, so they can intervene early.

**Clinician guidance**: Ask: "What thoughts, images, moods, situations, or behaviors tell you that a crisis might be developing?" Be specific. Vague answers ("when I feel bad") should be refined into concrete, observable indicators.

**Examples to offer if the patient is struggling:**
- "When I start thinking that everyone would be better off without me."
- "When I have not left my bedroom for more than a day."
- "When I start researching methods online."
- "When I stop responding to messages from people I care about."
- "When I feel an eerie calm after being very distressed."
- "When I drink alone at night."

### Step 2: Internal Coping Strategies

**Purpose**: Activities the patient can do alone, without needing to contact anyone, to reduce the intensity of distress and ride out the crisis.

**Clinician guidance**: Ask: "What can you do on your own, without contacting another person, to take your mind off your problems or help you feel a bit better?" These should be strategies the patient has used before or is willing to try. Test them: "On a scale of 1 to 10, how likely are you to actually do this when you are in crisis?"

**Examples:**
- Grounding exercises: 5-4-3-2-1 technique, box breathing, holding ice.
- Physical activity: walking, stretching, cleaning, cold shower.
- Distraction: watching a specific show or movie, listening to a playlist, solving puzzles.
- Journaling or writing down feelings.
- Going to a public place where there are other people.
- Prayer or religious practice (relevant for many Bahraini patients).

### Step 3: People and Social Settings for Distraction

**Purpose**: Contacts and places that provide distraction and connection without requiring disclosure of suicidal thoughts.

**Clinician guidance**: Ask: "Who are the people you can be around, or what are the places you can go, that help shift your attention? You do not need to tell them what you are going through."

**Examples:**
- Calling a friend to chat about everyday topics.
- Visiting a family member.
- Going to a coffee shop, mall, park, or mosque.

### Step 6: Making the Environment Safe

**Purpose**: Reduce access to lethal means during high-risk periods. Means restriction is one of the most effective suicide prevention strategies because suicidal crises are often time-limited.

**Clinician guidance**: Ask: "Are there things in your environment that you could use to hurt yourself? Can we make a plan to reduce access to those things during difficult times?" This should be collaborative, not coercive. Frame it as a temporary, practical safety measure.

**Areas to address:**
- **Medications**: Can excess medications be given to a trusted person? Can they be locked in a box with the key held by someone else?
- **Firearms**: Can they be stored outside the home or secured with a lock?
- **Sharp objects**: Can items that could be used for self-harm be secured?
- **Other means**: Are there location-specific risks (bridges, heights, traffic) to address?

## After Creating the Plan

### For the Patient

- Keep the safety plan accessible at all times: save a photo on your phone, keep a printed copy in your wallet, post one at home.
- Go through the steps in order during a crisis. Start at Step 1 and move sequentially. Do not skip to Step 5 unless the situation is immediately dangerous.
- Share the plan with at least one person you trust.
- Review and update it regularly, especially after a crisis episode or when circumstances change.
- Bring it to your therapy sessions for review.

### For the Clinician

- Provide the patient with a physical copy and encourage a digital backup.
- Review the safety plan at every session during acute risk periods.
- Update the plan after any crisis episode, change in living situation, change in support network, or change in means access.
- Document that a safety plan was created, reviewed, or updated in the clinical record.
- If the patient is unable or unwilling to complete a safety plan, document this and the clinical rationale for the alternative approach taken.

## Evidence Summary

- Stanley et al. (2018) found that the Safety Planning Intervention plus structured follow-up reduced suicidal behavior by 45 percent over six months compared to usual care.
- The VA has adopted safety planning as a standard of care across all Veterans Health Administration facilities.
- Safety planning is recommended by APA, NIMH, WHO, and NICE as a component of suicide risk management.
- Safety plans are most effective when they are personalized, practiced (not just created), and regularly updated.

This guide is for clinical and educational use. It does not replace professional judgment or comprehensive suicide risk assessment.
