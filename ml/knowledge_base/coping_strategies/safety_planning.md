## What Is a Safety Plan?

A safety plan is a personalized, step-by-step guide created in advance to help you stay safe during moments of suicidal crisis. Developed by Barbara Stanley and Gregory Brown, safety planning is an evidence-based intervention recommended by the NIMH, the WHO, and leading clinical guidelines worldwide. It is one of the most important tools for managing suicidal thoughts.

A safety plan is not a contract. It is a practical toolkit that guides you through a crisis when your ability to think clearly is compromised. Research shows that people who create and use safety plans have significantly fewer suicidal crises and suicide attempts.

## The Stanley-Brown Safety Plan: Six Steps

### Step 1: Recognize Your Warning Signs

Identify the thoughts, feelings, images, situations, or behaviors that signal a crisis may be building. These are your personal early warning signals. Examples:

- "When I start thinking I am a burden to everyone."
- "When I isolate for more than a day and stop answering messages."
- "When I stop eating or caring about basic hygiene."
- "When I feel a strange calm after being very distressed" (this may signal that a decision has been made).
- "When I start thinking about methods or searching for information online."

Write these down in your own words. The more specific, the more useful.

### Step 2: Use Internal Coping Strategies

List things you can do on your own, without contacting another person, to take your mind off distressing thoughts and ride out the intensity. These are strategies you can use immediately:

- Grounding exercises: the 5-4-3-2-1 technique, box breathing, holding ice (see grounding_techniques.md).
- Physical activity: walking, stretching, cleaning, any movement.
- Distraction: watching a specific show, listening to a playlist, solving puzzles, cooking.
- Journaling or writing down what you are feeling.
- Taking a cold shower or splashing cold water on your face.
- Going to a public place (library, coffee shop, park) where you are around people.

The goal is to reduce the immediate intensity so you can think more clearly.

### Step 3: Reach Out to People or Social Settings That Provide Distraction

Identify people you can contact or places you can go to shift your focus. You do not need to disclose what you are feeling. The goal is connection and distraction:

- A friend you can call to chat about everyday things.
- A family member you can visit.
- A coffee shop, mall, or community space where you can be around others.
- An online community or forum where you can engage.

Write down specific names, phone numbers, and places.

### Step 6: Make Your Environment Safe

Work with a trusted person to reduce access to lethal means. This is one of the most effective suicide prevention strategies. Means restriction saves lives because suicidal crises are often time-limited; removing access during the crisis period allows the intensity to pass.

- **Medications**: Give excess medications to a trusted person for safekeeping, or lock them in a location where access requires another person's involvement.
- **Firearms**: If applicable, store them outside the home or give them to someone trusted.
- **Sharp objects**: Secure or remove items that could be used for self-harm.
- **Other means**: Identify anything specific to your situation and create a plan to reduce access.

This step is best completed with a therapist, counselor, or trusted person when you are not in crisis.

## How to Use Your Safety Plan

- **Create it before a crisis**, when you are thinking clearly. Do not try to create it during acute distress.
- **Keep it accessible**: Save it on your phone, print a copy for your wallet, post one at home.
- **Go through the steps in order.** Start at Step 1 and move through sequentially. Do not skip to Step 5 unless Steps 1 through 4 have been exhausted or the situation is immediately dangerous.
- **Share it** with at least one person you trust.
- **Review and update it regularly** with your therapist or counselor.
- **Using your safety plan is a sign of strength**, not weakness. It means you are actively fighting for your life.

## Important Reminders

- Suicidal crises are temporary states, even when they feel permanent. The intensity will pass.
- Having suicidal thoughts does not mean you will act on them. Thoughts are not commands.
- You deserve help, and help is available right now.
- You matter.
