## Energy Management

When depression causes persistent fatigue, managing your limited energy intentionally becomes essential. Energy management strategies draw from occupational therapy, CBT, and acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT) to help you function as well as possible while respecting your body's current limits.

### Core Principles

**Accept your current capacity.** Fighting against fatigue with self-criticism ("I should be able to do more") wastes the energy you do have. Acknowledging your present state is not giving up; it is being strategic.

**Prioritize ruthlessly.** Not everything on your to-do list has equal importance. Each day, identify one to three things that truly need to happen. Let the rest wait without guilt.

### Practical Strategies

- **Use the "spoon theory" framework.** Imagine you start each day with a limited number of "spoons" (units of energy). Allocate them deliberately to the tasks that matter most.
- **Break tasks into micro-steps.** Instead of "clean the kitchen," try "put three dishes in the dishwasher." Completing a small step builds momentum without depleting your reserves.
- **Alternate activity and rest.** Use a pacing strategy: 20 minutes of activity followed by 10 minutes of rest. Avoid pushing through until you crash.
- **Identify your peak hours.** Most people with depression have a time of day when energy is slightly higher. Schedule important tasks during that window.
- **Move gently.** It sounds counterintuitive, but light physical activity (a short walk, gentle stretching) can improve energy levels. Start with just 5 to 10 minutes.
- **Reduce decision fatigue.** Simplify choices where you can: plan meals in advance, lay out clothes the night before, or use routines to reduce mental load.
- **Stay hydrated and nourished.** Dehydration and skipped meals worsen fatigue. Keep water and simple snacks accessible.

### A Compassionate Reminder

Fatigue from depression is real and physiological. It is not laziness or weakness. Treating yourself with patience as you manage your energy is itself a meaningful act of self-care.
