## Sleep Hygiene

Sleep hygiene refers to a set of evidence-based habits and environmental adjustments that promote consistent, restorative sleep. Because sleep disturbance and depression have a strong bidirectional relationship, improving sleep quality can meaningfully support mood recovery.

### Core Sleep Hygiene Practices

**Maintain a consistent schedule:**
- Go to bed and wake up at the same time every day, including weekends. Consistency helps regulate your circadian rhythm.
- Avoid sleeping in to "catch up" on sleep, as this can shift your internal clock.

**Create a sleep-supportive environment:**
- Keep your bedroom cool, dark, and quiet.
- Reserve the bed for sleep and intimacy only. Avoid working, scrolling, or watching TV in bed.
- Consider blackout curtains, earplugs, or a white noise machine if needed.

**Wind down before bed:**
- Establish a 30- to 60-minute pre-sleep routine that signals to your body it is time to rest.
- Try gentle stretching, reading a physical book, or a warm shower.
- Avoid screens for at least 30 minutes before bed. Blue light suppresses melatonin production.

**Watch what you consume:**
- Limit caffeine after noon, as its effects can last 6 to 8 hours.
- Avoid alcohol close to bedtime. Although it may help you fall asleep, it disrupts sleep architecture and leads to fragmented rest.
- Avoid heavy meals within 2 to 3 hours of bedtime.

**Manage nighttime wakefulness:**
- If you cannot fall asleep within 20 minutes, get out of bed and do a quiet, non-stimulating activity until you feel sleepy. Then return to bed.
- Avoid clock-watching, which increases anxiety about sleep.

### When to Seek More Support

If sleep problems persist despite consistent sleep hygiene practices, cognitive-behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I) is the gold-standard treatment recommended by the American Academy of Sleep Medicine. It is more effective than medication for long-term improvement. Talk to your healthcare provider about a referral.

This information is for educational purposes and does not replace professional treatment.

Source: American Academy of Sleep Medicine; NIMH; National Sleep Foundation
