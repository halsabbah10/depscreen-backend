# Starting and Stopping Antidepressants

## The Therapeutic Lag: Why Antidepressants Take Time

Antidepressants do not work like pain relievers or anxiolytics. They require time for neuroadaptive changes to occur.

### Timeline of Effects
- **Days 1-7**: Side effects may appear before any therapeutic benefit. Some patients feel worse (increased anxiety, agitation). This is expected.
- **Weeks 2-4**: Earliest signs of improvement — often noticed by others before the patient. Sleep and appetite may improve first; mood follows.
- **Weeks 4-6**: More substantial mood improvement in responders. Anhedonia and cognitive symptoms may take longer.
- **Weeks 6-8**: Full therapeutic effect. If no meaningful response by 6-8 weeks at adequate dose, re-evaluation is warranted.
- **Weeks 8-12**: For partial responders, dose optimization or augmentation considered.

### Why It Takes Time
- Acute serotonin/norepinephrine increase occurs within hours.
- Therapeutic effects depend on **downstream neuroplastic changes**: receptor desensitization (autoreceptor downregulation), enhanced BDNF expression, hippocampal neurogenesis, and cortical connectivity remodeling. These processes require weeks.

## Starting an Antidepressant: Titration

### General Principles
- **Start low, go slow** — particularly in elderly patients, those with anxiety, and those sensitive to medication.
- Begin at the lowest recommended starting dose.
- Increase every 1-4 weeks based on response and tolerability.
- Target the minimum effective dose, but do not underdose — inadequate dosing is a leading cause of "treatment failure."

### Typical Titration Examples
- **Sertraline**: 50 mg → 100 mg after 2-4 weeks → 150-200 mg if needed.
- **Escitalopram**: 10 mg → 20 mg after 2-4 weeks if needed.
- **Venlafaxine XR**: 37.5 mg → 75 mg after 1 week → 150 mg after 2-4 weeks → 225 mg if needed.
- **Mirtazapine**: 15 mg → 30 mg after 2 weeks → 45 mg if needed.

### What Constitutes an Adequate Trial?
An antidepressant trial is considered adequate when:
1. Therapeutic dose reached (not just starting dose).
2. Maintained at therapeutic dose for **at least 6-8 weeks**.
3. Adherence confirmed (medication actually taken consistently).

Failure to meet all three criteria means the medication has not been adequately trialed.

## Maintenance Treatment: How Long to Continue

### First Episode
- Continue at the effective dose for **at least 6-12 months** after achieving remission.
- Discontinuing too early significantly increases relapse risk (up to 50% within 6 months).

### Recurrent Episodes (2+ Episodes)
- **At least 2 years** of maintenance therapy, often longer.
- After 3+ episodes, indefinite treatment is frequently recommended, as recurrence risk exceeds 90%.

### Ongoing Risk Assessment
- Decision to taper involves balancing recurrence risk against side effect burden.
- Factors favoring longer treatment: severe episodes, residual symptoms, family history, chronic stressors, comorbid anxiety, history of suicidal ideation.

## Stopping Antidepressants: Never Abruptly

### Why Gradual Tapering Is Essential
Abrupt discontinuation can cause **antidepressant discontinuation syndrome** — a cluster of physical and psychological symptoms that are distressing, sometimes severe, and can be mistaken for relapse.

### Discontinuation Syndrome Symptoms
- **Sensory**: "Brain zaps" (electric shock sensations in head/body), paresthesias, visual disturbances.
- **Vestibular**: Dizziness, vertigo, lightheadedness, difficulty with balance.
- **Gastrointestinal**: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal cramps.
- **Affective**: Irritability, anxiety, agitation, low mood, tearfulness.
- **General**: Fatigue, malaise, flu-like symptoms, myalgias, chills, sweating.
- **Sleep**: Insomnia, vivid dreams, nightmares.

### Risk Factors for Severe Discontinuation
- **Short half-life agents**: Venlafaxine (highest risk), paroxetine, desvenlafaxine, fluvoxamine.
- **Long duration of treatment**: Higher risk after prolonged use.
- **Higher doses**: Greater neuroadaptation to reverse.
- **Lowest risk**: Fluoxetine (self-tapers due to ~4-6 day active metabolite half-life).

### Taper Protocols

**General recommendation**: Reduce dose by 25% every 2-4 weeks, with slower reductions at lower doses. The final steps are often the hardest.

| Medication | Typical Taper Approach |
|---|---|
| Sertraline | 200→150→100→50→25 mg, each step 2-4 weeks |
| Escitalopram | 20→15→10→5 mg, each step 2-4 weeks |
| Venlafaxine XR | Very gradual: 10-25% reductions q2-4 weeks. May need to open capsules and count beads for micro-taper. |
| Paroxetine | Slow taper essential: 20→15→10→5 mg, each step 2-4 weeks minimum |
| Mirtazapine | 45→30→15→7.5 mg, each step 2-4 weeks |

**Hyperbolic tapering**: Emerging evidence supports that dose reductions should follow the pharmacological dose-response curve (hyperbolic), meaning smaller absolute reductions at lower doses. For example, dropping from 20 mg to 10 mg SSRi reduces serotonin transporter occupancy by ~10%, but dropping from 10 mg to 0 mg reduces it by ~50%. This is why the last reduction is the hardest.

### Distinguishing Discontinuation from Relapse

| Feature | Discontinuation Syndrome | Relapse |
|---|---|---|
| Onset | Within days of dose reduction | Gradual, over weeks |
| Symptoms | Physical (brain zaps, dizziness, flu-like) | Mood symptoms predominate |
| Course | Resolves within 1-2 weeks (or with dose reinstatement) | Persists and worsens |
| Novel symptoms | Often includes symptoms never had before | Familiar pattern returns |

**If uncertain**: Reinstate the last effective dose. If symptoms resolve within 24-48 hours, it was discontinuation. Then resume a slower taper.

## Key Patient Counseling Points

1. **Never stop your antidepressant suddenly.** Even if you feel better, abrupt cessation can cause distressing symptoms and increase relapse risk.
2. **Feeling better does not mean you no longer need the medication.** The medication is part of why you feel better.
3. **Talk to your prescriber before any changes.** Together, you can plan a safe, gradual taper when the time is right.
4. **Missing doses matters** — especially for short-half-life medications. Keep a consistent daily routine.
5. **Stopping is a process**, not a single event. A proper taper may take weeks to months.
