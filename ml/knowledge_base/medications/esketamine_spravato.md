# Esketamine (Spravato)

## Overview

Esketamine (Spravato) is an intranasal formulation of the S-enantiomer of ketamine, representing the most significant mechanistic departure from traditional monoamine-based antidepressants in decades. It acts on the glutamatergic system rather than serotonin or norepinephrine.

## FDA Approvals

### Treatment-Resistant Depression (March 2019)
- Approved as adjunctive therapy (with an oral antidepressant) for adults with treatment-resistant depression (TRD).
- TRD defined as failure to respond to at least two adequate antidepressant trials in the current episode.

### Major Depressive Disorder with Acute Suicidal Ideation or Behavior (August 2020)
- Approved for adults with MDD and acute suicidal ideation or behavior, in conjunction with an oral antidepressant.
- The rapid onset (often within hours to days) makes it particularly valuable in this acute setting.

### Monotherapy for TRD (January 2025)
- Approved as monotherapy (without a concurrent oral antidepressant) for TRD in adults.
- This expanded indication allows use in patients who cannot tolerate oral antidepressants.

## Mechanism of Action

### NMDA Receptor Antagonism
- Esketamine blocks N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) glutamate receptors, particularly at GABAergic interneurons.
- This paradoxically increases glutamate release ("glutamate burst"), activating AMPA receptors.
- AMPA activation triggers intracellular signaling cascades → increased BDNF release → mTOR pathway activation → rapid synaptogenesis and synaptic plasticity.

### Why This Matters
- Traditional antidepressants take weeks to produce neuroplastic changes through monoamine-mediated pathways.
- Esketamine produces rapid synaptic remodeling (within hours), explaining the fast onset of action.
- This represents a fundamentally different approach — targeting the glutamate system rather than monoamine reuptake.

### Enantiomer Specificity
- Ketamine is a racemic mixture of S-ketamine and R-ketamine.
- S-ketamine (esketamine) has ~3-4x higher NMDA binding affinity than R-ketamine.
- Intranasal esketamine was developed for standardized dosing and regulatory approval rather than IV racemic ketamine.

## Administration and REMS (Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategy)

### Certified Settings Only
Esketamine must be administered in a **certified healthcare facility** under direct supervision. It cannot be dispensed for home use.

### Dosing Protocol

**Induction Phase (Weeks 1-4)**:
- 56 mg or 84 mg intranasally, twice per week.
- Each device delivers 28 mg (two sprays); doses require 2 or 3 devices per session.

**Maintenance Phase (Weeks 5-8)**:
- 56 mg or 84 mg once weekly.

**Extended Maintenance (Week 9+)**:
- 56 mg or 84 mg every 1-2 weeks (individualized to the minimum effective frequency).

### Post-Administration Observation
- **Mandatory 2-hour observation** after each dose.
- Monitor for: sedation, dissociation, blood pressure changes, perceptual disturbances.
- Blood pressure measured before dosing, at 40 minutes, and before discharge.
- Patient must not drive or operate machinery for the remainder of the dosing day.
- A responsible caregiver should be available for transport home.

## Efficacy Data

### TRD Trials (TRANSFORM/SUSTAIN)
- TRANSFORM-2: Esketamine 56 or 84 mg + new oral AD vs. placebo nasal spray + new oral AD. Statistically significant improvement in MADRS score at day 28 (difference: -4.0 points; p=0.020).
- SUSTAIN-1: Relapse prevention. Stable remitters randomized to continue esketamine or switch to placebo. Relapse rate: 26.7% (esketamine) vs. 45.3% (placebo). Stable responders: 25.8% vs. 57.6%.

### Suicidal Ideation (ASPIRE Trials)
- Rapid reduction in MADRS and CGI-SS scores within 24 hours.
- Statistically significant improvement over placebo + standard of care at 24 hours.
- This rapid onset is the primary clinical advantage in acute suicidal crises.

### Speed of Response
- Some patients experience clinically meaningful improvement within 4-24 hours.
- Full treatment response evaluated at 4 weeks (adequate trial duration).
- If no meaningful improvement by 4 weeks at adequate dose, reassess.

## Side Effects

### Common (During and Immediately After Dosing)
- **Dissociation** (40-45%): Feelings of detachment, altered sense of time, perceptual changes. Typically peaks at 40 minutes, resolves within 1.5 hours.
- **Dizziness** (30-35%).
- **Nausea** (25-30%).
- **Sedation/somnolence** (20-25%).
- **Vertigo** (20%).
- **Hypoesthesia** (oral numbness, ~15%).
- **Anxiety** (10-15%).
- **Blood pressure elevation**: Transient. Mean increase of ~7-9 mmHg systolic at 40 minutes. Resolves by 2 hours in most patients.

### Serious Risks
- **Suicidality**: Black box warning (class-wide for antidepressants). Paradoxically, esketamine may reduce suicidal ideation acutely.
- **Abuse potential**: Schedule III controlled substance. Ketamine is an established drug of abuse. REMS program mitigates diversion risk.
- **Significant BP elevation**: Withhold if BP >140/90 at pre-dose check. Contraindicated in aneurysmal vascular disease, arteriovenous malformation, intracerebral hemorrhage history.
- **Cognitive effects**: Attention and concentration impairment reported during and shortly after dosing. Long-term cognitive effects under ongoing study.
- **Ulcerative or interstitial cystitis**: Known with chronic ketamine abuse; unclear risk at esketamine treatment doses.

## Contraindications
- Aneurysmal vascular disease (intracranial, thoracic, abdominal, or peripheral).
- Arteriovenous malformation.
- History of intracerebral hemorrhage.
- Hypersensitivity to esketamine, ketamine, or excipients.

## Anti-Suicidal Properties

Esketamine is one of only two medications (along with lithium) with evidence for reducing suicidal thoughts independently:
- Lithium: Chronic, maintenance-phase anti-suicidal effect (weeks to months to manifest).
- Esketamine: Acute, rapid anti-suicidal effect (hours to days).
- Clozapine: FDA-approved for suicidality in schizophrenia (different population).

## Practical Considerations

- **Cost**: Approximately $600-900 per session before insurance. Variable coverage.
- **Time commitment**: Each session requires ~2.5 hours (dosing + observation). Twice weekly initially.
- **Long-term data**: Ongoing. Open-label extension studies show sustained benefit with continued use.
- **IV Ketamine vs. Esketamine**: IV racemic ketamine is used off-label at many clinics. It is not FDA-approved for depression, lacks REMS infrastructure, and has variable dosing protocols. Esketamine offers a standardized, regulated alternative.
