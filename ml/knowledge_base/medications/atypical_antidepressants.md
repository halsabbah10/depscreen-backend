# Atypical Antidepressants

## Overview

Atypical antidepressants are a heterogeneous group of medications that do not fit neatly into the SSRI, SNRI, TCA, or MAOI categories. Each has a distinct mechanism of action, offering alternatives when standard first-line agents are poorly tolerated or ineffective. Their unique pharmacological profiles make them valuable for targeting specific symptom clusters.

## Bupropion (Wellbutrin)

### Mechanism
Norepinephrine-dopamine reuptake inhibitor (NDRI). Does not affect serotonin, which accounts for its distinct side effect profile.

### Dose Ranges
- **Bupropion SR**: 150 mg once daily → 150 mg twice daily (max 400 mg/day)
- **Bupropion XL**: 150 mg once daily → 300 mg once daily (max 450 mg/day)

### Advantages
- **No sexual dysfunction** — a major differentiator. Often used as augmentation or switch when SSRIs/SNRIs cause sexual side effects.
- **Weight-neutral to weight loss** — one of few antidepressants not associated with weight gain.
- **Activating** — beneficial for patients with fatigue, psychomotor retardation, hypersomnia.
- **Smoking cessation** — FDA-approved as Zyban for this indication.
- **ADHD symptoms** — off-label benefit for comorbid attention difficulties.

### Risks and Limitations
- **Seizure risk**: Dose-dependent. Contraindicated above 450 mg/day, in seizure disorders, bulimia, anorexia (electrolyte instability), and abrupt benzodiazepine/alcohol withdrawal.
- **Not anxiolytic** — may worsen anxiety, especially at initiation. Poor choice as monotherapy for anxious depression.
- **Insomnia** — take last dose before 3 PM (for twice-daily SR) or take XL in the morning.
- No efficacy for panic disorder or OCD.

## Mirtazapine (Remeron)

### Mechanism
Noradrenergic and specific serotonergic antidepressant (NaSSA). Alpha-2 adrenergic antagonist enhancing NE and 5-HT release. Blocks 5-HT2A, 5-HT2C, 5-HT3, and H1 histamine receptors.

### Dose Ranges
- Start: 15 mg at bedtime
- Therapeutic: 15-45 mg/day
- Paradox: More sedating at lower doses (15 mg) due to predominant H1 blockade; less sedating at 30-45 mg as noradrenergic activation increases.

### Advantages
- **Strong sedation at low doses** — useful for depression with severe insomnia.
- **Appetite stimulation** — beneficial in underweight, anorexic, or elderly patients with poor appetite.
- **Rapid anxiolytic effect** — 5-HT2A/5-HT3 blockade provides early anti-anxiety benefits.
- **Antiemetic properties** — 5-HT3 blockade. Used off-label for nausea.
- **Minimal sexual dysfunction** compared to SSRIs/SNRIs.
- **Low drug interaction potential** — minimal CYP450 inhibition.

### Risks and Limitations
- **Weight gain** — significant, often 5-15 lbs. Most problematic side effect for long-term adherence.
- **Sedation** — useful or problematic depending on the patient.
- **Elevated lipids and triglycerides** in some patients.
- **Agranulocytosis** — extremely rare (<0.1%) but serious. Counsel patients to report fever, sore throat, mouth sores.

## Trazodone (Desyrel)

### Mechanism
Serotonin antagonist and reuptake inhibitor (SARI). Blocks 5-HT2A receptors and inhibits serotonin reuptake. Strong H1 and alpha-1 antagonism.

### Dose Ranges
- **For insomnia** (most common use): 25-100 mg at bedtime
- **For depression**: 150-400 mg/day (divided doses); max 600 mg/day (inpatient)

### Clinical Use
- Predominantly used as a **sleep aid** at low doses rather than a primary antidepressant.
- Antidepressant doses (≥150 mg) rarely used due to excessive sedation and orthostatic hypotension.
- Often co-prescribed with SSRIs/SNRIs to manage treatment-emergent insomnia.
- Non-addictive alternative to benzodiazepines for sleep.

### Risks
- **Priapism**: Rare (~1 in 10,000) but a urologic emergency. Counsel male patients.
- **Orthostatic hypotension**: Significant, especially in elderly. Fall risk.
- **Cardiac arrhythmias**: QT prolongation possible at high doses.
- **Excessive sedation**: Daytime grogginess common.

## Vortioxetine (Trintellix)

### Mechanism
Multimodal: SERT inhibition plus 5-HT3/5-HT7 antagonism, 5-HT1B partial agonism, 5-HT1A agonism. This combination modulates multiple neurotransmitter systems including glutamate, GABA, acetylcholine, and histamine.

### Dose Ranges
- Start: 10 mg/day
- Therapeutic: 10-20 mg/day
- Dose reduction to 5 mg if poorly tolerated

### Advantages
- **Cognitive benefits**: Demonstrated improvements in processing speed, executive function, and verbal memory in clinical trials — unique among antidepressants. Particularly relevant for patients with cognitive complaints ("brain fog").
- **Lower sexual dysfunction** rates compared to SSRIs at 10 mg.
- **Well-tolerated in elderly** patients.

### Risks
- **Nausea**: Most common side effect (dose-dependent, up to 30% at 20 mg). Taking with food helps.
- **Cost**: Expensive; limited generic availability in some markets.
- **CYP2D6 interactions**: Reduce dose by 50% in poor metabolizers or with strong CYP2D6 inhibitors.

## Choosing an Atypical Antidepressant

| Symptom Profile | Preferred Agent | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Sexual dysfunction on SSRI | Bupropion | No serotonergic mechanism |
| Severe insomnia | Mirtazapine or trazodone | Strong sedating properties |
| Poor appetite / underweight | Mirtazapine | Appetite stimulation |
| Fatigue / hypersomnia | Bupropion | Activating / dopaminergic |
| Cognitive complaints | Vortioxetine | Demonstrated procognitive effects |
| Comorbid smoking | Bupropion | Dual indication |
| Obesity / weight concerns | Bupropion | Weight-neutral to weight loss |
