# Treatment-Resistant Depression (TRD)

## Definition

Treatment-resistant depression is defined as major depressive disorder that has **failed to respond adequately to at least two antidepressant trials** of adequate dose and duration in the current episode. This affects approximately 30% of patients with MDD.

### What Constitutes an "Adequate Trial"?
1. **Adequate dose**: Minimum therapeutic dose reached (not just starting dose).
2. **Adequate duration**: At least 6-8 weeks at therapeutic dose.
3. **Adequate adherence**: Medication actually taken consistently (30-60% of patients have suboptimal adherence).

All three criteria must be met. Failure to meet any one criterion means the patient may be "pseudo-resistant" rather than truly treatment-resistant.

## Before Diagnosing TRD: Rule Out Pseudo-Resistance

### Adherence Assessment
- Up to 50% of patients do not take antidepressants as prescribed.
- Assess using direct questioning (non-judgmental), pill counts, pharmacy refill records.
- Common reasons: side effects, cost, forgetting, stigma, feeling better and stopping, not believing medication works.

### Dose Adequacy
- Many patients are maintained at starting doses that are sub-therapeutic.
- Ensure the dose has been optimized to the upper end of the therapeutic range before declaring failure.

### Duration Adequacy
- Response can take 6-8 weeks. Some patients improve at 8-12 weeks.
- Premature switching is a common clinical error.

### Diagnostic Reassessment
- **Bipolar depression**: Up to 40% of "treatment-resistant" unipolar depression cases may actually be bipolar. Antidepressants alone are often ineffective (and potentially harmful) in bipolar depression. Screen for prior manic/hypomanic episodes (MDQ, detailed history).
- **Comorbid anxiety disorders**: Present in >50% of MDD cases. May require higher doses or specific agents.
- **Substance use disorders**: Active alcohol, cannabis, or opioid use undermines treatment response. Screen with AUDIT, DAST-10.
- **Medical conditions masquerading as or exacerbating depression**:
  - Hypothyroidism (check TSH)
  - Vitamin B12/folate deficiency
  - Anemia (CBC)
  - Obstructive sleep apnea
  - Chronic pain syndromes
  - Cushing's syndrome, Addison's disease
  - Central nervous system conditions (MS, Parkinson's, post-stroke)
- **Personality disorders**: Comorbid borderline personality disorder or other cluster B disorders can reduce antidepressant response.

## Pharmacological Strategies for TRD

### Step 1: Optimize Current Treatment
- Maximize dose to top of therapeutic range.
- Extend duration to full 8-12 weeks.
- Address adherence barriers.
- Confirm adequate lab workup.

### Step 2: Switching Antidepressants
- **Within class** (SSRI → SSRI): ~25% response rate. Easiest switch, fewest interactions.
- **Between classes** (SSRI → SNRI or SSRI → bupropion): Preferred. Different mechanism may address non-responsive symptoms. ~30-35% response rate.
- **To a different class entirely** (SSRI → mirtazapine, SSRI → TCA): Consider based on symptom profile.
- Cross-taper or brief washout depending on interaction risk.

### Step 3: Augmentation
- **Lithium**: Strongest evidence. NNT ~5. See augmentation_strategies.md for details.
- **Atypical antipsychotics**: Aripiprazole, quetiapine XR, brexpiprazole, cariprazine (all FDA-approved for adjunctive MDD treatment).
- **T3 (liothyronine)**: 25-50 mcg/day. Evidence primarily with TCAs but some SSRI data.
- **Bupropion add-on**: Common in practice (STAR*D). Complementary mechanism.
- **Mirtazapine add-on**: Addresses insomnia, appetite, anxiety.

### Step 4: Esketamine (Spravato)
- FDA-approved for TRD (adjunctive and monotherapy).
- Intranasal, administered in certified settings. 2-hour observation required.
- Rapid onset (hours to days). NMDA antagonist mechanism.
- See esketamine_spravato.md for full details.
- Particularly valuable for patients with acute suicidal ideation.

### Step 5: IV Ketamine
- Off-label use in specialized clinics.
- Typical protocol: 0.5 mg/kg IV over 40 minutes, 2-3 times weekly for 2-4 weeks, then taper to maintenance.
- Rapid response (often within hours).
- Not FDA-approved for depression — lacks standardized protocols.
- Requires monitoring: vitals, dissociation, bladder health with chronic use.

## Neuromodulation Therapies

### Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT)
- **The most effective treatment for severe, treatment-resistant depression.** Response rates of 50-70%, even after multiple medication failures.
- Mechanism: Electrically induced generalized seizure under general anesthesia. Produces widespread neurobiological changes including neurotransmitter modulation, neuroplasticity, and neuroendocrine normalization.
- **Indications**: Severe TRD, active suicidality, psychotic depression, catatonia, depression in pregnancy (when medication is not feasible).
- **Modern ECT**: Ultra-brief pulse, unilateral electrode placement minimizes cognitive side effects.
- **Side effects**: Short-term memory impairment (most patients recover within weeks to months), headache, nausea, muscle soreness. Retrograde amnesia for events near treatment is possible.
- **Schedule**: Typically 2-3 sessions per week for 6-12 sessions, then maintenance (weekly → biweekly → monthly).

### Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS)
- **FDA-cleared for TRD** (failed ≥1 antidepressant trial).
- Non-invasive: Magnetic pulses delivered to the left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC).
- **Standard protocol**: 5 sessions/week for 4-6 weeks (20-30 sessions total).
- **Theta-burst stimulation (TBS)**: Accelerated protocol — 3-minute sessions (vs. 37 minutes for standard TMS). FDA-cleared. Stanford Accelerated Intelligent Neuromodulation Therapy (SAINT) protocol shows ~80% remission in open-label studies.
- Response rate: ~50-60% (response), ~30% (remission) in pivotal trials.
- Side effects: Scalp discomfort, headache. Seizure risk is very rare (<0.1%).
- Advantages: No anesthesia, no systemic side effects, performed in outpatient setting.

### Vagus Nerve Stimulation (VNS)
- FDA-approved for chronic or recurrent TRD (failed ≥4 adequate treatments).
- Surgically implanted pulse generator stimulates the left vagus nerve.
- Slow onset: benefits accumulate over months to years.
- Reserved for the most refractory cases.

### Deep Brain Stimulation (DBS)
- Investigational. Targets subcallosal cingulate cortex (Brodmann area 25) or other nodes.
- Early results promising but inconsistent across trials.
- Not standard of care.

## Psychotherapy in TRD

- Psychotherapy should not be abandoned in TRD — it remains a critical component.
- **CBT**: Evidence for augmentation benefit in TRD (Wiles et al., Lancet 2013).
- **CBASP (Cognitive Behavioral Analysis System of Psychotherapy)**: Specifically designed for chronic depression.
- **Behavioral Activation**: Robust evidence, particularly for anergic presentations.
- **Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT)**: Evidence for relapse prevention.

## Comprehensive TRD Evaluation Checklist

| Assessment | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Detailed medication history | Confirm adequate trials (dose, duration, adherence) |
| Structured diagnostic interview | Rule out bipolar, personality disorders |
| Substance use screening | AUDIT, DAST-10 |
| Medical workup: TSH, B12, folate, CBC, CMP | Rule out medical causes |
| Sleep assessment | OSA screening (STOP-BANG) |
| Pain assessment | Chronic pain exacerbating depression |
| Pharmacogenomic testing | Consider if ≥2 failures; may guide agent selection |
| Psychosocial assessment | Ongoing stressors, trauma, relationship conflict |

## Prognosis

- TRD is chronic but not hopeless. With systematic evaluation and stepped-care approach, the majority of patients achieve meaningful improvement.
- STAR*D demonstrated cumulative remission rates of ~67% through four treatment steps.
- Each successive step has lower remission rates — but options exist at every level.
- The most critical factors: accurate diagnosis, adequate trials, treatment persistence, and multimodal approach (medication + therapy + neuromodulation when indicated).
