# Common Side Effects of Antidepressants — What to Expect

## Starting an Antidepressant

Beginning antidepressant medication is a significant step. Most side effects are mild, peak in the first 1-2 weeks, and improve as your body adjusts. Understanding what to expect can help you stay on track with treatment during the adjustment period.

## Temporary Side Effects (Usually Resolve in 1-3 Weeks)

### Nausea and Stomach Upset
- The most common early side effect, especially with SSRIs and SNRIs.
- Taking medication with food or at bedtime can reduce nausea.
- Ginger tea or small frequent meals may help.
- Usually resolves within 7-14 days.

### Headache
- Common in the first week.
- Typically mild and responsive to over-the-counter pain relief.
- Report persistent or severe headaches to your provider.

### Initial Anxiety or Restlessness
- Paradoxically, some antidepressants (especially SSRIs) can briefly increase anxiety before reducing it.
- This "activation syndrome" is more common with activating agents (fluoxetine, sertraline, bupropion).
- Your provider may start at a lower dose or prescribe a short-term anxiolytic.
- Should improve within 1-2 weeks.

### Dizziness
- Especially common with SNRIs and mirtazapine.
- Rise slowly from sitting or lying positions.
- Stay hydrated. Avoid alcohol.

### Sleep Changes
- **Insomnia**: More common with activating antidepressants (SSRIs, bupropion). Take medication in the morning.
- **Drowsiness**: More common with mirtazapine, trazodone, sedating TCAs. Take at bedtime.
- Sleep often normalizes within 2-3 weeks.

### Dry Mouth
- Common with TCAs, paroxetine, and some SNRIs.
- Sugar-free gum, frequent water sips, and saliva substitutes can help.

### Diarrhea or Constipation
- SSRIs tend to cause diarrhea; SNRIs and TCAs tend to cause constipation.
- Adequate hydration and fiber intake are helpful.

## Persistent Side Effects (May Require Management)

### Sexual Dysfunction
- Affects 25-73% of patients on SSRIs/SNRIs.
- Includes decreased libido, difficulty with arousal, delayed orgasm, or anorgasmia.
- Often does not improve with time.
- **Management options**:
  - Dose reduction (if clinically appropriate).
  - Switch to bupropion, mirtazapine, or vortioxetine (lower sexual side effect rates).
  - Add bupropion as augmentation.
  - Discuss with your provider — this is a common, manageable problem.
- Never stop medication without talking to your provider first.

### Weight Changes
- **Weight gain**: Most common with mirtazapine, paroxetine, amitriptyline, and some other TCAs. Can be 5-15+ lbs over months.
- **Weight neutral**: Sertraline, escitalopram, venlafaxine, desvenlafaxine (variable).
- **Weight loss**: Bupropion, fluoxetine (short-term).
- Regular exercise and dietary monitoring can help. Discuss with your provider if weight change exceeds 5% of body weight.

### Excessive Sweating
- Common with SSRIs and SNRIs.
- Can be socially distressing.
- Options: dose reduction, anticholinergic agents (discuss with provider).

### Fatigue or Drowsiness
- If persistent beyond the adjustment period, timing changes (take sedating meds at night) or medication switch may help.

## When to Call Your Doctor — Do Not Wait

Contact your prescriber or seek immediate care for:

- **Worsening depression or new suicidal thoughts** — especially in the first few weeks or after dose changes. This is critical for patients under 25 (see FDA black box warning).
- **Agitation, restlessness, or feeling "wired"** that is distressing or worsening.
- **Signs of serotonin syndrome**: Confusion, rapid heart rate, high blood pressure, dilated pupils, muscle twitching or rigidity, heavy sweating, diarrhea, high fever. This is a medical emergency.
- **Severe allergic reaction**: Rash, hives, swelling of face/tongue, difficulty breathing.
- **Unusual bleeding or bruising**: SSRIs can affect platelet function.
- **Painful or prolonged erection (priapism)**: Rare but urgent, especially with trazodone. Seek ER care immediately.
- **Significant changes in heart rhythm**: Palpitations, fainting, chest pain (especially with TCAs).
- **Severe hypertension symptoms**: Sudden severe headache, neck stiffness, nausea (especially with MAOIs).
- **Seizure**: Seek emergency care. Particularly relevant with bupropion at high doses.

## What Not to Worry About

- Mild nausea or headache in the first week — your body is adjusting.
- Slight sleep changes — these typically normalize.
- Not feeling better immediately — therapeutic effects take 2-4 weeks to begin and 6-8 weeks for full benefit.
- Vivid dreams — common, usually not harmful, and often temporary.

## Tips for the Adjustment Period

1. **Give it time.** Most side effects diminish. Most benefits take weeks to appear.
2. **Take it consistently.** Same time each day builds steady blood levels.
3. **Don't adjust the dose yourself.** More is not better, and stopping abruptly is harmful.
4. **Keep a brief daily log.** Track mood, sleep, side effects. This helps your provider make informed adjustments.
5. **Stay in contact.** Follow up within 2-4 weeks of starting, or sooner if side effects are significant.
6. **Avoid alcohol.** It can worsen side effects and counteract treatment benefits.
