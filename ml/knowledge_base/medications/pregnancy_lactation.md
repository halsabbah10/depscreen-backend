# Antidepressants in Pregnancy and Lactation

## The Central Principle: Risk-Benefit Analysis

Treating depression during pregnancy is never a choice between "risk" and "no risk." It is a choice between the risks of medication exposure and the risks of untreated or undertreated depression. Both carry consequences for the mother and child.

## Risks of Untreated Depression in Pregnancy

Untreated maternal depression is not a benign condition:

- **Maternal**: Poor prenatal care, inadequate nutrition, substance use, preeclampsia, suicidal ideation, postpartum depression (up to 50% of antenatal depression progresses).
- **Obstetric**: Preterm birth (OR 1.4-1.6), low birth weight, preeclampsia association.
- **Fetal/Neonatal**: Elevated cortisol exposure (HPA axis programming), lower Apgar scores, NICU admission, impaired bonding.
- **Child development**: Increased risk of emotional and behavioral problems, cognitive delays, and attachment difficulties in offspring of untreated depressed mothers.

These risks are real, measurable, and well-documented. The decision to withhold treatment must weigh these outcomes.

## Antidepressant Safety Data in Pregnancy

### SSRIs — Most Studied

**Sertraline (Zoloft)**
- The most extensively studied antidepressant in pregnancy.
- No consistent evidence of major congenital malformations.
- Generally considered the preferred first-line SSRI in pregnancy.
- Low placental transfer.

**Fluoxetine (Prozac)**
- Extensive safety data. No consistent teratogenic signal.
- Long half-life may be advantageous (steady levels) or disadvantageous (longer neonatal exposure).

**Escitalopram (Lexapro) and Citalopram (Celexa)**
- Growing safety data. No consistent malformation signal.
- Reasonable alternatives.

**Paroxetine (Paxil)**
- Some studies suggest a small increased risk of cardiac malformations (atrial and ventricular septal defects), particularly with first-trimester exposure. Absolute risk remains low (~2% vs. 1% baseline).
- **Generally avoided as a first choice in pregnancy** due to this signal and short half-life (discontinuation risk during labor).
- FDA Pregnancy Category D (other SSRIs are Category C).

### SNRIs
- **Venlafaxine and duloxetine**: Less pregnancy data than SSRIs. Available evidence does not show major teratogenicity.
- Venlafaxine: concern for neonatal adaptation syndrome may be more pronounced.
- Used when SSRIs inadequate and clinical benefit is clear.

### Bupropion
- Registry data (Bupropion Pregnancy Registry) show no increased risk of major malformations.
- Some signal for cardiac defects not consistently replicated.
- Reasonable option, especially for patients with comorbid smoking cessation needs.

### TCAs
- Decades of data. No consistent teratogenic signal.
- Nortriptyline and desipramine preferred (less anticholinergic, less hypotension).
- Useful when SSRIs/SNRIs have failed.

### MAOIs
- Generally avoided in pregnancy due to dietary interaction risks, hypertensive crisis potential, and limited safety data.

## Neonatal Adaptation Syndrome (NAS)

### What It Is
Approximately 15-30% of neonates exposed to SSRIs/SNRIs in the third trimester may exhibit a transient self-limited syndrome:
- Irritability, jitteriness, tremor
- Respiratory distress (usually mild)
- Feeding difficulties
- Temperature instability
- Rarely: seizures

### Management
- Symptoms typically appear within hours of delivery and resolve within 3-5 days (up to 2 weeks).
- Usually self-limited; supportive care (swaddling, skin-to-skin, feeding support).
- Does not warrant NICU admission in most cases, though observation is appropriate.
- **Not a reason to stop medication in the third trimester** — abrupt discontinuation risks maternal relapse during the highest-risk period for postpartum depression.

## Persistent Pulmonary Hypertension of the Newborn (PPHN)

- Early studies suggested SSRI exposure after 20 weeks gestation increased PPHN risk (OR ~2). Absolute risk remained very low (~3 per 1,000 vs. 1.2 per 1,000 baseline).
- Subsequent larger studies have been inconsistent, and the FDA revised its warning in 2011, stating the link is not established.
- Current consensus: the risk, if any, is very small and does not outweigh benefits of treating moderate-severe depression.

## Lactation and Breastfeeding

### General Principles
- Benefits of breastfeeding are substantial for both mother and infant.
- Most antidepressants pass into breast milk in small quantities.
- The infant dose (as a percentage of maternal weight-adjusted dose) is the key metric.

### Preferred Agents During Breastfeeding
- **Sertraline**: Very low breast milk levels. Relative infant dose <2%. Most studied and preferred.
- **Paroxetine**: Low breast milk transfer. Acceptable for breastfeeding (though not preferred in pregnancy).
- **Nortriptyline**: Low levels in breast milk. Good option for those on TCAs.

### Acceptable Alternatives
- Escitalopram, fluoxetine, venlafaxine: Somewhat higher breast milk transfer but generally considered compatible with breastfeeding in most cases.
- Fluoxetine: Active metabolite can accumulate in neonates — monitor infant for irritability, poor feeding, sedation.

### Not Recommended During Breastfeeding
- **Lithium**: Significant breast milk levels; risk of neonatal toxicity. Requires infant serum monitoring if used.
- **Doxepin**: High relative infant dose; cases of infant sedation and respiratory depression reported.

## Clinical Decision-Making Framework

1. **Mild depression**: Consider psychotherapy alone (CBT, IPT have strong evidence in perinatal depression). If medication was used pre-pregnancy, consider tapering with close monitoring.
2. **Moderate depression**: Medication + therapy. If currently stable on an antidepressant, **strongly consider continuing** — switching or stopping during pregnancy increases relapse risk.
3. **Severe depression / history of suicidality**: Medication is generally essential. Do not discontinue without a compelling reason and close psychiatric follow-up.
4. **New-onset during pregnancy**: Sertraline or escitalopram as first-line. Consider psychotherapy concurrently.

### Do Not:
- Switch a stable patient to a "safer" medication solely because of pregnancy — the switch itself introduces risk (relapse during transition, exposure to two medications).
- Stop medication abruptly in the third trimester to "protect the baby" from NAS — maternal relapse risk far outweighs NAS risk.
- Dismiss patient concerns about medication — informed shared decision-making is essential.

## Key Counseling Points

- Untreated depression carries real risks to you and your baby. Treatment is not selfish — it is protective.
- Sertraline has the most safety data in pregnancy and breastfeeding.
- Neonatal adaptation syndrome is usually mild and temporary.
- Breastfeeding is compatible with most antidepressants.
- Every decision should be made together with your OB, psychiatrist, and pediatrician.
