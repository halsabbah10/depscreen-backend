# Antidepressant Considerations in Elderly Patients

## Overview

Late-life depression affects approximately 15-20% of community-dwelling adults over 65 and up to 30-40% in long-term care settings. It is underdiagnosed, undertreated, and associated with increased medical morbidity, cognitive decline, functional impairment, and suicide — older adults (particularly white men 85+) have the highest completed suicide rate of any demographic.

## Unique Challenges in Older Adults

- **Altered pharmacokinetics**: Reduced hepatic metabolism (decreased CYP450 activity), decreased renal clearance, increased body fat (longer half-lives for lipophilic drugs), reduced serum albumin (higher free drug levels).
- **Polypharmacy**: Average 65+ patient takes 5-9 medications. Drug interaction risk is high.
- **Medical comorbidities**: Cardiovascular disease, diabetes, arthritis, neurological conditions all affect medication choice.
- **Cognitive impairment**: Overlap between depression and early dementia. Anticholinergic medications can worsen cognition.
- **Fall risk**: Orthostatic hypotension and sedation from antidepressants are leading contributors to falls and fractures.

## Start Low, Go Slow (But Get There)

### Dosing Principles
- Start at 50% of the usual adult starting dose.
- Titrate in smaller increments at longer intervals (every 2-4 weeks).
- **But do not underdose.** "Start low, go slow" does not mean "stay low forever." Many elderly patients require full therapeutic doses.
- Inadequate dosing is a common reason for apparent "treatment resistance" in this population.

### Typical Starting Doses in Elderly
| Medication | Young Adult Start | Elderly Start |
|---|---|---|
| Sertraline | 50 mg | 25 mg |
| Escitalopram | 10 mg | 5 mg |
| Venlafaxine XR | 75 mg | 37.5 mg |
| Mirtazapine | 15 mg | 7.5 mg |
| Duloxetine | 30 mg | 20 mg |
| Bupropion XL | 150 mg | 150 mg (generally well-tolerated) |

## Preferred Agents

### First-Line: Escitalopram and Sertraline
- **Escitalopram**: Fewest CYP450 interactions of any SSRI. Clean pharmacological profile. Well-tolerated. Effective in late-life depression trials.
- **Sertraline**: Extensive safety data in elderly, including post-MI patients (SADHART trial). Moderate CYP2D6 inhibition but generally manageable.

### Second-Line Options
- **Duloxetine**: Useful for comorbid neuropathic pain, musculoskeletal pain, fibromyalgia. Avoid in hepatic impairment.
- **Venlafaxine**: Effective but monitor blood pressure. Higher discontinuation syndrome risk (educate about consistent dosing).
- **Mirtazapine**: Helpful when insomnia, poor appetite, or weight loss are prominent. Sedation at low doses can be therapeutic. Monitor for excessive somnolence and fall risk.
- **Bupropion**: Activating — useful for fatigue/apathy. Weight neutral. No sexual dysfunction. Caution with seizure history.

## Agents to Avoid (Beers Criteria and Clinical Evidence)

### Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs)
- **AGS Beers Criteria: AVOID as first-line** in older adults.
- Strong anticholinergic effects → cognitive impairment, delirium, constipation, urinary retention, dry mouth, blurred vision.
- Alpha-1 blockade → orthostatic hypotension → falls and fractures.
- Cardiac toxicity → QRS/QTc prolongation, arrhythmias.
- **Amitriptyline, doxepin (>6 mg), imipramine**: Highest risk. Avoid entirely in elderly if possible.
- **Nortriptyline, desipramine**: If TCA needed (pain, TRD), these have the lowest anticholinergic burden of the class. Require ECG monitoring and therapeutic drug levels.

### Paroxetine
- **Most anticholinergic SSRI** — Beers Criteria lists it as potentially inappropriate.
- Short half-life → worst discontinuation syndrome of SSRIs.
- Strong CYP2D6 inhibitor → high interaction potential in polypharmacy.
- **Prefer escitalopram or sertraline instead.**

### MAOIs
- Dietary restrictions are particularly problematic in elderly (institutional meals, cognitive impairment affecting compliance).
- Orthostatic hypotension exacerbates fall risk.
- Reserved for treatment-resistant cases under specialist care.

## Key Safety Concerns

### Anticholinergic Burden
- Cumulative anticholinergic burden from multiple medications (antidepressant + antihistamine + bladder medication + GI medication) is a major concern.
- Use the Anticholinergic Cognitive Burden (ACB) scale to assess total load.
- Each additional anticholinergic medication increases dementia risk and functional decline.
- **Audit the entire medication list**, not just the antidepressant.

### Hyponatremia (SIADH)
- SSRIs and SNRIs can cause syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion.
- Risk factors: age >65, female sex, low body weight, concurrent diuretics, low baseline sodium.
- Check serum sodium at baseline, 1-2 weeks after starting or dose change, and periodically thereafter.
- Symptoms: confusion, headache, nausea, lethargy, seizures (severe cases).
- More common with SSRIs than other classes.

### Fall Risk
- All antidepressants associated with increased fall risk (OR ~1.5-2.0 in elderly).
- Highest risk: TCAs (orthostatic hypotension), mirtazapine (sedation), trazodone (orthostatic + sedation).
- Lowest risk: bupropion, vortioxetine.
- Implement fall prevention: assess gait, home safety, medication timing.

### Bone Density
- SSRIs associated with reduced bone mineral density and increased fracture risk (OR ~1.3-1.7).
- Consider bone health monitoring in long-term SSRI users, especially those with osteoporosis risk factors.
- Ensure adequate calcium and vitamin D.

### QT Prolongation
- Citalopram: FDA-mandated max 20 mg/day in patients >60 due to dose-dependent QTc prolongation.
- Obtain ECG if using citalopram, escitalopram, or TCAs, especially with other QT-prolonging medications.

## Depression vs. Dementia: Pseudodementia

- Late-life depression can present with prominent cognitive symptoms ("pseudodementia"): poor concentration, slowed thinking, memory complaints.
- Distinguishing features: depression-related cognitive impairment tends to have more acute onset, subjective memory complaints exceeding objective deficits, "I don't know" responses (vs. confabulation in dementia), and improvement with antidepressant treatment.
- Depression and dementia coexist in ~30% of dementia patients — treat the depression regardless.
- Cognitive screening (MoCA, MMSE) before and after treatment helps clarify diagnosis.

## Monitoring Recommendations

| Parameter | Timing |
|---|---|
| Baseline labs | CBC, CMP (including sodium), TSH, B12, folate |
| Sodium | 1-2 weeks post-start, then periodically |
| Blood pressure | Each visit (SNRIs especially) |
| ECG | Baseline if cardiac risk, QT-prolonging drugs, or TCAs |
| Fall risk assessment | Baseline and ongoing |
| Cognitive screening | Baseline (MoCA) and after 8-12 weeks of treatment |
| Medication reconciliation | Every visit — check for interactions and anticholinergic burden |
